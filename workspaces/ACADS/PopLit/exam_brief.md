## Method — contradictions and the "so what?" test

The analytical move is the same across every text and framework in this brief: **locate a contradiction in the text, then push it with "so what?" until it produces an argument.** A text without contradictions is a text without power. An essay that names tensions without saying *what they do* is description, not analysis.

### The two-step move

1. **Name the contradiction.** Two things hold at once. The text is X *and* not-X. Resist resolving it.
2. **Ask "so what?"** What does the contradiction reveal about culture, power, South Asia, or the framework you are using? Push twice if needed — a contradiction is a clue; "so what?" is the analysis.

### Worked examples

**Example 1 — *The White Tiger*.**
*Contradiction:* Balram's success simultaneously **critiques** the system (escape requires murder, the family is sacrificed) and **vindicates** it (exceptional individuals do escape; the rooster coop has a hatch).
*So what?* The novel refuses to resolve this, and the refusal is the politics. Closing the contradiction in either direction would be ideological — pure critique lets the reader off, pure vindication betrays the coop's victims. **The structural critique and the entrepreneurial fantasy share a vocabulary** ("white tiger", self-made, the man from the Darkness), and Adiga is showing that entrapment: dissent has to be voiced in the language of the dissented-from.

**Example 2 — Manto.**
*Contradiction:* Manto was **destroyed** by the centre during his life (six obscenity trials, censorship, poverty, alcoholism) and is now **absorbed** by the centre after death (canonical, translated, taught).
*So what?* This is not a happy ending. It is the **margin–centre framework's defining manoeuvre**: the centre cannot tolerate the margin while it speaks, but recuperates it once it can no longer threaten. Posthumous canonization tells us less about literary justice than about the centre's confidence that the threat is now safely past.

**Example 3 — *Circle of Karma*.**
*Contradiction:* Karma is **productive** (gives meaning, enables endurance) *and* **limiting** (rationalises structural injustice as personal debt).
*So what?* Choden's refusal to choose between these is the novel's argument about culture: cultural frameworks are not good or bad, they are **operative** — they shape the field of what their bearers can do, see, and bear. To ask "should Tsomo accept her suffering?" is to import a Western-rights frame; the novel asks instead what kind of life karma makes possible and what it forecloses.

| Site | Contradiction | "So what?" landing |
|---|---|---|

| *Funny Boy* | Arjie's Tamil family is both victim of Sinhalese majoritarianism *and* enforcer of patriarchy on him | Margins are not pure; the oppressed reproduce hierarchies internally. **Double exclusion** is the analytic that exposes this — "the marginalised community" is itself stratified |
| *Hands* / Nepal | Nepal is included in the syllabus *because* it is excluded from South Asian studies | The syllabus's inclusion is an argument about its own canon — a self-conscious centre marking its own margin. The act of teaching the absence is the politics |
| *White Tiger* | Balram's success critiques the system *and* reproduces its grammar | The novel maps the trap of having to dissent in the language of the dissented-from; the rooster coop is intact for Balram's own taxi drivers |

### How to deploy in an essay

Build a paragraph in this shape:

> **Argument:** [claim using a named framework]
> **Textual evidence:** [specific scene/character/line]
> **And yet…** [the contradiction you have just exposed]
> **So what?** [what the contradiction reveals — about culture, South Asia, power, or the framework]

The "**And yet… so what?**" pivot is what turns a competent answer into an analytical one. **Do not skip it.**

## §2 Eleven frameworks

The instructor's review lists eleven introductory concepts. Names exactly as in the course review.

### 2.1 Culture as process
**Argument:** Culture is not a fixed essence but an ongoing activity of making, contesting, performing. **Deploy when** a prompt treats "South Asian culture" or "tradition" as a static noun; refuse the static frame.
**Move:** "The text shows culture as process by depicting [character] actively negotiating cultural meaning under [historical pressure], not transmitting a stable inheritance."
**Anchors:** *Circle of Karma* (Buddhism as lived daily practice, not doctrine), *Khamosh Pani* (Pakistani identity remade by 1979 Islamization), *White Tiger* (servitude reproduced and contested).

### 2.2 Borders & territoriality
**Argument:** Borders are produced and policed; they are political infrastructure, not natural facts. They operate at multiple scales — national, communal, domestic, bodily.
**Move:** "Borders in [text] operate not just at the geopolitical edge but at the [communal/domestic/bodily] scale: the same political logic enforces lines at every level."
**Anchors:** Manto's *Toba Tek Singh* (Bishen Singh dies in the no-man's-land between India and Pakistan — the partition border literally rejects him); *Funny Boy* (Tamil/Sinhalese territorial claim culminating in Black July 1983); *Osama* (border of the woman's body, the door of the home, public visibility).

### 2.3 Conceptualizing South Asia / Emergent South Asia
**Argument:** "South Asia" is a constructed category — a regional concept produced by colonial mapping, Cold War area studies, and contemporary diasporic and economic flows, not a self-evident geography. The course is itself a canon-formation act about what counts as South Asian.
**Move:** "The text contributes to an emergent rather than essentialist South Asia: it is read as 'representative' through institutional choices [publishing, prizes, syllabi], not because it transparently mirrors the region."
**Anchors:** *Hands* (Nepal's near-absence from South Asian curricula); *Kite Runner* ("Afghanistan" produced for Western markets); *White Tiger* (India narrated to China, sidestepping the Western gaze).

### 2.4 Margin–centre framework
**Argument:** Power operates by sorting voices into legitimate centres and marginal others. The relation is **structural and relational**, not geographical, and runs at multiple levels simultaneously.
**Levels (deploy whichever fits):** geopolitical (West/South Asia), national (dominant ethnicity/minorities), gendered (men/women), class (capital/labour), narrative (whose "I" speaks vs. who is spoken about).
**Move:** "The text operates a margin–centre logic in which X occupies the centre by virtue of [structural feature]; Y is rendered marginal by the same operation. The text [accepts / complicates / inverts] this arrangement."
**Anchors:** *Kite Runner* (Amir narrates, Hassan is narrated about); Manto (sex workers placed at narrative centre — inversion); *Funny Boy* (Tamil minority at national margin); *White Tiger* (Balram from "the Darkness" speaks to Wen Jiabao).

### 2.5 Transnationalism & globalization
**Argument:** People, capital, and texts cross borders — but **asymmetrically**. Capital and culture move freely; bodies do not.
**Move:** "Globalization in [text] is asymmetric: [the garment / the novel / the brand] moves; [the worker / the refugee / the Hazara] cannot. The critical force is in making this asymmetry visible."
**Anchors:** *Machinists / True Cost* (the shirt enters Western markets, the Bangladeshi worker does not); *Kite Runner* (the novel circulates globally, Afghan refugees do not); *White Tiger* (epistolary letter sent to China — global address).

### 2.6 Diaspora
**Argument:** Diaspora is not nostalgia or assimilation but **continuous renegotiation** in the gap between origin and adoption. The diasporic homeland is partly imagined, often more "traditional" than the homeland itself.
**Move:** "The text figures identity as constant negotiation, not as a stable home carried from origin to destination."
**Anchors:** *Kite Runner* (Hosseini wrote it from California — Afghanistan partly reconstructed through nostalgia and research; Amir's California life as escape and unfinished business). Use only as comparator: *Reluctant Fundamentalist*, *Namesake*.

### 2.7 Gendering South Asia
**Argument:** "Gendering" is a verb, not a topic. It exposes gender as a **structural infrastructure** through which violence, silence, labour, agency, and visibility are unequally distributed.
**Five mechanisms:** public/private binary; body as territory; honour logic (women's bodies as community honour); silence as gendered testimony; gender as performance.
**Move:** "Gendering [text] reveals that gender is the structural mechanism through which [violence / silence / labour] is distributed — not a thematic flavour but a political infrastructure."
**Anchors:** *Osama* (gender as performance under Taliban surveillance — Butler); *Khamosh Pani* (silence as the form women's testimony takes); *Circle of Karma* (Tsomo's body as the site of labour and patriarchal violence); *True Cost* (~80% women workers — gender disciplines the global supply chain).

### 2.8 Violence & conflict
**Argument:** Violence in the texts is rarely an eruption from nowhere — it is the **everyday reproduction of conflict**, prepared by small acts of differentiation, exclusion, and discursive marking that accumulate before catastrophe.
**Move:** "[Catastrophic event] did not happen suddenly; the text shows it being prepared through everyday practices — [school scene / domestic conversation / discriminatory law]."
**Anchors:** *Funny Boy* (Black July 1983 prepared by years of Sinhalese Buddhist nationalism, school discrimination, mixed-neighborhood erosion); Manto's Partition stories (community fracture as ordinary cruelty, not aberration); *Khamosh Pani* (1979 communal politics re-erupting on the same body that absorbed 1947).

### 2.9 Censorship as productive practice
**Argument:** Censorship is not just suppression — what a state censors **reveals what it considers dangerous**. Censorship produces meaning about power and its anxieties.
**Move:** "Manto was prosecuted for [content], which tells us less about Manto than about what [post-Partition Pakistan / the Taliban] could not face about itself. Censorship is the state confessing."
**Anchors:** Manto's six obscenity trials (three British India, three Pakistan) for *Khol Do*, *Thanda Gosht*, etc. — the new Pakistani state could not bear to see Partition's sexual violence depicted because it had to construct a respectable national identity. Compare *Osama*'s Taliban (more extreme version of the same logic — total censorship of art, music, female visibility). Manto's reply: *"If you cannot bear my stories, it is because we live in unbearable times."*

### 2.10 Politics of representation (framing, distribution, reception)
**Argument:** When X represents Y, three things are political: **mimesis** (accuracy), **power** (who has the platform), **reception** (who consumes, with what effect).
**Move:** "The politics of representation in this text turn on its addressee. The text [reinforces / disrupts] a centre-margin extraction in which the West consumes South Asia as spectacle."
**Anchors:** *Kite Runner* — "Afghanistan Meets the Amazon" (Edwards): post-9/11 American readers used the novel to ask "why do they hate us" and find "good Afghans"; the novel became a tool of American self-understanding. Compare *White Tiger* (addresses China, not the West — sideways global gaze) and *True Cost* (direct accusation of Western consumer).

### 2.11 Intimate geopolitics (Funny Boy unit, but generalizable)
**Argument:** Large-scale political conflict is **reproduced in intimate everyday spaces** — the home, the school, the body, the friendship group. Geopolitics enters through ordinary interactions long before catastrophic violence.
**Move:** "The text figures the [civil war / Islamization / Taliban rule] not as external context but as already inside the home: in [specific scene of intimate erosion]."
**Anchors:** *Funny Boy* (Sri Lankan conflict enters Arjie's family through Radha Aunty's broken cross-ethnic engagement, school caning by Black Tie, mixed-neighbourhood fracture, finally the burning of the family home); *Khamosh Pani* (the nation's Islamization enters Ayesha's life through her own son Saleem's radicalization); *Osama* (Taliban authority intrudes into the domestic sphere — there is no "private" left).

### 3.1 *Osama* (2003) — Barmak — Afghanistan

**Plot in one line:** A 12-year-old girl in Taliban-era Kabul disguises as a boy ('Osama') so she can earn for her widowed mother; she is discovered and forcibly married to an elderly mullah.

**Why it matters:** First film made in Afghanistan after the Taliban; shot in Dari, on location — distribution counterpart to *Kite Runner* (see cross-link).

**Argument 1 — Gender is performance, not essence.**
Evidence: The girl does not just wear boy's clothes — she has to *enact* masculinity continuously: her gait, her silence at the madrassa, her participation in male physical training, her hair-cutting scene. Every moment is conscious performance under threat of exposure. The film makes Butler legible: the same child in different clothes occupies entirely different social positions.

**Argument 2 — Taliban power is panoptic — Foucault.**
Evidence: She must maintain the disguise even when no Taliban are visible — she has internalized the surveillance. The street executions, the public whipping of her mother for going out without a male guardian, the spectacle-trial at the end ("punishment as exemplary, public, deterrent") show power reproducing itself through visible discipline.

**Argument 3 — Erasure of the public/private binary.**
Evidence: The traditional patriarchal binary at least leaves women a "private" sphere of safety. The Taliban collapses this: women cannot enter the public (mother cannot work → starvation), but the home is also unsafe (Taliban authority intrudes; the family hides the disguise from neighbours). Cramped interiors and the constant intrusion of Taliban authority into domestic space are the film's visual grammar.

**Argument 4 — Agency under constraint, refusal of triumphal resistance.**
Evidence: The disguise itself is agency (refusal to starve passively), as is the neighbour who suggests it, the mother who agrees, the woman doctor who treats women secretly. But the protagonist is *caught* and *punished*. The film refuses a resistance-narrative ending: under such systems, individual agency reaches structural limits.

**Argument 5 — Afghanistan is not "naturally" oppressive; it is the product of specific political conditions.**
Evidence: The film is set 1996–2001. Male breadwinners are absent because they have been killed in Soviet/civil/US-front wars. Gender oppression is conditioned by **decades of war** (Soviet 1979, civil war 1989–96, Taliban 1996, US intervention 2001) — Cold War interventions produced the conditions that made Taliban rule possible.

**Cross-text:** vs. *Kite Runner* — same country, same period, **opposite framing**: *Osama* is made *in* Afghanistan, in Dari, from within Afghan experience; *Kite Runner* is written in English, in America, *for* Western markets (see §3.2). vs. *Khamosh Pani* — both put a woman's body at the centre as the site where political regimes enforce themselves; both refuse triumphal resolution.

### 3.2 *The Kite Runner* (2003) — Hosseini — Afghanistan (diasporic novel)

**Plot in one line:** Amir, a wealthy Pashtun boy in Kabul, fails to defend his Hazara servant-friend Hassan from rape; decades later, in California exile, he returns to Taliban-era Afghanistan to rescue Hassan's son Sohrab from a Talib who turns out to be Amir's childhood tormentor Assef.

**The course's question:** Not "is this a good story about Afghanistan?" but **who tells the story, for whom, and what is constructed, amplified, or erased?**

**Argument 1 — Narrative framing reproduces the margin–centre at the level of voice.**
Evidence: Amir is the narrator — wealthy, Pashtun, Kabul-urban, English-educated. Hassan, the Hazara servant, is *narrated about* — even his rape is filtered through Amir's guilt rather than Hassan's experience. Hassan's letter (ch. 17) appears, but only as relayed by Rahim Khan. **The marginal character is seen, not heard.** This is a narrative choice with political consequences.

**Argument 2 — The universal vs. the local: Afghanistan as backdrop.**
Evidence: The novel's global success rests on themes coded "universal" — father-son guilt, friendship, redemption. These translate Afghanistan into a "backdrop" rather than the substance of the story. Ethnic and class hierarchies (Pashtun/Hazara, master/servant) are *present but not analysed*; the Taliban appears as a villain *enabling plot resolution* rather than as a political formation. This is **simplified South Asia produced for outside consumption**.

**Argument 3 — Reception is not passive (Edwards, "Afghanistan Meets the Amazon").**
Evidence: Post-9/11 US readers brought their own anxieties to the text — using it to understand "why they hate us", to find "good Afghans", to process the War on Terror. The novel became **a tool of American self-understanding more than Afghan self-expression**. The same text means different things in Kabul and Kansas; reception is constitutive of meaning.

**Argument 4 — Margin–centre framework in global publishing: literature from the margin enters the centre when it does not challenge centre assumptions.**
Evidence: *Kite Runner* succeeded because it confirmed Western moral categories (individual guilt, redemption, the bad-Talib villain, the good-Afghan-with-Western-values protagonist). Compare Manto, who was *not* globally celebrated in his lifetime — his work refused the centre's tastes. The contrast is the lesson: which margin-stories travel, and why.

**Argument 5 — Diaspora and authenticity.**
Evidence: Hosseini left Afghanistan as a child; his Kabul is partly imagined, reconstructed through nostalgia and research. The novel was received as "an authentic representation" of Afghanistan. This does not make it dishonest — but the **claim to authenticity is itself a structure of power**: a diasporic author becomes the global voice of the homeland for readers who have no other source.

**Cross-text:** vs. *Osama* — see 3.1. vs. Manto — both are tested by their reception: Manto censored for refusing comfort, Hosseini celebrated for providing it; the **reception gap is itself the lesson** about the margin-centre framework in literary production. vs. *White Tiger* — both end with the protagonist transformed and successful, but Adiga refuses redemption and Hosseini grants it; ask why.

### 3.3 Manto — Pakistan (Partition stories)

**Bio in one line:** Saadat Hasan Manto (1912–1955), Urdu short-story writer, migrated Bombay→Lahore at Partition; six obscenity trials (three British India, three Pakistan); died of alcoholism in poverty; now canonical.

**The four stories you should be able to cite:** *Toba Tek Singh*, *Khol Do* ("Open it"), *Thanda Gosht* ("Cold Meat"), *Mozelle* / *Hatak* (sex worker stories).

**Argument 1 — Partition as backdrop, not historical event.**
Evidence: Manto refuses the nationalist celebration of 1947 (independence) and writes Partition as **lived catastrophe** for ordinary, often marginal, people. *Toba Tek Singh*: Bishen Singh, a mental-asylum inmate, dies on the strip of no-man's-land between India and Pakistan when the asylums are partitioned — the border itself becomes the figure of madness. Manto refuses both Indian and Pakistani official narratives.

**Argument 2 — Character writing as inversion of margin and centre.**
Evidence: His characters are people erased from "respectable" literature — sex workers (Mozelle, Sugandhi in *Hatak*), pimps, the mentally ill (Bishen Singh), petty criminals, soldiers, refugees. He **humanizes without sentimentalizing or moralizing**: a sex worker is not a symbol of decline but a person with a name, history, inner life. **The character is a social x-ray**: through the marginal life, the whole society's hypocrisies become visible.

**Argument 3 — Censorship as productive practice — what was censored tells you what the state could not face.**
Evidence: *Khol Do* (story of an abducted girl Sakina who, in a refugee camp, on hearing the doctor say "open it" — the window — automatically reaches for the drawstring of her trousers; her father realises the unspeakable) was prosecuted in Pakistan. The newly-formed Pakistani state needed a respectable national identity; Manto's stories showed sexual violence committed *in the name of* that identity. Manto's defence — *"If you cannot bear my stories, it is because we live in unbearable times"* — is itself a political act.

**Argument 4 — Centre–margin in literary production, with retrospective irony.**
Evidence: The Pakistani literary establishment (educated, middle-class, morally conservative, building a "respectable" national literature) censored Manto in his lifetime. He is *now* canonical — taught, translated, celebrated. **The margin has been absorbed into the centre, but only after his death.** During his life the centre destroyed him.

**Cross-text:** vs. *Khamosh Pani* — both write Partition's gendered violence; Manto says it explicitly and is censored, *Khamosh Pani* embeds it in silence (different aesthetic strategies for the same wound). vs. *Kite Runner* — opposite reception trajectories: Manto refused to make his work comfortable; Hosseini (structurally) made his comfortable. vs. *Osama* — the Taliban's total censorship is the more extreme version of the same logic that prosecuted Manto.

### 3.4 *Khamosh Pani* / *Silent Waters* (2003) — Sumar — Pakistan

**Plot in one line:** In rural Punjab in **1979**, during Zia ul-Haq's Islamization, devout Muslim widow Ayesha is gradually revealed to be **Veero** — a Sikh woman left behind / abducted at Partition (1947) and converted; her son Saleem is being recruited by an Islamist movement; Sikh pilgrims arrive on a religious visit; her past surfaces; the film ends in tragedy.

**Secondary source pairing:** Urvashi Bhutalia, *The Other Side of Silence* (oral histories of Partition women).

**Argument 1 — Gendering Partition.**
Evidence: Bhutalia documents that Partition violence was not gender-neutral — women (Hindu, Sikh, Muslim) were abducted, raped, killed; families killed their own women to "protect honour" before enemy men reached them. Women's bodies became the **terrain** on which communal violence was fought. *Khamosh Pani* fictionalizes this: Ayesha's identity has been rearranged by gendered violence — she was either abducted or jumped into a well to escape (the film keeps it deliberately ambiguous). The film is, on a single body, the entire gendered logic of Partition.

**Argument 2 — Partition as continued event.**
Evidence: The film structurally argues that 1947 is **not finished**. The 1979 setting is essential: 32 years later, Pakistan's Islamization re-erupts the same questions of religious belonging that 1947 left unresolved. The arrival of the Sikh pilgrims (a 1979 event) reopens the 1947 wound. Saleem's radicalization mirrors his mother's history — the post-Partition nation is still being defined on her body.

**Argument 3 — Silence as a stylistic device — read what the film refuses to say.**
Evidence: The title is *Silent Waters*. Ayesha does not speak about her past — not because she has forgotten but because to speak is to risk death (exposure) and to relive trauma. **Silence is performed, like the protagonist's gender performance in *Osama*** — a continuously enacted suppression of identity, a survival strategy. The film asks the viewer to **read what is not said**: the pauses, the averted eyes, the moments when the past almost breaks through.

**Argument 4 — Oral history as feminist methodology.**
Evidence: Bhutalia's *Other Side of Silence* literally goes to women whose silence had been kept for decades and asks them to speak. Her use of oral history is itself a margin-centre intervention: women's voices were not in the official archives. *Khamosh Pani* dramatizes both the silence and its cost.

**Cross-text:** vs. *Osama* — both centre a woman whose body is the site of regime enforcement; both use disguise/silence as survival; both refuse triumphal endings (the protagonist is not saved). vs. Manto — Manto says explicitly what *Khamosh Pani* says obliquely; **what Manto was censored for saying, Sumar says by not-saying**.

### 3.5 *The Machinists* (2012) & *The True Cost* (2015) — Bangladesh

**Films in one line each:** *Machinists* (Dill-Riaz) follows garment workers inside Dhaka factories — their routines, friendships, ambitions. *True Cost* (Morgan) is a global investigation into fast fashion that traces a garment from Bangladesh to a Western mall. Both orbit the **Rana Plaza collapse, 24 April 2013** — over 1,100 killed, mostly women, after structural warnings were ignored.

**Argument 1 — Rana Plaza makes global labour networks visible.**
Evidence: Rana Plaza produced for Primark, Walmart, Benetton — major Western brands. **Production is outsourced to the Global South**; profit flows to the Global North; the risk and suffering stay in Bangladesh. This is not an accident or a "developing country failing" — it is the logic of contemporary global capitalism: the South produces cheaply so the North can consume cheaply. The garment industry is ~80% of Bangladesh's export earnings; the workers who produce that wealth are paid poverty wages.

**Argument 2 — Labour hazards as social, not technical.**
Evidence: The Rana Plaza workers were ordered back into a building they knew was cracking, threatened with withheld wages. The hazard was not the building — it was a system that valued their lives less than a production deadline. Hazards are products of social and economic relations.

**Argument 3 — Gender and labour: the supply chain is feminised by design.**
Evidence: ~80% of Bangladeshi garment workers are women; this is not coincidental. Women are preferred because they are considered "more docile", "easier to discipline", "less likely to unionize". The gender hierarchy is reproduced inside the factory: women do unskilled sewing; men hold supervisory positions. Gender is the discipline of the global supply chain.

**Argument 4 — Dignity of labour vs. exploitation are not opposites.**
Evidence: *Machinists* takes a crucial formal choice: it does **not** present workers as victims. It shows them with pride in their work, complex relationships, humour, ambition — **full human beings**. The political insight is that you can respect the worker *and* recognize that the system exploiting her is unjust. Dignity and exploitation coexist.

**Argument 5 — Critique of technocratic solutions.**
Evidence: After Rana Plaza, the Western response was building inspections, fire-safety audits, CSR programmes. *True Cost* argues these are **technocratic** — treating a structural problem (a system that externalizes costs onto Global South workers) as a technical failure (bad buildings). As long as fast-fashion brands compete on low prices, pressure on factories to cut costs (including safety) is irreducible. No number of inspections can fix that.

**Argument 6 — Culture as process: fashion creates cultural demand to drive economic exploitation.**
Evidence: "Fast fashion" means ~52 micro-seasons per year instead of two — **culture (fashion) is being continuously manufactured to maintain consumption**. This is culture-as-process in the bluntest economic register: the cultural cycle drives the supply chain that produces Rana Plaza.

**Argument 7 — Bangladesh as emerging South Asian tiger — the contradiction.**
Evidence: "South Asian tiger" references the East Asian growth economies. Bangladesh's garment-led growth ties to its 1971 Liberation War — economic independence as national self-determination. But its "success" is built on the exploitation of its own workers for Western consumption. The films ask: **is this development, or a new form of global inequality?**

**Cross-text:** vs. *White Tiger* — both interrogate the promise of development under neoliberal globalisation; in Bangladesh global capital exploits, in India domestic capital + caste-class system exploits. vs. *Hands* — both put **the body and the hand at the centre of labour and exploitation**. vs. *Circle of Karma* — Tsomo's body and the garment worker's body are both Global South female bodies bearing disproportionate physical cost.

### 3.6 *The Circle of Karma* (2005) — Choden — Bhutan

**Plot in one line:** Tsomo, a poor rural Bhutanese woman, suffers an abusive marriage, poverty, repeated loss; she undertakes a series of pilgrimages across Bhutan and to India, always seeking something she cannot name. The novel is structured around karmic logic and memory.

**Why this text matters:** First English-language novel by a Bhutanese woman; rare insider perspective on Bhutanese culture. Bhutan is one of the least-studied South Asian countries.

**Argument 1 — Buddhism as lived practice — culture as process in operation.**
Evidence: The novel's Buddhism is not abstract doctrine but daily ritual: prayer flags, offerings, pilgrimage, the monastery, karmic interpretation of every event. Tsomo's understanding of her own life — *why* she suffers, *what* she should do — is filtered through Buddhist cultural forms. This is **culture as process**: religion is a living cultural system organizing experience, not a static catalogue.

**Argument 2 — Tension between spirituality and materiality is the location of culture.**
Evidence: Tsomo cannot purely accept her poverty as karma (Buddhist detachment) — she must also *act* to survive, work, eat. The novel inhabits the gap between the spiritual framework and material need. **Culture lives in this gap** between ideal and lived experience — not in the doctrine, not in the body alone, but in the friction.

**Argument 3 — Memory-based narrative structure mirrors karmic logic.**
Evidence: The novel moves through time non-linearly, structured by what Tsomo remembers, understands, still cannot make sense of. In Buddhism, memory and accumulated experience (karma) shape the present moment — the form enacts the content. **The novel's memory structure also recovers women's lives** that are usually absent from official records, parallel to Bhutalia's oral-history method in the Pakistan unit.

**Argument 4 — Body as a site of labour and suffering.**
Evidence: Tsomo's body is not an object of desire but the **instrument and recipient** of physical work — farming, carrying, walking hundreds of miles on pilgrimage — and of violence (the abusive husband). **The body is where social conditions are materially inscribed.** Her pilgrimages are simultaneously bodily endurance and geographic transgression — a poor rural woman walking alone through male-dominated space is crossing social as well as physical boundaries; the body in motion is a form of agency.

**Argument 5 — Karma as culturally embedded epistemology — productive *and* limiting.**
Evidence: Karma is not just doctrine; it is an **epistemology** — a way of knowing that organizes experience. **Productive function:** karma gives Tsomo a framework for enduring suffering without despair — her pain is meaningful (caused by past actions) and purposeful (generating merit). She survives because karma gives suffering form. **Limiting function:** karma can encourage acceptance of structural injustice — *if suffering is karmic, is resistance futile?* Patriarchal abuse and rural poverty risk being misread as personal karmic debt rather than social failure. The novel does not resolve this tension; it inhabits it honestly.

**Argument 6 — Everyday practice of karma.**
Evidence: The novel shows karma not as abstract theology but **everyday currency**: small acts of kindness, decisions in difficult moments, helping a stranger on the road, response to being wronged — each is read by Tsomo as karmic. The novel resists the Western tendency to read Buddhism through an exotic / mystical lens; karma for Tsomo is **as ordinary as paying a bill**. Buddhism is not a monument but a daily practice.

**Cross-text:** vs. *White Tiger* — **two opposite responses to structural poverty**: Tsomo endures within a karmic framework that finds meaning in suffering; Balram refuses acceptance and escapes through violence. The comparison asks: *what frameworks does a society offer for responding to poverty, and what actions do they enable or foreclose?* vs. *Funny Boy* — both texts show **culturally embedded epistemologies**: in Bhutan, karma organizes responses to suffering; in Sri Lanka, ethnic identity organizes violence — both are ways of knowing that structure action; culture as process operates in productive *and* destructive directions. vs. *Machinists* — both show the female body in the Global South as the site where social structure becomes physical reality.

### 3.7 *Funny Boy* (1994) — Selvadurai — Sri Lanka

**Plot in one line:** Coming-of-age novel set in Colombo from the 1970s through the **1983 anti-Tamil Black July riots**. Arjie is a Tamil boy who is also gay ('funny' in the family's euphemism); the novel traces his growing awareness of both his sexuality and the escalating Sinhalese-Tamil conflict, ending in the destruction of his family home and forced emigration.

**Why this text matters:** One of the few South Asian novels to address homosexuality as **intersecting** with ethnic and political identity. Refuses to separate "personal" from "political".

**Argument 1 — Sri Lankan conflict: religious, ethnic, territorial — culturally embedded.**
Evidence: The civil conflict (1983–2009) pitted the Sinhalese-Buddhist majority against the Tamil minority. Roots: British colonial administrative preference for Tamils, then post-independence **Sinhalese-Buddhist nationalism** that defined "Sri Lankan" as Sinhalese and Buddhist (Tamils, as Hindus or Christians, were excluded from the national definition). The Tamil militant movement (LTTE) sought the homeland Eelam — the conflict became **territorial**: who belongs where.

**Argument 2 — Double exclusion: Arjie is doubly marginal — Tamil + gay.**
Evidence: In Sinhalese spaces (school under Black Tie's Sinhala-only regime), he is Tamil — marked, suspect, in danger. In his Tamil family, he is "funny" — non-conforming, source of shame (forbidden from playing 'bride-bride'). **The two marginalities are not additive but interactive**: being Tamil shapes how his gender non-conformity is experienced; being gay shapes how he experiences ethnic violence (his sensitivity to mechanisms of exclusion comes from his own).

**Argument 3 — The personal as political.**
Evidence: Selvadurai refuses to separate Arjie's sexual coming-of-age from Sri Lanka's political breakdown. The 'Pigs Can't Fly' chapter (the bride-bride game) is about gender policing; 'Radha Aunty' is about cross-ethnic love being broken by communal pressure; 'Small Choices' is about a Tamil family negotiating a Sinhalese-dominated state; the final chapter ('Riot Journal') burns the home in Black July. **The personal experience of exclusion and the political experience of communal violence are the same story.**

**Argument 4 — Intimate geopolitics: the conflict enters the home gradually.**
Evidence: At first the conflict is distant — news, adult conversation. Then it enters more intimately: Radha Aunty's broken cross-ethnic engagement (Anil); the family's mixed social world fracturing; finally the destruction of the family home itself in the 1983 riots. **The geopolitical becomes intimate** — large-scale political conflict is reproduced in intimate everyday spaces (home, school, beach house, friendship). Spaces in the novel are maps of social inclusion and exclusion.

**Argument 5 — Everyday reproduction of conflict.**
Evidence: **Black July did not happen suddenly.** It was prepared by decades of discriminatory legislation (Sinhala Only Act 1956), Sinhalese-Buddhist nationalism in education and media, and the everyday marking of Tamils as 'other'. The novel makes this visible at the micro-level: changing atmospheres at school, the Tamil family's discomfort at Sinhalese events, the increasing impossibility of cross-ethnic friendships. Arjie is a witness and participant in this gradual hardening; **his own experience of exclusion sensitizes him to the larger mechanism**.

**Cross-text:** vs. *Osama* — both centre **doubly marginal** protagonists (Tamil + gay; female + impersonating a boy); both show **identity as policed by political regimes** (Sri Lankan ethnic nationalism; Taliban gender enforcement); both use the private/intimate sphere as the first site of political violence; both refuse triumphal resolution. vs. *Circle of Karma* — both show how **cultural frameworks organize responses to suffering**: in Bhutan karma gives meaning to endurance; in Sri Lanka ethnic identity gives meaning to violence — culture as process can run in productive or destructive directions.

### 3.8 *Hands* — Nepal (short text)

**Form:** Short text read as a **dream narrative** with allegorical / political dimensions.

**Why this text matters:** Nepal is among the least-studied South Asian countries — marginalised within South Asian studies despite its strategic location and rich cultural and political history. The text raises a meta-question: **whose stories count as South Asian literature, and why?**

**Argument 1 — Dream narrative as form — different rules from realist fiction.**
Evidence: In a dream narrative, logic can suspend, time collapses, images carry symbolic weight rather than literal meaning, the boundary between self and other becomes unstable. The form lets the text work allegorically — individual experience can become representative of collective or political realities. Dream narratives are well-suited to representing experiences that cannot be fully articulated in realist terms — trauma, aspiration, political longing, the experience of marginality. **Reading the dream means asking what each image represents and what its emotional logic is — a mode of "reading silence" through symbol and form.**

**Argument 2 — Intergenerational conflict as political allegory.**
Evidence: The conflict between older and younger generations in the text functions as **allegory**: tradition vs. modernity, political stasis vs. desire for change. Nepal's political history makes this resonant — absolute monarchy until 1990, constitutional monarchy, Maoist insurgency 1996–2006 (fundamentally a generational and class conflict, young rural people challenging an old political order), republic 2008. **Allegory is a form of agency under constraint** — using narrative indirection to say what direct speech cannot.

**Argument 3 — Symbolism of hands operating on multiple registers simultaneously.**
- **Hands as labour:** primary instrument of physical work; in an agrarian society, the working body, source of survival and dignity.
- **Hands as relation:** reach toward, hold, strike, withdraw — a father's hand, a son's hand carry authority, care, violence, inheritance.
- **Hands as agency:** to have hands is to be able to act, build, resist — symbol of human capacity and possibility of change.
- **Hands as loss:** harmed, idle, severed hands signify devastation of livelihood, dignity, relational capacity. Particularly powerful in a labour context.

**Argument 4 — Nepal's marginality at multiple scales.**
Evidence: Nepal is marginal *within* South Asia — small, landlocked, mountainous, no sea access, economically dependent on India. **Marginality and migration**: Nepal is one of the largest sources of regional labour migrants — millions of Nepalis labour in India, the Gulf (Qatar World Cup construction deaths), elsewhere — diaspora as forced economic export. **Internal margins**: caste (Brahmin-Chhetri at top, Dalit at bottom), geography (Kathmandu valley vs. remote hills, Terai). Margin–centre operates at multiple scales — Nepal is a margin within a region that is itself a margin within the global system.

**Argument 5 — Canon formation: the meta-question.**
Evidence: **Why is Nepali literature almost entirely absent from South Asian studies curricula?** This is canon formation — the process by which texts come to be considered "important" through institutional, commercial, and political decisions. Circular logic: Nepali texts are not taught because not translated; not translated because no perceived demand; no demand because not taught. **The course's inclusion of Nepal (however briefly) is an argument**: South Asian studies must attend to its own blind spots, its internal canons, its reproduction of centre-margin hierarchies at the level of academic knowledge production.

**Cross-text:** vs. *Machinists / True Cost* — both speak to **labour migration and the body as site of exploitation**: Nepali workers labour abroad for Indian / Gulf capital; Bangladeshi workers labour at home for Western brands. The body of the Global South labours for more powerful actors. The Nepal unit also raises the canon-formation question: **why is Bangladesh's story more globally visible (Rana Plaza in international news) while Nepal's remains obscure?**

### 3.9 *The White Tiger* (2008) — Adiga — India

**Plot in one line:** Balram Halwai, born in "the Darkness" of rural Bihar, becomes a driver for a wealthy Delhi family (the Stork's son Ashok and his wife Pinky), murders Ashok with a broken Johnnie Walker bottle, and uses the stolen money to start a successful taxi service in Bangalore. The novel is told as a series of seven nightly letters from Balram to **Wen Jiabao**, the Chinese Premier visiting India.

**Why this text matters:** Booker Prize 2008. One of the most explicit literary engagements with **post-liberalisation India** — the India of IT, call centres, rising GDP alongside extreme rural poverty.

**Argument 1 — Neoliberal promise vs. structural reality.**
Evidence: India's post-1991 economic liberalisation produced the IT boom and a new middle class; the neoliberal promise is meritocratic — anyone can succeed through effort. Adiga tests this empirically through Balram. Balram is intelligent (the schoolteacher calls him "the white tiger" — once-in-a-generation), but his family pulls him from school to work; structural poverty defeats individual merit. **The promise is real but structurally inaccessible through legitimate means**: his success requires the abandonment of his family (who are then killed in retaliation by the Stork) and the murder of Ashok. Adiga uses Balram's success precisely to *expose* the meritocratic narrative as fiction — the cost of upward mobility is murder.

**Argument 2 — Narrative structure (epistolary, unreliable, retrospective) is itself the politics.**
Evidence: Balram writes seven nightly letters to Wen Jiabao — China's Premier, the *other* rising Asian power. The address is the political logic of the book: he bypasses the Western audience and narrates India to **a sideways global power**. Compare *Kite Runner / Slumdog* (Afghanistan/India narrated *to* the West). Balram is also an **unreliable narrator** — a self-confessed murderer telling his story from a position of success. The reader knows the outcome from the start, so the question is not 'what happens' but **'how does it happen, and what does it cost?'** The moral ambiguity (sympathetic and monstrous at once) is political: it prevents easy condemnation of Balram without also condemning the system that produced him.

**Argument 3 — Spatial structure: the Light and the Darkness.**
Evidence: India is mapped onto two zones: **the Darkness** (rural Bihar, Balram's home — caste hierarchy, feudal landlords ['the Stork', 'the Buffalo'], poverty, immobility) and **the Light** (Delhi, Bangalore — wealth, IT, apparent meritocracy, opportunity). This is a **spatial version of the margin–centre framework**. The geography is **not natural but produced**: post-liberalisation India invested in cities and IT infrastructure while rural areas were structurally neglected. The Light is **materially produced by the Darkness** (Delhi consumes village labour). **Movement between zones is policed**: Balram crosses through violence (murder) because legitimate crossing is foreclosed.

**Argument 4 — The rooster coop — Adiga's theoretical contribution.**
Evidence: Balram's central metaphor: roosters in a tiny cage at a market see their brothers being slaughtered yet do not rebel. Why don't India's poor rebel?
- **Family hostage:** rebel and your family will be killed; the poor are kept in line by their love for kin (Balram's family is in fact killed after he murders Ashok).
- **Internalized servility:** the servant believes himself to be a servant; the cage is in the head — Balram's family members are loyal to the Stork even as he exploits them.
- **Trustworthiness:** the master class trusts servants with money, keys, children, knowing the servant cannot steal because to do so would destroy his family.
**This is brilliant because it is structural, not condescending**: the cage is sustained not by external force but by the very bonds (family) the poor most value.

**Argument 5 — White tiger as exception — does Balram's success critique or affirm the system?**
Evidence: Balram calls himself "the white tiger" — born once in a generation. By framing his success as **exceptional**, the novel holds two readings simultaneously: (A) **system-critique** — exit requires murder, the system is indicted; (B) **system-affirming** — exceptional individuals do escape, the rooster coop has a hatch, the meritocratic narrative is partly vindicated. The novel **does not resolve the ambiguity** — the deepest irony is that Balram, having escaped, **narrates his liberation in the exact entrepreneurial-heroism vocabulary the coop produces** (the white tiger, the self-made man, the son of darkness reaching the light). He has escaped its physical structure but reproduces its ideological grammar.

**Argument 6 — Balram as entrepreneur — neoliberal subject formation.**
Evidence: The novel ends with Balram running a successful taxi company in Bangalore — he is now the employer, not the employee. He has not overthrown the system; he has joined it. The rooster coop for *his* workers remains intact. **Entrepreneurialism is the highest value of neoliberal culture** — the self-made individual creating value through initiative. Balram achieves the ideal. The novel asks: is this a victory for the marginalized, or simply the **reproduction of exploitation with a new person on top?**

**Cross-text:** vs. *Machinists / True Cost* — both interrogate development under globalisation; in Bangladesh global capital exploits, in India domestic capital + caste-class system exploits; both ask who actually benefits from "rising" Asian economies. vs. *Circle of Karma* — see 3.6 (two opposite responses to structural marginality, Buddhist vs. neoliberal). vs. *Hands* — both deal with structural poverty, the rural body, and the mechanisms of its reproduction. vs. *Kite Runner* — Hosseini's redemption arc relies on the protagonist being morally restored through individual action; Adiga's novel **denies that possibility** — Balram's "redemption" is mockery; the structural conditions are not transcended by individual virtue.

## §5 Question-type drills

The instructor named three question types. For each, here is the move.

### 5.1 Comparison between texts and countries
**Structure:** Pick your axis from §4 → name both texts in opening sentence → state the **structural similarity** → state the **revealing difference** → close with what the comparison teaches about South Asia.
**Don't:** plot-summary both texts in parallel paragraphs. **Do:** run a single thesis through both.
**Sample opener:** *"Read together, [Text A] and [Text B] argue that [shared structural insight]; their difference is not in subject but in [aesthetic / addressee / ideology], and that difference is itself the lesson — [insight about South Asia / culture / margin–centre]."*

### 5.2 Familiarity with the text (plot, character, narrative structure)
**Structure:** Lead with a **claim about narrative structure or character function**, not chronological summary. Name a specific scene, character action, or structural feature; analyse what it *does*.
**Don't:** "First X happens, then Y, then Z."
**Do:** *"The novel's [epistolary form / unreliable narrator / non-linear memory structure / narrator-protagonist split / silent female lead] is itself political because [...]. This is visible in the moment when [specific scene with concrete detail]."*
**Use:** specific names (Hassan, Sakina, Ayesha/Veero, Saleem, Tsomo, Arjie, Radha Aunty, Ashok, the Stork, Wen Jiabao, Bishen Singh) and specific scenes (the rape in the alley, "open it", the well, the bride-bride game, Black July, the rooster coop, Toba Tek Singh's no-man's-land).

### 5.3 Culture and South Asia (the two primary categories)
**Structure:** Define **culture as process** in one sentence → define the relevant scale of "South Asia" (national / regional / diasporic / margin-of-margin) → use the text as evidence → state what this teaches about the **construction** of South Asia as a category.
**Anchor question to fall back on:** *Whose South Asia is being constructed in this text, by whom, for what audience, with what consequences?*

### 5.4 Use textual evidence (instructor: "important")
- Quote or paraphrase a **specific scene, line, or character moment**, not a theme.
- Name characters by name and role (servant, narrator, mother, son).
- For films: name a *visual* moment (the opening scene, the discovery scene, the public punishment) — visual evidence counts.
- For Manto: name a story (*Toba Tek Singh*, *Khol Do*, *Thanda Gosht*) and one defining detail per story.
- For Bhutalia: she is a *secondary source* — cite her by argument (oral history methodology recovers what official archives erased).

## §7 Standard paragraph template (use verbatim)

> **Argument:** [Claim using a named framework — e.g., "*Khamosh Pani* operates a 'partition as continued event' logic, in which 1947's gendered violence does not stay in 1947 but lives on in Veero/Ayesha's silenced body in 1979."]
>
> **Textual evidence:** [Specific scene/character/line — e.g., "Ayesha lives daily as 'Ayesha' rather than 'Veero'; her son Saleem's 1979 radicalization mirrors the communal logic that produced her abduction in 1947; the arrival of the Sikh pilgrims after 32 years reopens the wound."]

> **And yet…** [The contradiction — e.g., "the silence that lets her survive is also the silence that lets the violence remain unmetabolised; her endurance and the regime's continuity rest on the same suppression."]
>
> **So what?** [What the contradiction reveals — e.g., "Trauma cannot be metabolised inside the regime that produced it; the post-Partition nation reproduces itself by *requiring* women's silence. The 1979 setting is not background but argument."]
