# HOUR 1 — Theoretical Frameworks ⭐

**Goal:** Internalize 6 frameworks deeply enough to deploy in any prompt within 90 seconds.

**Why first:** Every question in a literature comprehensive forces you to pick a *lens*. If your lens is slow to retrieve, every answer suffers. By the end of this hour, these 6 frameworks should feel automatic.

---

## Framework 1: Culture as Process

**The core idea:** Culture is not an artifact (a thing) but a process (an activity). Every "cultural identity" is being made, contested, performed, reshaped — never finished.

**The intuition:** When you say "Pakistani culture" or "Bhutanese culture," whose version do you mean? The 1947 version? The diaspora's version? The Taliban's version? Tsomo's? They all conflict. **Culture is the site of conflict, not the resolution of it.**

**The opposite (which the texts reject):** Culture as a list of customs, foods, festivals — a fixed essence you either belong to or don't.

**Deploy when:** Any prompt asks about "South Asian culture," "tradition," "heritage," "belonging." Refuse the static frame.

**Texts that demonstrate it:**
- *Circle of Karma* — Bhutanese Buddhism is shown being LIVED and SUFFERED, not described as doctrine
- *Funny Boy* — Sri Lankan identity is being made through who counts as Sri Lankan (Tamil exclusion)
- *Khamosh Pani* — Pakistani identity is being remade in 1979 through Islamization
- *White Tiger* — "Indian culture" of servitude (rooster coop) is being actively reproduced and contested

**Strong move in essay:** "The text demonstrates **culture as process** by depicting [character] not as the bearer of a stable culture but as actively negotiating, performing, or contesting cultural meaning under specific historical pressure."

---

## Framework 2: Margin–Centre

**The core idea:** Power operates by establishing **centers** (whose voices are normal/legitimate/heard) and **margins** (whose voices are exotic/illegitimate/silent). Margin–centre is **relational, not geographical** — it shifts depending on context.

**5 levels at which margin-centre operates:**

| Level | Centre | Margin | Example texts |
|---|---|---|---|
| Geopolitical | The West | South Asia | Kite Runner, Slumdog |
| National | Dominant ethnicity/religion | Minorities | Funny Boy (Tamil), Lajja (Hindu in Bangladesh) |
| Within community | Men | Women | Osama, Khamosh Pani, Circle of Karma |
| Class | Upper class | Workers/servants | White Tiger, garment industry films |
| Narrative | Whose "I" tells the story | Whose story is told *about* | Kite Runner (Amir tells, Hassan is told about) |

**Crucial nuance:** Manto's signature move is **inversion** — he places the formal margin (sex workers, the insane, criminals) at the narrative centre, exposing how "respectable" society's power depends on excluding them.

**Deploy when:** Almost any prompt. Always ask three questions: (1) Who is centered? (2) Who is marginal? (3) What does the centering *do*?

**Strong move in essay:** "The text operates a **margin–centre** logic in which [X] occupies the centre by virtue of [structural feature]; [Y] is rendered marginal not by accident but by the same structural operation. The text's politics lie in whether it accepts, complicates, or inverts this arrangement."

---

## Framework 3: Transnationalism & Globalization

**The core idea:** People, capital, texts, and meanings cross borders. The nation-state is no longer (was never) a sealed container. South Asia is constantly traveling — and being constructed — across borders.

**Two distinct flows:**

1. **Bodies:** Diaspora, migration, refugees, NRIs (non-resident Indians). Movement of people.
2. **Texts and capital:** Books, films, fashion, ideas, commodities. Movement of objects and meanings.

**The crucial asymmetry:** Capital and culture move freely; bodies do not. A Bangladeshi shirt enters America without a visa; a Bangladeshi worker cannot. The Kite Runner crosses borders effortlessly; Afghan refugees do not.

**Texts:**
- *Kite Runner* — Afghan diaspora in California; the novel itself is a transnational object
- *Reluctant Fundamentalist* — Pakistani Princeton/Wall Street trajectory
- *Namesake* — Indian-American second-generation
- *White Tiger* — addressed to the Chinese Premier, signaling India's place in a globalized order
- *True Cost / Machinists* — supply chains as the infrastructure of globalization
- *Manto* — partition created the very borders this framework crosses

**Deploy when:** Texts involve diaspora, migration, global supply chains, or texts circulating internationally.

**Strong move in essay:** "**Globalization is asymmetric** in this text — capital moves but bodies are constrained, or texts circulate but the lives they depict cannot. The text's critical force lies in making this asymmetry visible."

---

## Framework 4: Gendering South Asia

**The core idea:** "Gendering" is a verb. It means analyzing how power is **structured by** gender — how access to public space, narrative, survival, and dignity is unequally distributed along gender lines. Gender is not a topic; it is a lens.

**5 mechanisms to recognize:**

1. **Public/private binary:** Patriarchy relegates women to "private" spaces; any incursion into public is policed as transgression. (*Osama* makes this explicit through Taliban erasure; *Funny Boy* through domestic policing of Arjie's gender play.)

2. **Body as territory:** Gender violence treats women's bodies as symbolic territory. Partition rapes (Manto, Khamosh Pani) attack communities *through* women's bodies. The garment worker's body bears the cost of global capital (*True Cost*).

3. **Honor logic:** Women's "honor" stands in for community/national honor. This is why partition violence concentrated on women — to violate them was to violate the other community.

4. **Silence as gendered:** Whose speech is heard? Whose silence is read as consent? (*Khamosh Pani* makes silence visible as the form women's testimony often takes.)

5. **Performance:** *Osama* exposes gender as performance — when the disguise becomes survival, you see that femininity and masculinity are *enactments*, not essences.

**Deploy when:** Any prompt mentioning gender, women, sexuality, body, identity, or power.

**Strong move in essay:** "**Gendering** [text] reveals not just that women appear in the narrative but that gender is the *structural mechanism* through which [violence/silence/labour/agency] is distributed. The text exposes gender as a political infrastructure, not a thematic flavor."

---

## Framework 5: Politics of Representation

**The core idea:** When X represents Y, something political is happening. Representation is never neutral.

**3 layers to interrogate:**

1. **Mimesis:** How accurately does the representation reflect what it depicts?
2. **Power:** Who has the platform to represent? (Hosseini represents Afghanistan globally; an actual Hazara peasant does not.)
3. **Reception:** Who consumes the representation? What ideological work does it do for them?

**The Western reception problem (recurs across course):**

| Text | What it offers Western readers | What it lets them avoid |
|---|---|---|
| *Kite Runner* | Universal redemption arc; understandable Afghanistan | Confronting US complicity in creating the Taliban |
| *Slumdog Millionaire* | Visual spectacle of poverty; rags-to-riches optimism | Structural critique of inequality |
| *White Tiger* | First-person rage from below; sociological texture | (resists this trap — see Hour 2) |
| *True Cost* | Direct confrontation: "you wear what they died making" | (also resists — direct accusation) |

**Deploy when:** Any text written for or consumed by international audiences; any film with Western directors/funding.

**Strong move in essay:** "The **politics of representation** in this text turn on its addressee. Who is being told this story, and what are they invited to *do* with it? The text either reinforces a centre-margin extraction (the West consumes South Asia as spectacle) or disrupts it (the West is implicated, accused, or destabilized)."

---

## Framework 6: Diaspora & Identity in Displacement

**The core idea:** When people move, identity does not stay still. The diasporic subject is forged in the *gap* between origin and adoption — often unable to fully belong to either.

**4 features of the diasporic condition:**

1. **Hybridity:** Multiple cultural belongings coexist, not always peacefully.
2. **Frozen homeland:** The diasporic "homeland" is reimagined, often more "traditional" than the homeland itself, because culture freezes at the moment of migration.
3. **Generational gap:** First generation experiences displacement; second generation experiences rootlessness *without* the displacement memory.
4. **Authenticity wars:** Who is the "real" Indian/Pakistani/Afghan? Diasporic communities often police authenticity more rigidly than the homeland.

**Texts:**
- *Kite Runner* — Amir's California life as escape, but his Kabul guilt as the unfinished business of diaspora
- *Reluctant Fundamentalist* — Changez's hybrid identity collapses under post-9/11 pressure; he discovers belonging is not granted by performance
- *Namesake* — Gogol's Bengali-American negotiation; intergenerational diaspora

**Deploy when:** Any text involving migration, exile, refugee, NRI, second-generation experience.

**Strong move in essay:** "Diaspora is not nostalgia — it is constant **negotiation**. The text figures identity not as a stable home one carries from origin to destination but as a continuous renegotiation in the gap between."

---

## How to deploy these in essays

**The cardinal rule:** Pick ONE primary framework, support with 1-2 others. Don't list all six.

**Deployment template:**
1. **Open** with a thesis using the primary framework: "X demonstrates [framework] through [textual feature]."
2. **Evidence 1:** Specific scene/character that illustrates the framework operating
3. **Evidence 2:** Second specific moment that complicates or deepens the operation
4. **Counter-move:** A complication, ambiguity, or limit of the framework's reading
5. **Conclusion:** What this analysis reveals about the broader course question (South Asia as region, power, representation, etc.)

# HOUR 2 — India: The White Tiger (Priority #2)

**Goal:** Master the richest analytical text in the course. White Tiger is statistically likely to anchor at least one essay prompt.

---

## Plot in 90 seconds

Balram Halwai, born in "the Darkness" (rural Bihar), works his way to becoming a driver for a wealthy landlord's son Ashok in Delhi. He kills Ashok, steals his money, and uses it to start a taxi service in Bangalore. The novel is structured as a series of letters Balram writes to **Wen Jiabao**, the Chinese Premier visiting India, telling him "the real story" of Indian entrepreneurship.

That's it. Don't waste exam time on plot summary.

---

## The 6 analytical moves you must own

### Move 1: Neoliberal Promise vs Structural Reality

**The argument:** The novel is set during the "India Shining" / IT-boom era. The state's narrative was meritocracy: anyone can rise in the new India. Adiga tests this empirically through Balram. **Verdict: rising requires murder, not merit.** The system is rigged; only structural violation breaks the rooster coop.

**Evidence:**
- Balram's intelligence (the white tiger) is recognized in school but the family pulls him from school to work — structural poverty defeats individual merit
- His success requires (a) his family being effectively abandoned/killed by the landlord in retaliation; (b) murdering Ashok. Both are violences the meritocratic narrative cannot acknowledge.

**Deploy:** "Adiga deploys Balram's success precisely to *expose* the meritocratic narrative as fiction. The novel does not refute neoliberalism by showing failure; it refutes it by showing that the cost of success is murder and the abandonment of family."

### Move 2: Epistolary Structure — Who Is the Audience?

**The argument:** The novel is structured as letters to **Wen Jiabao**, Chinese Premier. This addressee is not incidental — it is the entire political logic of the book.

Why Wen? Because:
- China is the OTHER rising Asian power
- Wen is visiting India to study its "model"
- Balram positions himself as an authoritative explainer of "real India" — implying the official Indian narrative is propaganda
- The Chinese Premier is a figure who, like Balram, is supposed to know about scale, hierarchy, and revolution

**Compare to Kite Runner / Slumdog:** Those texts narrate India/Afghanistan to a Western audience. White Tiger narrates India to **another rising power** — a sideways global gaze that bypasses the West.

**Deploy:** "The epistolary structure makes the **politics of representation** central: Balram is performing for Wen, which means we are reading a *performed* truth, not a transparent confession. The novel knows that any narration of India is also a politics of who is being addressed."

### Move 3: Light and Darkness — Spatial Logic

**The argument:** The novel maps India onto two zones:
- **The Darkness:** Rural India (Bihar, the Ganges village). Servitude, tradition, immobility, death.
- **The Light:** Urban India (Delhi, Bangalore, Gurgaon). Mobility, modernity, possibility, reinvention.

But the spatial binary is a trap, not an aspiration:
- Movement from Darkness to Light is what Balram desires AND it is the journey that destroys him morally
- The Light depends on the Darkness — Delhi consumes the rural labor of the village
- The boundary is policed by the master class (the landlord, Ashok), not chosen by individuals

**Deploy:** "The light/darkness spatial logic appears to be aspirational geography, but Adiga uses it to expose the **infrastructure of exploitation**: the Light is materially produced by the Darkness; the boundary is enforced by violence; transgression requires moral self-destruction."

### Move 4: The Rooster Coop ⭐ (Adiga's most original concept)

**The argument:** Why don't India's poor rebel? Balram's answer is the rooster coop:

> *"In the old days, hundreds of cocks would be kept here in a tiny cage, packed as tight as worms in a belly... they see the organs of their brothers lying around them. They know they're next. Yet they do not rebel. They do not try to get out of the coop."*

The rooster coop is sustained NOT by external force but by:
1. **Family hostage:** Rebel and your family will be killed. The poor are kept in line by their love for kin.
2. **Internalized servility:** The servant believes himself to be a servant. The cage is in the head.
3. **Trustworthiness:** The Indian master class trusts servants with its money, its keys, its children — knowing that the servant *cannot* steal because to do so would destroy his own family.

**Why this is brilliant:** It explains a sociological puzzle (why does India have such low rates of class rebellion despite extreme inequality?) without the usual condescending answers (the poor are stupid, religious, fatalistic). It points to a *structural* mechanism: the family as a hostage held by the master class.

**Deploy:** "The rooster coop is Adiga's **theoretical contribution** — not just a metaphor but a structural analysis of how Indian inequality reproduces itself. Servitude is not enforced from above; it is enforced from within, through the very bonds (family) that the poor most value."

### Move 5: White Tiger as Exception — Does Balram's Success Critique or Affirm the System?

**The deep ambiguity (this is exam gold):**

Balram is "the white tiger" — a creature born once in a generation. By calling himself this, he frames his success as **exceptional**, not replicable.

Two readings:
- **Reading A (system-critique):** Balram's success exposes the system's brutality — the only way out is murder. The novel is a structural indictment.
- **Reading B (system-affirming):** Balram's success vindicates the system — exceptional individuals do escape; the rooster coop has a hatch. The novel is the very meritocratic narrative it claims to critique.

The text holds BOTH. Don't resolve it.

**Deploy:** "The novel's final irony is that Balram, having escaped the rooster coop, narrates his liberation in the *exact language* of entrepreneurial heroism that the coop produces — the white tiger, the self-made man, the son of darkness who reaches the light. He has escaped its physical structure but reproduces its ideological grammar. The novel asks whether escape is possible without complicity."

### Move 6: Sycophancy, Servitude, and Neoliberal Subject Formation

**The argument:** Balram describes the servant's posture in vivid terms — bowing, smiling, anticipating wishes, never resting. This isn't just oppression; it is **subject formation**. The servant becomes a particular kind of person.

This connects to a broader claim about neoliberalism: the new economy doesn't just exploit labour, it produces a *type* of person — the eager-to-please, self-monitoring, infinitely available worker. Balram's "service" mirrors the call-center worker, the gig laborer, the smiling Uber driver.

**Deploy:** "Adiga shows that servitude is not just an economic position — it is a **subject formation**. The body, the smile, the deference are produced by the system. Balram's break with this formation is not just a class jump; it is a refusal of the kind of person India is being produced to be."

# HOUR 3 — India: Slumdog Millionaire + India Synthesis

**Goal:** Position Slumdog as White Tiger's foil; lock in the comparative argument.

---

## Slumdog Millionaire (2008) — 4 analytical moves

### Move 1: Poverty Porn Debate

**The argument:** Slumdog was accused — most famously by Salman Rushdie and various Indian critics — of "poverty porn": aestheticizing extreme poverty for Western consumption. Mumbai's slums become a visual playground; suffering becomes spectacle.

**Evidence for the charge:**
- Vibrant, kinetic cinematography of squalor (Boyle's signature style)
- Western director (Danny Boyle), Western screenwriter (Simon Beaufoy), Western funding
- The film won massively in the West (8 Oscars including Best Picture); reception in India was more mixed
- The narrative arc resolves through *individual luck and love* — not through any structural critique

**Evidence against:**
- The film does show systemic violence (police torture, child trafficking, communal riots)
- The slum is depicted with affection AND brutality
- Jamal's success is improbable — the film knows it is fairy tale

**Deploy:** "The poverty porn debate is irreducible — both readings are textually supported. The deeper point is that the **politics of representation** require asking who is consuming what, with what effect, regardless of the filmmakers' intentions."

### Move 2: The Game Show as Capitalist Allegory

**The argument:** *Who Wants to Be a Millionaire* is the perfect symbol of neoliberal global culture:
- Individual contestants competing for money
- Knowledge as commodity
- "Lock it in" — the irreversibility of decisions, the high-stakes individuation
- A global format (every country has a version) — globalization at the level of culture

The film uses the game show structure to ask: in this world, **how is success determined?** The answer is *NOT* meritocracy (Jamal isn't smart) and *NOT* hard work — it is the random accident of his life having taught him these specific things.

**Deploy:** "The game show in *Slumdog* functions as a **neoliberal allegory** — a stylized arena in which the question of how individuals succeed is staged. The film's answer is not meritocratic but accidental, undermining the very ideology the format embodies."

### Move 3: Western Gaze on Mumbai

**The argument:** The film constructs Mumbai for non-Indian audiences. Indian viewers see a familiar city; international viewers see "the global slum." This is the Kite Runner problem in cinematic form.

But: Indian audiences have also embraced the film. The film's politics cannot be reduced to "outsider gaze" — it has been claimed and rejected by multiple constituencies.

**Deploy:** "Slumdog enacts the **politics of representation** by being legible primarily to non-Indian audiences while depicting an Indian space. Whether this is exploitation or translation depends on whose terms we accept — there is no neutral position."

### Move 4: Romantic Resolution as Ideological Closure

**The argument:** The film resolves through Jamal and Latika's union. Love conquers all — the slum, the gangsters, the game show. This is **Bollywood-style ideological closure**: structural problems are resolved at the level of the individual romantic pair.

Compare White Tiger: Balram has no romantic redemption. His wife (a brief mention) is gone. His liberation is cold, monetary, and morally compromised. White Tiger refuses the romantic closure that Slumdog accepts.

**Deploy:** "The romantic resolution in *Slumdog* performs **ideological closure** — the film resolves at the personal level what it cannot resolve at the structural level. *White Tiger*, in contrast, withholds this consolation, forcing the reader to sit with the cost of escape."

---

## The White Tiger vs Slumdog comparison ⭐

This comparison is high-probability for the exam.

| Feature | White Tiger | Slumdog Millionaire |
|---|---|---|
| Form | Novel, epistolary, first-person | Film, third-person omniscient (with flashbacks) |
| Hero | Balram — murderer, anti-hero | Jamal — innocent, sympathetic |
| Path to success | Murder | Coincidence + love |
| Critique style | First-person rage, ironic | Visual exuberance, romantic |
| Audience addressed | Wen Jiabao (sideways global) | Western audiences (direct) |
| Ideological work | Refuses closure | Romantic closure |
| Politics of poverty | Structural | Visual/spectacular |

**The synthesis argument:** Both texts depict the underclass rising. White Tiger uses literary irony to critique the system that requires murder for mobility. Slumdog uses cinematic spectacle to celebrate (or aestheticize) the rare escape. The two texts represent the **two dominant modes** in which contemporary South Asian poverty enters global circulation: as ideological critique (White Tiger) and as visual consumption (Slumdog).

---

## India unit — synthesis arguments

If you get an India-only essay prompt, your spine arguments are:

1. **Both texts depict mobility through exception** — Balram is the white tiger; Jamal is the slum boy who wins the game show. Neither offers structural transformation.
2. **Both texts are addressed to global audiences** — but Slumdog accepts the Western frame; White Tiger displaces it onto China.
3. **Both texts use form to make their political argument** — the novel's epistolary irony (White Tiger); the film's visual exuberance (Slumdog).
4. **Together they map the politics of representation of contemporary India** — the IT-boom era's promise (visible in Jamal's success) and its lie (audible in Balram's confession).

# HOUR 4 — Pakistan: Manto (Priority #3)

**Goal:** Master Manto's technique and what it reveals about partition, censorship, and power.

---

## Why Manto matters in this course

Manto (1912-1955) is the **most analytically generative** Pakistani writer on the syllabus. His stories are short — you can read several quickly — and his technique is precise enough to give you concrete deployable arguments.

The course covers him through **partition, character writing, censorship, and center-margin** — these are your four spines.

---

## The 4 analytical moves

### Move 1: Partition as Backdrop, Not Subject

**The argument:** Manto never writes a "partition novel" with grand political vision. He writes **partition's residue** — the human aftermath. The violence is off-stage; its effects are on the page.

**Why this matters:**
- Refuses the heroic/political framing of partition (no Nehru, no Jinnah, no high politics)
- Centers the lives wrecked by the political event — sex workers, refugees, ordinary people
- Treats partition as **continuing event**, not historical episode (people are still living its consequences)

**Key story:** *Toba Tek Singh* — a Sikh inmate of a Pakistani lunatic asylum is to be transferred to India under partition's "exchange." He refuses, demanding to know whether his hometown Toba Tek Singh is in India or Pakistan. No one can tell him. He dies in the no-man's-land between borders.

**This story does so much:**
- The asylum as metonym for partition's logic — people sorted by religion as if by madness
- The "lunatic" as the only one who recognizes the absurdity
- The no-man's-land as the only honest place for partition's victims
- The body-on-the-border as the image of partition's cost

**Deploy:** "Manto's signature move is to displace **partition** from the level of national event to the level of human residue. Partition is not the subject of his stories — it is the structuring absence from which his characters cannot escape."

### Move 2: The Art of Character Writing — Centering the Margin

**The argument:** Manto's characters are systematically the marginal — sex workers, criminals, the insane, the displaced. He gives them **interiority and dignity** at the moment the new states (India, Pakistan) are drawing lines of belonging that exclude them.

This is a political act. The newly-partitioned states wanted to define their citizens — respectable, religious, productive. Manto's characters are none of these. By centering them, he refuses the new state's definition of who counts.

**Examples:**
- *Khol Do* — A father searches for his daughter Sakina after partition violence. When found, she is so traumatized by repeated rape that she mechanically responds to "khol do" (open it) by opening her clothes. The father weeps with joy that she is alive; the doctor recognizes the horror.
- *Thanda Gosht* — An adulterous couple; the man is impotent because he attempted to rape an already-dead girl during partition violence. The story makes partition violence visceral and inseparable from sexual life.
- *Bu* — A man's obsession with a tribal woman's body odor. Allegedly obscene; deeply about class and racialized desire.

**Deploy:** "Manto's **character writing** is a political method. By centering sex workers, the insane, and the criminalized — the people the new nation-state rendered marginal — he makes interiority and dignity available to those the nationalist project excluded."

### Move 3: Obscenity Trials as Productive Censorship ⭐

**The argument:** Manto was tried for obscenity multiple times, most famously for *Khol Do*, *Thanda Gosht*, and *Bu*. He was convicted in some cases. The trials are usually framed as "Manto vs the state" — but the more analytical move is to ask:

**What does censorship reveal?** What the state finds threatening tells you what it most needs to suppress. The Pakistani state in the late 40s/early 50s found Manto's depictions of female sexuality, partition rape, and male impotence threatening because:

1. They acknowledged that partition violence was sexual — which the state wanted to deny
2. They depicted female desire and agency — which the patriarchal nation-state wanted contained
3. They showed national heroes (the Muslim man) as morally compromised — which nationalism could not tolerate

**The Foucault move:** Censorship is not just suppression — it is **productive**. It produces categories (obscene/respectable), generates discourse (court cases, public debate), and reveals the contours of power. Manto's trials are not the silencing of his work — they are the proof of its political force.

**Deploy:** "Manto's obscenity trials demonstrate **productive censorship**: the state's attempt to suppress him *reveals* what it most needs to deny — partition's sexual violence, female agency, and the moral ambiguity of the new nation. Censorship is not silence; it is the loud admission of what cannot be borne."

### Move 4: Center-Margin Inversion

**The argument:** In Manto, the formally "respectable" characters (politicians, soldiers, landlords, religious figures) are morally bankrupt. The formally "disreputable" characters (sex workers, the mad, the criminal) retain humanity, complexity, even nobility.

This is a sustained **inversion of the center-margin logic**. Society's center is hollow; its margin holds the moral weight.

**This connects to broader course frameworks:**
- Margin–centre framework (Hour 1) — Manto enacts inversion as a literary practice
- Politics of representation — Manto's centering of the margin is itself a representational politics

**Deploy:** "Manto enacts a sustained **center-margin inversion**: the social center (state, respectable society) is hollowed out morally, while the social margin (sex worker, lunatic, refugee) is invested with the human dignity the center claims but cannot sustain."

# HOUR 5 — Pakistan: Khamosh Pani + Reluctant Fundamentalist

**Goal:** Two films, two distinct arguments. Khamosh Pani for partition/gender/silence. Reluctant Fundamentalist for post-9/11 hybridity.

## Khamosh Pani (2003, dir. Sabiha Sumar) — 4 analytical moves

### Setting and plot in 60 seconds
Set in 1979, Charkhi village (Punjab, Pakistan), during Zia-ul-Haq's Islamization campaign. Ayesha is a Muslim widow raising her son Saleem. Her son becomes radicalized. Sikh pilgrims arrive seeking lost relatives from partition. Ayesha is revealed to be a Sikh who was kidnapped/abducted/converted during partition (in 1947). When confronted, she takes her own life by jumping into the village well. Her son becomes a hardline Islamist.

### Move 1: Gendering the Partition

**The argument:** Partition is conventionally narrated as a political event (independence, two-nation theory, mass migration). Khamosh Pani insists partition is **inseparable from gender violence** — and that the violence is sustained, not closed.

Mechanisms:
- The well (where Ayesha fetches water; where she ultimately drowns) is the spatial metaphor for women's relation to community life — Sikh women jumped into wells to escape partition rape
- Ayesha's body bears the secret of partition for 32 years
- Her conversion was forced (whether through abduction, marriage, or survival) — the narrative carefully refuses to clarify, because *forced* is the only honest description across all those modes
- The patriarchal frame: women's "honor" was the site partition violence operated on; her family's "honor" is what she chose silence to protect

**Deploy:** "Khamosh Pani makes visible the **gendering of partition**: the political event was lived in women's bodies, and continues to live there. The film refuses the clean narrative of partition's closure by showing it active in 1979, in Ayesha's silence."

### Move 2: Partition as Continued Event

**The argument:** Set in 1979 (Zia's Islamization), the film shows partition not as a 1947 event but as a logic that recurs. The same religious-nationalist mechanism that displaced Ayesha in 1947 now radicalizes her son in 1979. **Partition is a structure, not an episode.**

This is a major analytical contribution:
- Refuses linear history (partition → independence → modernity)
- Insists on the **recursivity** of religious nationalism
- Predicts (the film was made in 2003) the further intensifications post-9/11

**Connects to:** Manto (partition as residue), Lajja (1992 communal violence as partition's echo).

**Deploy:** "The film refuses the historicist closure that treats partition as a 1947 event. By setting itself in 1979, it shows partition as a **continuing structure** — the same religious-nationalist logic that displaced Ayesha now radicalizes her son. Partition is recursive, not historical."

### Move 3: Silence as Stylistic and Political Device

**The argument:** Ayesha's silence is the film's central form. She does not testify, explain, or accuse. The film respects this silence as a *form of speech* — silence is what survival required, and it is what remembering requires too.

The film's title — *Khamosh Pani* (Silent Waters) — names this silence as central. The well is silent water. Ayesha's history is silent water. Partition's truth is silent water.

**Reading silence (Urvashi Butalia's contribution):**
- Butalia, in *The Other Side of Silence*, collected women's testimonies of partition that official archives erased
- Silence, for women survivors, was often the only available form — testimony cost too much
- Reading silence as text is itself a feminist methodology

**Deploy:** "The film deploys **silence as stylistic and political device**. Ayesha's silence is not absence of speech but a form of speech — the form survival demanded. The film does what Urvashi Butalia does archivally: it makes silence legible as women's testimony of partition."

### Move 4: Intergenerational Transmission of Trauma and Radicalism

**The argument:** Saleem, Ayesha's son, becomes radicalized — joining the Islamist movement that Zia is licensing. There's a brutal irony: Saleem doesn't know his mother is Sikh. His radicalism is built on a national/religious identity that is itself a partition fiction.

The film shows trauma's intergenerational logic:
- The mother's silence does not protect the son — it deprives him of a history that would complicate his identity
- The father's absence (he died) leaves an ideological vacuum filled by Zia's Islamization
- The son becomes the very kind of religious militant that destroyed his mother's life

**Deploy:** "The film stages **intergenerational trauma transmission**: the mother's protective silence enables the son's radicalization. The very absence of her history — which she withheld for survival — leaves him available to a religious nationalism that is itself partition's afterlife."

## The Reluctant Fundamentalist (2013, dir. Mira Nair) — 3 analytical moves

### Setting and plot in 60 seconds
Changez, a Pakistani man educated at Princeton, works in a Wall Street consulting firm. He is in Manila during 9/11 and, watching the towers fall, smiles — a moment that haunts the film. As post-9/11 anti-Muslim climate intensifies, his American belonging cracks. He returns to Pakistan, becomes a university professor, and is suspected of fundamentalism. The frame: he tells his story to an American journalist (or CIA operative — ambiguous) over dinner in Lahore.

### Move 1: Post-9/11 Identity and the Collapse of Hybridity

**The argument:** Changez bought into the American dream as completely as one can: Princeton, Wall Street, white girlfriend, the works. 9/11 reveals this belonging was conditional — granted only as long as he didn't read as "Muslim" in a way the post-9/11 climate began to enforce.

The smile during 9/11 is the film's key moment. Why does Changez smile? He himself doesn't fully know. But the smile is the registration of a previously-unconscious resentment — a recognition that the empire whose center he had reached has just been struck. He is a Pakistani Muslim watching America wounded. The smile is **the involuntary admission that hybridity was always shadowed**.

**Deploy:** "The film stages the **collapse of post-9/11 hybridity**: Changez's American belonging is revealed as conditional, sustainable only while his Muslim identity remained illegible. 9/11 makes him legible against his will, and his hybrid self cannot survive that legibility."

### Move 2: The Framing Device — Surveillance Made Form

**The argument:** Changez tells his story to an American across a table in Lahore. The American may be a journalist or a CIA operative — the film keeps this ambiguous until late.

This framing is **the film's politics of representation in formal shape**:
- South Asians are perpetually narrating themselves to suspicious Westerners post-9/11
- The American audience is structurally interrogator — friendly or hostile
- The text exists only because it is being demanded by the American interlocutor

**Compare White Tiger:** Adiga's epistolary form addresses the Chinese Premier — sideways global. Reluctant Fundamentalist addresses the American — directly back into the imperial center. The two films figure two different positions of South Asian narration in global circuits.

**Deploy:** "The framing device — Changez narrating to an American who may be CIA — makes **surveillance the form of the text**. The film does not just depict post-9/11 surveillance of Muslims; it enacts surveillance as the very condition under which Pakistani self-narration becomes possible."

### Move 3: Fundamentalism as Misnomer

**The argument:** The film's title is ironic. Changez is a "reluctant fundamentalist" — but the question is what kind of fundamentalist. Religious? Political? Or something else?

The film suggests Changez has not become a religious fundamentalist; he has returned to a position of national/political loyalty after America's betrayal. The Western reader/viewer's assumption that "fundamentalist" means "Islamic terrorist" is itself part of what the film critiques.

There's also a deeper move: **the film accuses American capitalism of being its own fundamentalism.** Changez's job at the consulting firm reduces companies to numbers, fires workers, follows a doctrine ruthlessly. This is "fundamentalism" too — economic, secular, but no less rigid.

**Deploy:** "The title's irony cuts two ways: Changez is not the Islamic fundamentalist the West fears; *and* American capitalism is itself a kind of fundamentalism — doctrinal, ruthless, devoted to a single principle. The film redistributes the term, refusing the West's monopoly on its meaning."

## Comparative Argument 1: Gender and Violence Across South Asia

**Spine thesis:** Across the South Asian texts in this course, women's bodies become the **site where political violence is made personal and intimate**. Each text exposes a different mechanism through which gender mediates power — but the pattern is structural, not coincidental.

**Texts and their specific mechanism:**

| Text | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Osama (Afghanistan) | Public erasure of women under Taliban; gender as the axis of state-controlled visibility |
| Khamosh Pani (Pakistan) | Partition violence on women's bodies; women's silence as the form of survival and the cost of the political settlement |
| Manto (Pakistan) | Partition rape rendered through specific characters (Sakina in Khol Do); women as bearers of communal violence |
| Circle of Karma (Bhutan) | Women's bodies as sites of labour and karmic suffering; spirituality and corporeality intersect on the female body |
| Funny Boy (Sri Lanka) | Domestic policing of gender (Arjie's bride-bride play); the family as a site of gender enforcement before the political enters |
| The Machinists / True Cost (Bangladesh) | Working women's bodies as the cost of global supply chains; capital extracted through gendered labour |

**The deeper argument:** This is not a list — it is a structural claim. South Asian literature consistently exposes gender as the *infrastructure* through which political violence operates. To gender these texts is to see that what looks like "personal" experience is the most political content of all.

**Counter-move (to show range):** But gender is also where agency emerges. Tsomo's pilgrimage, Ayesha's silence as a chosen form, Osama's strategic disguise, Manto's sex workers' refusal of nationalist categorization — all are forms of gendered agency under constraint. The argument is not that women are only victims but that gender is the lens through which both violence and agency operate in this canon.

---

## Comparative Argument 2: Partition and Its Afterlives

**Spine thesis:** Partition (1947) is not a historical event in the South Asian literary canon — it is a **structure of recursive violence** that returns whenever religious nationalism rises. The texts in this course return to partition obsessively because it has not been resolved.

**Texts:**

| Text | What it does with partition |
|---|---|
| Manto | Partition as immediate residue — the human aftermath of 1947 |
| Khamosh Pani | Partition as continuing — Ayesha's secret in 1979; her son's radicalization as partition's afterlife |
| Lajja (Taslima Nasrin) | Partition's logic recurring in 1992 (Babri Masjid demolition) — Hindu minority persecution in Bangladesh as the same religious-nationalist mechanism |
| Reluctant Fundamentalist | Post-9/11 as another inflection — Pakistani identity continually re-formed by religious-nationalist pressures |
| Funny Boy | Sri Lanka's ethnic conflict (1983 anti-Tamil pogroms) as a parallel structure of communal violence — partition's logic in a different national setting |

**The deeper argument:** Partition is not a Pakistani or Indian phenomenon — it is the South Asian *form* through which religious nationalism produces violence. The Sri Lankan civil war, the 1992 communal violence, the Zia Islamization, post-9/11 Pakistani identity politics all share the partition structure: the use of religious-ethnic identity to define national belonging through expulsion of the other.

**Counter-move:** Yet each text refuses the closure of mourning. None of them say: "and now we have made peace with this." They keep the wound open, because closure would normalize the violence.

---

## Comparative Argument 3: Labour, Capital, and Bodies

**Spine thesis:** Across the texts, capitalism's promise (development, mobility, modernity) is shown to depend on the **exploitation of specific bodies — gendered, classed, racialized.** South Asian literature consistently makes the body of the worker, the servant, the subaltern visible as the cost of the global economy.

**Texts:**

| Text | The body / the labour |
|---|---|
| White Tiger | The servant body — Balram's smile, deference, anticipation; servitude as subject formation |
| The Machinists / True Cost (Bangladesh) | The garment worker's body — Rana Plaza as the literal materialization of supply-chain violence |
| Slumdog Millionaire | The slum child as labour pool (begging rings, child trafficking); the slum itself as labour for global media |
| Circle of Karma | Tsomo's body as site of karmic and labouring suffering — the spiritual is corporeal |
| Funny Boy | The labour of performing normative gender; the cost of being illegible |

**The deeper argument:** "Globalization" or "development" is not abstract — it is materially produced by specific bodies. The texts in this course refuse the abstraction. They insist: *whose* body bears the cost? Where is the labour? Who is invisible?

**Counter-move:** The texts also explore agency within labour — Balram's escape (however compromised), the garment workers' organizing (in True Cost), Tsomo's pilgrimage as labour-toward-meaning. Labour is not only exploitation; it is also where lives are lived.

---

## Comparative Argument 4: Who Tells the Story? (The Politics of Narration)

**Spine thesis:** South Asian texts are obsessively self-conscious about **who is telling the story, to whom, in what language**. The very form of these texts encodes political claims about audience and authority.

**Texts and their narrative politics:**

| Text | Narrative form | Politics |
|---|---|---|
| Kite Runner | Amir (Pashtun, US-emigrated) tells Hassan's (Hazara) story | Margin's story told by centre; Western audience |
| Slumdog Millionaire | Western director films Indian story | The West's gaze on India |
| White Tiger | Balram's letters to Wen Jiabao | Sideways global addressee — the rising power |
| Reluctant Fundamentalist | Changez to American across a table | Surveillance as the form of self-narration |
| Khamosh Pani | Partial narration; silence as the dominant form | Refuses the testimony-confession structure |
| Funny Boy | First-person retrospective by Arjie | The marginal speaks — but in literary English (which is also a politics) |
| Manto | Third-person but centered on the marginal | Inversion of who is granted interiority |

**The deeper argument:** The question "who tells the story" is itself the **political question** of South Asian literature. Each text's choice of narrator, addressee, and language is a positioning vis-à-vis power — Western reception, national authority, religious-ethnic norms, gendered silences.

**Counter-move:** This self-consciousness can become its own trap — texts can become so concerned with the politics of narration that they fail to do anything else. The strongest texts in the course (Manto, Khamosh Pani) carry the politics implicitly through form, rather than thematizing it.

---

## "South Asia as a Region" Argument

If the prompt asks about South Asia as a region (likely):

**Spine thesis:** South Asia is a region defined not by uniformity but by **shared structures of violence, hybridity, and resistance**. The texts in this course make South Asia visible not as a coherent identity but as a **set of overlapping problems** — partition's afterlife, gender as infrastructure, the politics of representation, the postcolonial condition — that emerge in different national inflections.

**Common features across the texts:**
1. **Colonial inheritance:** All these countries are post-colonial; cartography (borders), institutions, languages of literature inherit British structures
2. **Religious nationalism:** Each country's politics is mediated by religious-ethnic identity (Hindu/Muslim, Sinhala/Tamil, Sunni/Shia, Buddhist/non-Buddhist)
3. **Gender as political infrastructure:** Across all texts, gender mediates national belonging
4. **Diaspora and global circulation:** Each text exists in transnational circulation — written for or shaped by global audiences
5. **The marginal as the truth:** The South Asian literary canon studied here consistently centers margins to expose the violence of centres

**Deploy:** "South Asia is a region not by virtue of cultural sameness but by virtue of **shared structural problems** that take national forms. The course's value is to make these structural commonalities legible across the apparent differences of nation, language, and genre."

# HOUR 7 — Afghanistan: Osama (Priority #5)

**Goal:** Two analytical units — Osama (this hour), Kite Runner (next). Osama anchors the gender-violence argument.

## Osama (2003, dir. Siddiq Barmak) — Plot in 60 seconds

A 12-year-old girl in Taliban-era Afghanistan is dressed as a boy by her widowed mother and grandmother because women cannot leave the house unaccompanied by a man, and the family has no income. She works at a tea shop, attends a madrassa, struggles to maintain the disguise. Her gender is discovered. She is taken into Taliban custody, "married" to an old mullah, and locked in his home. The film ends with no rescue.

---

## The 5 analytical moves

### Move 1: Gender as Erasure of Public Space

**The argument:** Under the Taliban, the public sphere is **gender-coded**. Women cannot work, study, or move freely without a male guardian (mahram). The disguise plot is forced by this gendering — the family cannot survive economically because the public sphere has been closed to women.

The film makes visible something usually invisible: that the public sphere is *not* gender-neutral; under Taliban rule, the gendering of public space is total and explicit.

**Specific scene:** The opening protest of women demanding work — the camera shows them being dispersed by Taliban guns. This is the film's premise: women's removal from public life is enforced by violence, not custom.

**Deploy:** "Osama makes visible the **gendering of public space** — the material consequences of relegating women to a 'private' sphere when public life is the site of survival. The disguise is not a personal choice but a structural necessity imposed by the gendered geography of Taliban rule."

### Move 2: Surveillance and the Disciplinary Gaze

**The argument:** The film is saturated with the Taliban gaze. Watching, inspecting, disciplining. Osama (as a girl in disguise) lives under constant threat of being seen.

**This is Foucauldian:** the watched internalizes the watcher. Osama's body posture, her walking, her response to being addressed — all are shaped by the awareness that she is being inspected. **The Taliban gaze produces a particular kind of subject** — one for whom every gesture is a calculation.

**Specific scene:** The bathing scene at the madrassa — Osama avoids it, knowing exposure is fatal. Her body becomes a problem because of the gaze it lives under.

**Deploy:** "The film stages **disciplinary surveillance** in its purest form: the Taliban gaze is not occasional but constant, producing a subject (Osama) whose every gesture must anticipate its scrutiny. The watched internalize the watcher — gender becomes performance because performance is the only available survival."

### Move 3: Erasure of the Public/Private Binary

**The argument:** Liberal political theory rests on a public/private distinction — the home is the realm of intimacy, freedom from state intrusion. Osama dismantles this. There is **no private space** for women under the Taliban.

- The home is invaded (Taliban search the women's houses)
- The woman cannot stay in the "private" because she has no income
- The mullah's home — at the film's end — is the private as prison
- The madrassa, the tea shop, the courtroom — all are extensions of state power

**The political insight:** The public/private binary that liberal political theory takes for granted is itself a privilege of certain political regimes. Under others, the binary collapses, and women are visible everywhere — and protected nowhere.

**Deploy:** "Osama erases the **public/private binary** that liberal political theory presumes. There is no domestic refuge from the state because the state has colonized the domestic. Women are visible everywhere — to be inspected, married, punished — and protected nowhere."

### Move 4: Agency Within Constraint

**The argument:** Osama is not a passive victim. She actively strategizes — adopting the disguise, navigating the madrassa, maintaining the deception. The film insists on her **agency under structural constraint**.

This is important because:
- It refuses the "Muslim woman as victim" trope that Western media often deploys
- It locates agency not in liberation (she does not escape) but in survival
- It treats her decisions as decisions, not as the absence of decisions

**The crucial point:** Agency is not liberation. The film does not free her at the end. Her agency is in the struggle itself, in the strategic intelligence she brings to her impossible situation. **This is a more demanding theory of agency** than the liberal one.

**Deploy:** "The film stages **agency under constraint** — neither the heroic liberation of the liberal frame nor the passive victimhood of the Orientalist gaze. Osama's agency is in her strategic intelligence, exercised within structural impossibility. Liberation does not come; the film refuses that consolation."

### Move 5: Place the Narrative in the Socio-political History

**The argument:** Osama was the first Afghan film made after the fall of the Taliban (2001). The film is itself an act of recovery — it could not have been made under the Taliban. Its existence registers a political moment.

But the film does not produce triumphant post-Taliban liberation imagery. Its final shot — Osama trapped in the mullah's house — refuses the "and now Afghanistan is free" narrative. The film insists that the Taliban's gender regime is not just a historical episode; its effects persist.

**Connect to:** The Kite Runner (next hour), which the course pairs with Osama for Afghanistan.

**Deploy:** "Osama exists as **a political artifact** — the first post-Taliban Afghan film. But its refusal of triumphant closure (the ending traps Osama, not frees her) registers a sober understanding: the Taliban regime is not just an event but a structure whose effects persist beyond the regime's fall."

# HOUR 8 — Afghanistan: The Kite Runner

**Goal:** The Kite Runner is the most important text for the **politics of representation** argument. Master the framework here; you will use it across the course.

---

## Plot in 90 seconds

Amir, a Pashtun boy in 1970s Kabul, is best friends with Hassan, a Hazara boy who is also his family's servant. After winning a kite-fighting tournament, Amir witnesses Hassan being raped by Assef and does nothing. Wracked by guilt, Amir frames Hassan for theft, getting Hassan and his father expelled from the household. Amir and his father flee to America during the Soviet invasion. Decades later, Amir returns to Taliban-era Afghanistan to rescue Hassan's son Sohrab — only after learning Hassan was his half-brother. The novel ends with Amir's redemption in Sohrab's eventual smile.

---

## The 5 analytical moves

### Move 1: Who Tells the Story — Margin–Centre at the Narrative Level

**The argument:** Amir is the narrator. Amir is **Pashtun, literate, wealthy, US-emigrated**. Hassan is Hazara, illiterate, servant, killed in Kabul. The novel is the story of Amir's guilt about Hassan — but Hassan never speaks for himself. **The centre tells the margin's story, claiming authority to do so.**

This is the novel's **central political problem**. Even as it foregrounds Hazara oppression (a real and underrepresented topic), it does so by occupying the position of the dominant Pashtun who speaks *for* the Hazara.

**Deploy:** "The novel's politics turn on its narrative form: Amir, the Pashtun, narrates Hassan, the Hazara. Hazara experience enters literature, but only through the Pashtun's mouth. The novel reproduces the **margin–centre** logic at the level of who is granted narrative authority — even while critiquing that logic at the level of plot."

### Move 2: Constructing Afghanistan for Western Audiences ⭐

**The argument:** The Kite Runner became a global bestseller, especially in the US. Why? Because it made Afghanistan **legible to American readers in a familiar emotional grammar**: guilt, redemption, father-son, brotherhood, atonement. It is, in Hosseini's own phrase, "a novel about friendship and betrayal that happens to be set in Afghanistan."

**The cost of this legibility:**
- Afghanistan becomes a backdrop for a universal moral drama
- Political complexity (the Soviet invasion, the US arming the mujahideen, the rise of the Taliban as a US-supported phenomenon, Pashtun-Hazara structural inequality) is flattened
- American readers can feel they "understand" Afghanistan without confronting US complicity

**The course's framing — "Afghanistan Meets the Amazon":**
This phrase (the topic from your course review) names the problem: when Afghanistan enters Amazon-bestseller-list circulation, what gets to count as Afghanistan? The novel's success is itself a politics — what *kind* of representation gets globally circulated?

**Deploy:** "The novel's global success is inseparable from its **politics of representation**: it succeeds with American audiences because it packages Afghanistan in a redemption-narrative framework legible to Western emotional grammar. The cost is that Afghan political complexity — particularly US complicity in the conditions it depicts — is rendered invisible."

### Move 3: Universal vs Local — The Translation Problem

**The argument:** The novel's defenders say: "It tells a universal human story about guilt and redemption, just happens to be set in Afghanistan."

The critique: there is no such thing as a "universal" story that "happens to be" set somewhere. The setting *is* the story. By extracting "universal themes" from Afghan specificity, the novel does ideological work — it makes Afghanistan legible by deleting what cannot be universalized (Hazara-Pashtun politics, Soviet/US imperial dynamics, Islam as more than backdrop).

**The deeper point:** "Universal" usually means "Western." A novel that has to translate its setting to Western emotional categories is not universal — it is provincial in a particular way (provincial to the West).

**Deploy:** "The novel deploys the **universal/local** distinction in a way that conceals its politics. The 'universal' redemption story the novel claims to tell is itself culturally specific — to a Western Christian-Protestant grammar of guilt and atonement. To call this universal is to mistake the dominant for the human."

### Move 4: The Rape Scene — Class, Gender, Ethnic Violence Compressed

**The argument:** The scene where Hassan is raped by Assef is the novel's pivotal moment. It is also a compressed image of **multiple vectors of violence**:
- **Ethnic:** Assef is Pashtun supremacist; he hates Hazaras and uses sexual violence as ethnic violence
- **Class:** Hassan is the servant; his body is already understood as available
- **Gender:** Sexual violence, even between men, deploys gender's logic — feminizing the victim, asserting masculine dominance
- **Political:** The scene foreshadows the Taliban's later violence — Assef returns as a Talib

Amir's failure to intervene is the novel's moral crux — but the scene matters beyond Amir's guilt. **It compresses the structure of Afghan violence into a single moment.**

**Deploy:** "The rape scene is the novel's structural compression — ethnic supremacy, class hierarchy, gendered violence, and political prefiguration converge in a single moment. Amir's failure is the personal axis; the scene's larger work is to make the *structure* of Afghan violence visible at the level of the body."

### Move 5: Diaspora and Return — California to Kabul

**The argument:** The second half of the novel is structured as the diasporic return: Amir, Americanized, goes back to Taliban-era Kabul to rescue Sohrab. This return is staged as **redemption**, but the politics are more troubled.

- The diasporic Amir has the resources (passport, money, English) to enter and exit Afghanistan in a way Hassan never could
- His "rescue" of Sohrab uses American institutions (the embassy, adoption) that themselves are part of the imperial structure he is supposedly transcending
- The novel ends in California with Sohrab — Afghanistan saved by being extracted to the West

**The deeper question:** Is the novel's redemption arc a critique of American complicity, or its celebration? The diasporic Amir is positioned as the savior, and his American resources are the means of salvation. **The novel may be more comfortable with American power than its critics often acknowledge.**

**Deploy:** "The diasporic return resolves the novel's moral plot but raises a political question it does not answer: Amir's redemption requires the very American institutional power (passport, embassy, adoption) that is part of the imperial structure that produced the conditions he rescues Sohrab from. The redemption arc may be more entangled with American power than the novel's surface claims."

# HOUR 9 — Sri Lanka: Funny Boy (Priority #6)

**Goal:** Master the intersectionality argument and the "intimate geopolitics" framework.

## Plot in 90 seconds

Funny Boy (1994, by Shyam Selvadurai) is a coming-of-age novel set in Sri Lanka in the late 1970s and early 1980s, ending with the **Black July 1983 anti-Tamil pogroms**. Arjie, a Tamil boy from a Colombo middle-class family, gradually realizes he is gay. The novel is told in six linked stories. The political backdrop — the rising Sinhala-Tamil ethnic conflict — increasingly intrudes on the family's life until the final story, "Riot Journal: An Epilogue," when the violence destroys their home and they flee to Canada.

---

## The 5 analytical moves

### Move 1: Double Exclusion (Intersectionality)

**The argument:** Arjie occupies two minority positions simultaneously:
- **Ethnic:** Tamil in a Sinhala-majority Sri Lanka
- **Sexual:** Queer in a heteronormative culture

**The double exclusion is not additive — it is interactive.** Each marginalization shapes the other:
- The family's Tamil minority status makes them vulnerable; this vulnerability *intensifies* their need for Arjie to be conventionally male (a strong patriarchal son)
- His queerness is read as a betrayal of family integrity at the very moment the family is under ethnic siege
- The categories that mark him (Tamil/queer) are the categories the dominant culture uses to mark people for exclusion

**This is intersectionality before the term was popularized in South Asian literary studies.** Selvadurai shows that minority identity does not work as a sum of separate axes; the axes constitute each other.

**Deploy:** "The novel stages **double exclusion** as interactive, not additive. Arjie's queerness and Tamilness are not separate marginalizations he sums up — they constitute each other. The family's ethnic vulnerability *intensifies* its policing of his gender; his queerness *deepens* his estrangement from the only refuge minority status would otherwise offer."

### Move 2: Intimate Geopolitics ⭐

**The argument:** The novel's signal contribution is the concept of **intimate geopolitics** — the demonstration that macro-political conflict (ethnic war, communal violence) is reproduced and lived through intimate spaces: the family, sexuality, childhood, the home.

**The political enters the intimate at every scale:**
- Arjie's "bride-bride" play (childhood gender bending) is policed by the family because of their political vulnerability
- The marriage of Anula (Sinhalese) and Daryl (Tamil journalist) becomes the family's political crisis
- Radha Aunty's Tamil-Sinhala romance is destroyed by communal violence
- The school is the site where Tamil students are humiliated by Sinhalese teachers
- The home is finally destroyed — the political ends the intimate

**This refuses the public/private split** that liberal political theory takes for granted. There is no private space free of politics in Sri Lanka; the political is always already intimate.

**Deploy:** "Selvadurai's central contribution is the concept of **intimate geopolitics** — the demonstration that ethnic conflict is not a macro-event that occasionally intrudes on private life but a structure that constitutes private life. Family, sexuality, childhood, and home are the *primary* sites of geopolitics, not its peripheral effects."

### Move 3: Everyday Reproduction of Conflict

**The argument:** The 1983 riots are the novel's climax, but Selvadurai shows ethnic conflict as **maintained daily** through small exclusions, neighborhood tensions, school dynamics, language requirements. The riot is not a rupture; it is the intensification of what was already structural.

**Specific sites of daily conflict:**
- School: Sinhalese teachers humiliating Tamil students; Sinhalese as compulsory medium
- Workplace: Tamil professionals hitting glass ceilings
- Neighborhood: Sinhala neighbors who become attackers in 1983 were neighbors before
- Marriage: cross-ethnic relationships policed by both communities

**This refuses the "explosion of violence" narrative** that treats ethnic conflict as anomalous. Selvadurai insists: 1983 is what 1978 was preparing for. Violence is everyday.

**Deploy:** "The novel refuses the spectacle of violent rupture by showing **everyday reproduction of conflict**. The 1983 riots do not break with daily life — they intensify what daily life had been. The everyday is not the absence of violence but its low-grade form."

### Move 4: Bildungsroman Inverted

**The argument:** Funny Boy is structurally a coming-of-age novel (bildungsroman) — Arjie grows into self-knowledge across six linked stories. But the form is **inverted**: traditionally, the bildungsroman moves from naivety to social integration. Funny Boy moves from naivety to **forced exile**. Arjie comes of age into a world that has no place for him.

The bildungsroman traditionally serves the project of national integration (the protagonist learns to be a citizen). Funny Boy uses the form against itself — Arjie cannot become a Sri Lankan citizen because Sri Lanka has rendered him impossible.

**Deploy:** "Selvadurai inverts the **bildungsroman**: traditionally a form of social integration, here it becomes a form of **disintegration**. Arjie comes of age into exile, not into citizenship. The form's classical function — producing the national subject — is refused; the protagonist is produced as exile, not as citizen."

### Move 5: Minority Discourse and Canon

**The argument:** Funny Boy is itself an act of **minority literary intervention** — a Tamil-Sri Lankan text written in English, by a diasporic author (Selvadurai lives in Canada). Its existence is its argument: that minority voices can constitute literature, even when their nation denies them political recognition.

But this is also fraught:
- Selvadurai writes in English, accessing global literary circulation that Tamil-language Sri Lankan writers rarely reach
- The diasporic writer can speak with authority about a homeland to which return is impossible
- The book becomes Sri Lankan literature *in English* for the global market — a particular form of representation

**Compare:** Kite Runner, similarly written by a diasporic author for global audiences. Both books raise the question: who writes minority literature, in what language, for whom?

**Deploy:** "The novel's existence enacts a politics of **canon formation**: it demands recognition of Tamil-queer experience as literature, but does so through the diasporic-English form that grants global legibility. The novel's representational politics are inseparable from its conditions of production and circulation."

## Bangladesh — Texts

The course covers Bangladesh through:

- *The True Cost* (2015, dir. Andrew Morgan) — documentary on global fast fashion
- Taslima Nasrin's *Lajja (Shame)* — novel on Hindu minority persecution in Bangladesh

These are connected by the question: **at what cost does Bangladesh's modernity arrive?**

---

## Bangladesh — 5 analytical moves

### Move 1: Rana Plaza and Global Supply Chains

**The argument:** On 24 April 2013, the Rana Plaza building in Savar, Bangladesh, collapsed. 1,134 garment workers were killed; 2,500 injured. The building housed factories making clothes for Walmart, Primark, Mango, Benetton, JC Penney, and other Western brands.

**This event is the analytical key for Bangladesh in the course.** It materially shows:
- Global supply chains: a t-shirt traveling from Bangladeshi garment worker to American consumer
- Hidden infrastructure: the consumer rarely thinks about the factory
- Body cost: the workers' bodies bear the cost of cheap clothing
- Brand obfuscation: Western brands deflected responsibility ("we didn't know our suppliers used this building")

**Deploy:** "Rana Plaza materializes **global capital's supply chain**: the Bangladeshi worker's body is the hidden infrastructure of Western fashion consumption. The Machinists and True Cost make this infrastructure visible — they refuse the consumer-end abstraction that allows the cost to remain invisible."

### Move 2: North-South Inequality

**The argument:** Bangladesh produces clothes; the Global North captures the value. The "Bangladesh discount" — labour priced low enough to compete globally — is structural to global fashion's profitability.

This is not "underdevelopment." It is **integration into global capital on disadvantageous terms**. Bangladesh is not outside the global economy; it is positioned within it as the labour-intensive periphery.

**The "race to the bottom":** Brands shift production wherever labour is cheapest and least regulated. Bangladesh's garment industry depends on this — but the dependency means improving labour conditions risks losing contracts.

**Deploy:** "The garment industry materializes **North-South inequality** not as Bangladesh's exclusion from globalization but as its specific *integration* — as the disposable-labour periphery on which the global fashion industry depends."

### Move 3: Critique of Technocratic Solutions

**The argument:** After Rana Plaza, the response was largely **technocratic**: the Accord on Fire and Building Safety, the Alliance for Bangladesh Worker Safety, brand audits, certification schemes.

**True Cost and The Machinists are skeptical of these solutions.** The argument:
- Audits and certifications protect brands' reputations, not workers' lives
- "Ethical sourcing" allows the consumer to feel good without changing the supply chain
- Self-regulation by industry has structural incentive to fail
- The technocratic frame depoliticizes — it treats systemic exploitation as a problem of better paperwork

**Deploy:** "The films perform a **critique of technocratic solutions**: post-Rana Plaza certifications and audits depoliticize what is a structural problem of global capital, providing brands with reputational cover while leaving the underlying labour relation intact."

### Move 4: Dignity of Labour vs Exploitation

**The argument:** The films do NOT depict Bangladeshi workers as passive victims. They show:
- Workers organizing (unions, strikes, despite repression)
- Workers' pride in skill and craft
- Workers' demand for dignity, not charity

**This is important.** The Western "ethical consumer" frame often constructs the Bangladeshi worker as a victim to be rescued. The films refuse this — workers are political agents demanding their rights.

**Compare:** Adiga's Balram is also constructed as agent rather than victim. Both texts insist that the Global South labourer is not the consumer's project.

**Deploy:** "The films refuse the **victim-rescue grammar** of Western ethical consumption. Bangladeshi workers appear as political agents — organizing, striking, demanding dignity — not as objects of charity. This refusal is itself a politics of representation."

### Move 5: Bangladesh as Emerging Tiger / Development Paradox

**The argument:** Bangladesh is one of the fastest-growing economies in South Asia ("Bangladesh tiger"). GDP is rising; poverty rates are falling; the garment industry has lifted millions out of extreme poverty.

But the development comes through:
- Female labour (90%+ of garment workers are women) under hazardous conditions
- Environmental cost (water pollution, garment waste)
- Persistent low wages
- Repression of labour organizing

**The paradox:** Bangladesh's development is real AND extractive. The same labour force whose conditions appall Western consumers is also the labour force whose income is changing rural-urban demographics, female literacy, marriage age. The texts hold both.

**Deploy:** "Bangladesh registers a **development paradox**: the garment industry is simultaneously the engine of unprecedented economic mobility and the site of severe labour exploitation. The texts refuse to resolve this — both descriptions are true, and the political question is how to demand the gains without the costs."

## Lajja (Taslima Nasrin) — 3 analytical moves

### Setting and plot in 60 seconds
Set in 1992, after the demolition of the Babri Masjid in India (December 1992). The Dutta family (Hindu, in Bangladesh) faces escalating violence from Bangladeshi Muslims angered by what is happening to Muslims in India. The novel chronicles their persecution. Nasrin herself was forced into exile after the book was banned in Bangladesh; a fatwa was issued against her.

### Move 1: Minority of a Minority

**The argument:** Bangladeshi Hindus are a minority in a Muslim-majority country (and that country is itself a Muslim minority country). When violence in India targets Indian Muslims, Bangladeshi Hindus become the local target of revenge — they are, literally, the minority of a minority. Religious violence is **transnational** in its logic — events in one country trigger violence against minorities in another.

**Deploy:** "Lajja exposes the **transnational logic of religious nationalism**: the Babri Masjid demolition in India produces violence against Hindus in Bangladesh. Religious-nationalist violence is not contained by national borders; it operates as a regional system."

### Move 2: Censorship and the Author's Body

**The argument:** Lajja was banned in Bangladesh. Nasrin received a fatwa. She has lived in exile since 1994. The book's reception itself becomes part of its meaning.

**This connects to Manto.** Both writers' bodies become political through state/community censorship. Censorship is not just literary repression; it produces particular kinds of authors — exiles, refugees, internationalized.

**Deploy:** "The reception of Lajja — its banning, the fatwa against Nasrin, her exile — connects to Manto's obscenity trials as instances of **productive censorship**: the state's attempt to suppress these texts reveals the very thing the state cannot tolerate (Hindu suffering, female sexuality), and produces the writer as international political figure."

### Move 3: Communal Violence as Continued Partition

**The argument:** Lajja's 1992 setting echoes Khamosh Pani's 1979. Both show how communal violence is **partition's afterlife** — recurring whenever religious nationalism rises. Lajja makes this explicit: the 1992 violence in Bangladesh follows the same logic as 1947 partition.

**Deploy:** "Lajja extends Khamosh Pani's argument: religious-communal violence is **partition continued**, not partition repeated. The 1992 violence in Bangladesh follows the same structural logic as 1947 — religious-nationalist mobilization, gendered violence, displacement. There is one structure with multiple inflections."

## Nepal — "Hands" — 4 analytical moves

The course covers Nepal through a story called "Hands" (limited material in your review document — this is the topic noted in your course review). Given limited textual detail, focus on the **structural arguments**.

### Move 1: Dream Narrative as Form

**Deploy:** "The dream-narrative form of 'Hands' refuses the realist national-allegorical mode that dominates much South Asian literature. This formal choice reflects Nepal's structural marginality — Nepal does not narrate itself in the realist forms claimed by larger national literatures."

### Move 2: Hands as Symbol — Labour, Heritage, Connection

**The argument:** Hands signify:

- Connection (touch, family, intergenerational continuity)

**Deploy:** "The motif of hands compresses **labour, heritage, and connection**: the hands that work, the hands that pass skill across generations, the hands that touch. Their loss or transformation under modernity is the story's political analysis."

### Move 3: Intergenerational Conflict as Political Allegory

**The argument:** The story stages intergenerational conflict — parents' traditional values vs children's modernizing aspirations. This is a familiar trope, but its political meaning in Nepal is specific:

Nepal in the late 20th century was caught between traditional monarchy, Maoist insurgency, and democratization. The intergenerational conflict in literary form **allegorizes** this national struggle — the parents are the old order; the children are the modernizing/revolutionary present.

**Deploy:** "Intergenerational conflict in 'Hands' functions as **political allegory**: the family's generational struggle stages Nepal's national struggle between traditional, monarchical, and modernizing/revolutionary forces."

### Move 4: Canon Formation and Nepal's Marginality

**The argument:** Nepal is structurally marginal in South Asian studies. India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Sri Lanka dominate the canon. Bhutan and Nepal are afterthoughts.

**The story's existence in this course is itself a political act** — it asserts Nepal as worth canonical attention. The course's gesture mirrors Selvadurai's: bringing the marginal into the canon is itself representational politics.

**Connect to:** Funny Boy (canon formation through diasporic English literature); the broader question of who counts as "South Asian literature."

**Deploy:** "The inclusion of Nepalese literature in this course performs **canon formation**: Nepal's structural marginality in South Asian studies is registered, and the marginality is itself the story's analytical content. The story stages canon politics in its very existence as classroom text."

# HOUR 11 — Bhutan: The Circle of Karma (Priority #8)

**Goal:** Efficient coverage. Bhutan is unlikely to anchor more than one question; nail 4-5 deployable arguments.

## Plot in 90 seconds

The Circle of Karma (2005) by Kunzang Choden — the first English-language novel by a Bhutanese woman. Tsomo, a Bhutanese woman from a traditional family, leaves home after losing a child and her husband. The novel follows her wandering — through Bhutan, into India, on pilgrimage, into multiple relationships — across decades. Built around her experiences of suffering, religious devotion, and reflection.

---

## The 5 analytical moves

### Move 1: Memory-Based Narrative Structure

**The argument:** The novel is structured by memory, not chronology. Tsomo's life unfolds through her remembering — events return, get reframed, reverberate. The form *enacts* the karmic logic: events are connected by causation across time, not arranged in sequence.

**Form mirrors content:** Karma is a logic of cause-and-consequence across time (and lives). The novel's memory-structure literally renders this — events repeat, transform, return.

**Deploy:** "The novel's **memory-based narrative structure** formally enacts the karmic worldview it depicts. Karma is a logic of recurrence and consequence across time; the form of the novel — events returning through memory, reverberating across decades — is karma's literary embodiment."

### Move 2: Body as Site of Labour and Suffering

**The argument:** Tsomo's body bears the cost of her life — physical labour, miscarriage, illness, aging. Bhutanese spirituality (Buddhism) does not transcend the body; it locates the spiritual in bodily experience.

**This refuses the West's mind-body split.** In Tsomo's world, suffering happens *in* the body, and meaning is made *through* it. The pilgrimage is corporeal; karma works through the body.

**Deploy:** "The novel locates **spirituality in corporeality**: Tsomo's body is not transcended by her religious life but is the very site at which karma operates. This refuses the Western mind-body division — meaning is bodily, suffering is sacred, the spiritual cannot be extracted from the physical."

### Move 3: Karma as Epistemology, Not Just Ethics ⭐

**The argument:** Western readers often understand karma as a moral principle ("you reap what you sow"). The novel expands this:

**Karma is a way of knowing.** It is an epistemology — a system for organizing experience and producing meaning. Events are *meaningful* because they are caused. Nothing is random. This is not just ethics; it is the cognitive framework through which Tsomo *understands her life*.

When suffering happens, she does not ask "why me?" (the Western question that presumes randomness). She asks "what does this teach me about my path?" (the karmic question that presumes meaningful causation).

**Deploy:** "Karma in the novel functions as **epistemology, not just ethics**: it is the cognitive framework through which Tsomo organizes her experience. Suffering is not random; it is meaningful because caused. This represents a fundamentally different mode of meaning-making from the Western framework that presumes randomness and the question 'why me?'"

### Move 4: Productive AND Limiting Functions of Karma

**The argument:** Karma is *productive* — it gives suffering meaning, dignity, and trajectory. Tsomo's life has shape because karma provides a framework.

But karma can also be *limiting*:
- It can rationalize injustice ("she suffers because of past-life karma" can silence critique of present exploitation)
- It can foreclose political action (if everything is meaningful, structural change may seem irrelevant)
- It can be deployed by the powerful to keep the suffering passive

**The novel holds both.** It doesn't celebrate karma uncritically; it shows karma's two-sided operation.

**Deploy:** "The novel does not celebrate karma uncritically — it holds the framework's **double face**. Karma's productive function (giving suffering meaning) and its limiting function (rationalizing injustice) coexist. The novel's analytical maturity is in refusing to choose between celebration and critique."

### Move 5: Bhutan's Cultural Specificity in South Asia

**The argument:** Bhutan is **distinct** in the South Asian region:
- Buddhist (not Hindu, Muslim, or Christian dominant)
- Never colonized in the same way (no British rule)
- Late democratization (constitutional monarchy until 2008)
- Distinctive ideological position: GNH (Gross National Happiness) as alternative to GDP

The novel registers this specificity. It does not narrate Bhutanese identity through partition, colonial trauma, or globalization — the structures that dominate other South Asian texts. It narrates through karma, pilgrimage, and Buddhist ethics.

**This is important for the "South Asia as region" argument.** Bhutan's inclusion shows that the region cannot be defined by any single shared experience. South Asia is heterogeneous; the unity is structural (postcolonial, religious-national, etc.) and partial.

**Deploy:** "The novel registers Bhutan's **cultural specificity** in the South Asian region — Buddhist worldview, late democratization, distinctive non-colonial trajectory. Including Bhutan in the South Asian frame complicates any essentialist regional definition: South Asia's coherence is partial and structural, not cultural."

## Essay Templates (use these structures)

### Template 1: Single-Text Analysis with a Theoretical Lens

**Prompt form:** "Analyze [text] using [framework]" OR "How does [text] represent [theme]?"

**Structure (45-60 min, 1500-2000 words):**

1. **Opening (150 words):**
   - Frame the prompt's terms; refuse any static or naive framing
   - State your thesis using the primary framework
   - Preview your three moves

2. **Move 1: The framework's first operation in the text (350 words):**
   - Specific scene/character as evidence
   - Show *how* the framework operates (not just that it does)

3. **Move 2: A second operation, deepening or complicating (350 words):**
   - Different scene/character
   - This is where you show analytical range

4. **Move 3: A counter-move or complication (300 words):**
   - The framework's limit, ambiguity, or cost
   - This is what separates a good essay from a great one — willingness to complicate

5. **Conclusion (200 words):**
   - What does this analysis reveal about the larger course question (South Asia as region, power, representation)?
   - Don't summarize; connect outward

### Template 2: Comparative Analysis

**Prompt form:** "Compare two texts on the theme of [X]"

**Structure (60 min, 2000 words):**

1. **Opening (200 words):**
   - Define the theme analytically (not descriptively)
   - State the comparative thesis: similar in [way] but differ structurally in [way]
   - Preview both texts

2. **Text 1 analysis (500 words):**
   - 2-3 specific moments
   - Theoretical frame for how this text approaches the theme

3. **Text 2 analysis (500 words):**
   - 2-3 specific moments
   - Theoretical frame for how *this* text approaches the theme — different from Text 1

4. **The comparative move (500 words):**
   - This is the key — don't just list both texts. Argue: the difference between them reveals X about the theme
   - What does the comparison teach us that neither text alone could?

5. **Conclusion (200 words):**
   - Both texts together produce an argument about [theme] in the South Asian context
   - Connect to broader course frameworks

### Template 3: Meta-Question on Representation / South Asia as Region

**Prompt form:** "What does the course suggest about [South Asia as region / popular culture / representation]?"

**Structure (60-75 min, 2000-2500 words):**

1. **Opening (250 words):**
   - Frame the meta-question as itself a course concern (refuse to treat it naively)
   - Thesis: South Asia is unified by [structural feature], not by [essentialist feature]
   - Three texts you'll use as evidence

2. **Text 1 evidence (450 words):**
   - The structural feature operating in this text

3. **Text 2 evidence (450 words):**
   - The same structural feature, but in different national/textual form

4. **Text 3 evidence (450 words):**
   - The same structural feature, but a different inflection

5. **The structural argument (300 words):**
   - The three texts together demonstrate the structural feature
   - Refuse essentialist or static accounts

6. **Conclusion (250 words):**
   - The course's contribution: making structural commonalities legible across national differences

## Exam-Day Tactics

### Time management for 180 minutes (typical exam structure)

If 3 questions, 60 min each:
- 5 min: read the prompt; outline the essay (use templates above)
- 50 min: write
- 5 min: review for thesis clarity and one missing reference

If 2 questions, 90 min each:
- 10 min: outline
- 70 min: write
- 10 min: review

### What to bring (open book — bring everything that helps)

- This document (or your printed/handwritten notes from it)
- Your reference sheet (one page is enough)
- The handout
- Pens, water, watch
- Any annotated copies of texts

### What examiners reward

- **Specific evidence** — name the scene, the character, the line
- **Frameworks deployed deeply** — not name-dropped, but worked through
- **Counter-moves** — showing you can hold complications, not just one-sided arguments
- **Cross-text connections** — even in single-text questions, gesturing to the wider course
- **Analytical voice** — confident, not hedging; use the analytical phrases you've learned

### What to avoid

- **Plot summary** — every sentence should make an argument, not describe events
- **Listing frameworks** without deploying them
- **Writing about texts without evidence** — always cite a scene/moment
- **Abstract politics** without textual grounding
- **Five-paragraph high-school structure** — write for the analytical level the course expects
