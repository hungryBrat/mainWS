## Format-Aware Strategy (Open Book)

Since the exam is open book, **memorization is not the bottleneck — speed and analytical readiness are.**

The examiner tests:
- Can you build an argument quickly using the right theoretical lens?
- Can you cite specific textual evidence (scene, character, motif) to anchor your analysis?
- Can you compare across texts/countries with precision?

**Do not spend study time trying to memorize plot.** Spend it:
1. Internalizing the analytical frameworks so you can deploy them in under 2 minutes
2. Knowing *exactly which scene/detail* proves each argument for each text
3. Building a reference sheet you can open in the exam and scan in 30 seconds

**First deliverable of this plan:** a one-page reference sheet (Hour 1).

### Hour 2 | 1:00–2:00 | Afghanistan
**Texts:** Osama (2003 film) + The Kite Runner (novel + 2007 film)

**Primary goal:** Two arguments per text, ready to write in an exam.

**Osama — 3 analytical moves:**
1. **Gender as erasure of public space:** Under Taliban, Osama's disguise plot is driven by the complete removal of women from public life. The film makes the public/private binary collapse visible — there is no "safe" private space either (the madrassa).
2. **Power and surveillance:** The gaze in the film is always the Taliban gaze — watching, disciplining. Foucauldian: the watched internalize the watcher. Osama survives by performing gender under surveillance.
3. **Agency within constraint:** Osama is NOT a passive victim — she actively strategizes. The film asks where agency lives when structural freedom is absent.

**Kite Runner — 3 analytical moves:**
1. **Who tells the story:** Amir (Pashtun, literate, emigrated to US) tells the story of Afghanistan. Hassan (Hazara, illiterate, stays) is the marginalized one whose story is *told by* Amir, not *told by himself*. The centre speaks for the margin.
2. **Constructing South Asia for the West:** The novel succeeds globally because it packages Afghanistan in redemption-narrative form legible to American readers. "Afghanistan meets the Amazon" — it sells because it fits a Western emotional grammar.
3. **What stories gain acceptance:** Universal themes (guilt, redemption, father-son) make the local palatable globally. But what gets lost? Political complexity, complicity of the diaspora, the Hazara's own subjectivity.

**Reference sheet entry:** Afghanistan → *Osama*: gender erasure, surveillance, agency. *Kite Runner*: narrative legitimacy, margin-centre (Amir/Hassan), Western reception.

### Hour 3 | 2:00–3:00 | Pakistan — Manto
**Texts:** Manto short stories (especially partition-era)

**Primary goal:** Understand Manto's technique and what it exposes about power.

**4 analytical moves:**
1. **Partition as backdrop, not subject:** Manto never describes partition politically — he shows its human residue (the sex worker, the displaced man, the mad). The violence is off-stage; its effects are on the page.
2. **Art of character writing:** Manto centers the socially marginal — sex workers, the criminalized, the insane. This is a political act: the margin is given interiority and dignity at the moment the state was drawing new lines of belonging.
3. **Obscenity trials as productive censorship:** Manto was tried for obscenity. The trials reveal what the newly-formed Pakistani state found threatening: the sexuality and agency of women, the ambiguity of partition violence. What is censored tells you what power needs to suppress.
4. **Center-margin inversion:** The "respectable" characters in Manto (politicians, soldiers, landlords) are often morally bankrupt. The "disreputable" characters retain humanity. This is a sustained critique of who the nation-state centers.

**Key story to remember:** *Toba Tek Singh* — a Sikh in a Pakistani asylum who refuses to move to India; dies in the no-man's-land between borders. Metaphor for partition's absurdity and its cost to ordinary people.

**Reference sheet entry:** Manto → partition as human cost; marginal characters with dignity; obscenity = productive censorship; center-margin inversion.

### Hour 4 | 3:00–4:00 | Pakistan — Khamosh Pani + Reluctant Fundamentalist
**Texts:** Khamosh Pani (2003 film, dir. Sabiha Sumar) + The Reluctant Fundamentalist (2013 film)

**Khamosh Pani — 4 analytical moves:**
1. **Gendering the partition:** Ayesha converted during partition, buried her past. The film shows partition NOT as a historical event that ended but as a wound women carry in their bodies and silence.
2. **Partition as continued event:** The film is set in 1979 (Zia-ul-Haq's Islamization). Partition's violence resurfaces — the daughter's forced conversion in 1947 echoes in the son's radicalization in 1979. History doesn't close.
3. **Silence as stylistic device:** Ayesha's silence is both survival strategy and narrative technique. The film asks viewers to *read silence* — what is not said is the whole story.
4. **Urvashi Butalia connection:** Butalia's work (*The Other Side of Silence*) collected women's testimonies of partition that official history erased. Khamosh Pani does cinematically what Butalia does archivally — centers women's experience of partition.

**Reluctant Fundamentalist — 3 analytical moves:**
1. **Post-9/11 identity:** Changez (Pakistani, Princeton, Wall Street) is forced by geopolitical events to confront his hybridity. The West he bought into rejects him; he rejects it back.
2. **Pakistan in the Western gaze:** The film/novel uses a framing device — Changez tells his story to an American, who may be CIA. The very structure enacts the surveillance and suspicion South Asians face post-9/11.
3. **Hybrid identity and its collapse:** Changez's identity was built on performing Western success. When that performance fails under scrutiny, he must choose — and the novel's ambiguity about what he chooses is the point.

**Reference sheet entry:** Khamosh Pani → partition in women's bodies; silence as text; Butalia parallel. Reluctant Fundamentalist → post-9/11 hybridity; Pakistani under Western gaze.

### Hour 5 | 4:00–5:00 | Bangladesh + Nepal
**Texts:** The Machinists (2012), The True Cost (2015), Taslima Nasrin's Lajja; Nepal's "Hands"

**Bangladesh — 4 analytical moves:**
1. **Rana Plaza and global labour networks:** The 2013 garment factory collapse kills 1,100 workers making clothes for Western brands. The Machinists and True Cost make the global supply chain visible — the fashion consumer in London is connected to the body under rubble in Dhaka.
2. **North-South inequality:** Bangladesh produces the clothes; the North captures the value. The "Bangladesh discount" — cheap labour as development strategy — is critiqued as exploitation dressed as opportunity.
3. **Critique of technocratic solutions:** CSR initiatives, safety certificates, "ethical sourcing" — the films argue these patch the wound without addressing structural exploitation. Technocratic solutions protect the system.
4. **Bangladesh as emerging tiger:** Development paradox — economic growth (GDP, garment exports) coexists with labour hazard, gender exploitation (90% female garment workers), and environmental cost.

**Lajja (Taslima Nasrin):** Hindu minority persecution in Bangladesh after Babri Masjid demolition (1992). Minority within a minority (Hindu woman in Muslim-majority Bangladesh). Nasrin herself exiled — the book was banned.

**Nepal — "Hands" story:**
- Dream narrative structure: non-linear, symbolic — appropriate for Nepal's marginality in South Asian literary canon
- Intergenerational conflict: parents' traditional craft/values vs children's aspirations — political allegory for Nepal caught between traditional monarchy and modernization
- Symbolism of hands: labour, skill, connection to land and heritage; what is lost when hands stop doing the old work
- Nepal's marginality: even within South Asian studies, Nepal is an afterthought — the story itself enacts this marginality

**Reference sheet entry:** Bangladesh → global supply chain; labour exploitation; development paradox; Nasrin/Lajja = minority persecution. Nepal/"Hands" → dream narrative; intergenerational = political allegory; canon marginality.

### Hour 6 | 5:00–6:00 | Bhutan
**Text:** The Circle of Karma — Kunzang Choden

**Primary goal:** Efficient coverage — 4 lectures only, likely one exam question.

**4 analytical moves:**
1. **Memory-based narrative:** The novel is structured around Tsomo's memories and wanderings. Memory is not chronological — it follows the logic of karma (action-consequence loops). The form enacts the content.
2. **Body as site of labour and suffering:** Tsomo's body bears the cost of her spiritual and material journey. The body is not transcended by spirituality — it is the *place* where karma plays out.
3. **Karma as epistemology, not just ethics:** Karma is not just "you reap what you sow" — it is a complete *way of knowing* and *organizing* experience. Events are meaningful because they are caused; nothing is random. This frames how Tsomo processes suffering.
4. **Productive AND limiting functions of karma:** Karma provides dignity and meaning to suffering (productive). But it can also rationalize exploitation — "she suffers because of past-life karma" can silence critique of present injustice (limiting). The novel holds both.

**Bhutan in South Asia:** Hidden kingdom; GNH (Gross National Happiness) as alternative to GDP; Buddhist state; recent democratization; not a typical South Asian narrative.

**Reference sheet entry:** Circle of Karma → memory-form mirrors karma; body as karma's site; karma = epistemology; productive AND limiting functions.

### Hour 7 | 6:00–7:00 | Sri Lanka
**Text:** Funny Boy — Shyam Selvadurai

**5 analytical moves:**
1. **Double exclusion:** Arjie is Tamil (ethnic minority) in Sri Lanka AND queer. Each identity marginalizes him; together they produce a compound exclusion — the margin of the margin. Intersectionality before the term was widely used.
2. **Intimate geopolitics:** The macro-political (Sinhalese-Tamil conflict, 1983 riots, anti-Tamil violence) intrudes into the most intimate spaces — the family, sexuality, childhood. There is no refuge in the private. "Intimate geopolitics" — the political is always already personal.
3. **Everyday reproduction of conflict:** The riots are the climax, but Selvadurai shows how conflict is *maintained daily* through small exclusions, neighborhood tensions, school dynamics. Ethnic conflict is not a rupture — it is a structure of daily life.
4. **Minority discourse:** How do minorities navigate identity when the state defines belonging in ethnic/religious terms? Arjie cannot be fully Tamil or fully Sinhalese; cannot be "normally" gendered. He occupies a space the dominant categories cannot name.
5. **Coming-of-age under constraint:** The bildungsroman form is deployed ironically — Arjie grows into an understanding of the world that forecloses rather than opens futures. His coming-of-age is a coming-into-exile.

**Key scene:** The "bride-bride" play — Arjie's childhood gender-bending; the family's policing of it. Shows how gender normativity and ethnic belonging are enforced at the domestic scale.

**Reference sheet entry:** Funny Boy → double exclusion (Tamil + queer); intimate geopolitics; everyday conflict reproduction; minority discourse; bride-bride scene.

### Hour 8 | 7:00–8:00 | India — The White Tiger
**Text:** The White Tiger — Aravind Adiga

**6 analytical moves — this is the richest analytical unit:**
1. **Neoliberal promise vs reality:** The novel is set during India's IT-boom / "India shining" era. Balram's story tests the claim that meritocracy is now possible in India. Verdict: the system is rigged; his success requires murder, not merit.
2. **Epistolary structure:** Balram writes letters to the Chinese Premier. Why? Because Wen Jiabao is visiting India; Balram wants to tell him the *real* story of Indian entrepreneurship. The audience is foreign — the whole novel is explaining India to an outsider. This mirrors the Kite Runner problem: who is the story for?
3. **Light and darkness:** Villages = darkness (servitude, tradition, immobility). City (Delhi/Gurgaon) = light (freedom, modernity, possibility). But Balram's transgression — moving from darkness to light — requires violence. The spatial metaphor is both aspiration and trap.
4. **The Rooster Coop:** Why don't the poor rebel? Balram's answer: because their family is hostage. The master class controls not through force but through the servant's love for his family. The poor police themselves. This is Balram's most original insight — and it takes him 350 pages to break free of it.
5. **White Tiger as individual vs systemic critique:** Balram's success as the "white tiger" (one-in-a-generation exception) DOES NOT critique the coop — it proves the coop works. One escape does not change the structure. The novel's irony: Balram narrates his liberation using the exact logic of the system he escaped.
6. **Slumdog comparison:** Slumdog Millionaire (2008) also shows a poor Indian boy rising through exceptional circumstances. Comparison: Slumdog uses Bollywood-style wish fulfillment for Western consumption; White Tiger uses first-person irony for literary critique. Slumdog is poverty porn; White Tiger is poverty rage.

**Reference sheet entry:** White Tiger → neoliberal failure; epistolary (explaining India to the West); light/dark spatial; rooster coop = structural servitude; white tiger = individual exception proves system; compare Slumdog (aesthetics of poverty).

### Hour 9 | 8:00–9:00 | Comparative Synthesis — Cross-Cutting Themes
**No new texts — synthesis only.**

**Primary goal:** Build 4 comparative essay outlines. These are your most high-value exam weapons.

**Theme 1: Gender and Violence across South Asia**
- Osama: public erasure, surveillance, embodied risk
- Khamosh Pani: partition violence on women's bodies; silence as survival
- Circle of Karma: body as site of suffering; karma as explanation/rationalization
- Funny Boy: domestic gender policing as precursor to political violence
- Common argument: In South Asian literature, women's bodies are the site where political violence is made intimate and personal. Each text shows a different mechanism of this inscription.

**Theme 2: Partition and Its Afterlives**
- Manto: partition as human residue; the cost on marginalized people
- Khamosh Pani: partition as unfinished — resurfaces 30 years later
- Lajja: partition logic (religious violence, displacement) recurring in 1992 Babri aftermath
- Common argument: Partition is not a historical event that ended in 1947 — it is a structure of violence that recurs whenever religious nationalism rises. South Asian literature returns to it obsessively because it has not been resolved.

**Theme 3: Labour, Capital, and Exploitation**
- Bangladesh/True Cost: global supply chain; garment workers' bodies as the cost of fast fashion
- White Tiger: domestic labour; the servant class; rooster coop
- Slumdog: slum children as raw material for global entertainment industry
- Common argument: South Asian texts consistently expose how capitalism's promises (development, meritocracy, global integration) rest on the exploitation of specific bodies — gendered, classed, racialized.

**Theme 4: Who Tells the Story? (Narrative Politics)**
- Kite Runner: Amir tells Hassan's story; Western reception shapes what "Afghanistan" means
- Slumdog: Danny Boyle (British director) tells Mumbai's story; poverty aestheticized for global audience
- Reluctant Fundamentalist: Pakistani man tells his story TO an American — the frame enacts surveillance
- White Tiger: Balram tells India's story to a Chinese Premier — the addressee reveals the politics
- Common argument: The question of who narrates, for whom, and in what language is itself a political act in South Asian literature. "Constructing South Asia for the outside" is a recurring anxiety.

### Hour 10 | 9:00–10:00 | Essay Structure Practice
**No reading — writing practice only.**

Write essay outlines (not full essays) for 3 prompts. Aim for 10 minutes each, then review.

**Prompt 1:** "Choose one text and analyze how it represents the relationship between gender and political power."
- Pick: Osama OR Khamosh Pani (your stronger one)
- Structure: Thesis (gender IS the site where political power operates) → Evidence point 1 (specific scene) → Evidence point 2 (specific scene) → Theoretical frame (margin-centre / gendering South Asia) → Complication (where is agency?) → Conclusion (what does this say about South Asia?)

**Prompt 2:** "Compare two texts from different South Asian countries on the theme of minority identity."
- Pick: Funny Boy (Sri Lanka) + Manto (Pakistan)
- Structure: Frame (minority discourse in South Asia) → Text 1 analysis → Text 2 analysis → Comparison (what is similar? what is structurally different?) → Broader argument (what does South Asian literature DO with minority identity?)

**Prompt 3:** "What is the relationship between popular culture and politics in South Asian representation?"
- This is the meta-question of the whole course.
- Structure: Define "popular culture" as contested (not just entertainment — it is the site where ideology is produced and contested) → Example 1 (Kite Runner: popular globally, politically simplifying) → Example 2 (White Tiger: literary but mass-distributed) → Example 3 (True Cost: documentary as political intervention) → Conclusion: popular culture is never innocent — it always represents *someone's* South Asia.

## Things Examiners in This Course Almost Certainly Test

Based on syllabus structure, course description, and literary-studies norms for open-book comprehensives:

1. **Essay on one text with a theoretical lens** — "Analyze [text] using the margin-centre framework" or "How does [text] represent gender?"
2. **Comparative question** — "Compare two South Asian texts on the theme of [identity/violence/labour/partition]"
3. **Meta question on representation** — "What does popular culture do that literary fiction cannot, or vice versa?"
4. **South Asia as region question** — "What common threads connect the texts studied, despite their national differences?"

The most dangerous trap: writing plot summary instead of analysis. Every sentence in your exam answer should make an *argument*, not describe what happens.
