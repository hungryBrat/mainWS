# PoM Part 2 — Teams, Communication, Personality (extracted from slides)

## Teams (Ch 11 — Robbins & Coulter)

### Key Definitions
- **Group**: Two or more interacting and interdependent individuals who come together to achieve specific goals.
- **Formal group**: Work group defined by the organization's structure that has designated work assignments and tasks; appropriate behaviors are defined by and directed toward organizational goals.
- **Informal group**: Group independently formed to meet the social needs of its members (friendships, common interests).
- **Command group**: Determined by the org chart; individuals report directly to a given manager.
- **Task group**: Brought together to complete a specific job task; usually temporary, disbands when task completes.
- **Cross-functional team**: Brings together knowledge and skills of individuals from various work areas (or whose members have been trained to do each other's jobs).
- **Self-managed team**: Essentially independent group that, in addition to its tasks, takes on traditional managerial responsibilities (hiring, planning, scheduling, performance evaluations) — operates without a manager and is responsible for a complete work process or segment.
- **Work team**: A group whose members work intensely on a specific common goal using positive synergy, individual and mutual accountability, and complementary skills.
- **Role**: Set of expected behavior patterns attributed to someone occupying a given position in a social unit.
- **Role conflict**: Differing role expectations. **Role ambiguity**: Uncertainty about role expectations.
- **Norms**: Acceptable standards or expectations shared by group members.
- **Conformity**: Individuals adjusting to be accepted; group pressures shift judgment/attitudes.
- **Groupthink**: Extensive pressure of others in a strongly cohesive or threatened group that causes members to change opinions to conform to the group.
- **Status system**: Formal/informal prestige grading or ranking serving as recognition and motivator. Effective when perceived ranking and status symbols are congruent.
- **Social loafing**: Tendency for individuals to expend less effort working collectively than working individually.
- **Group cohesiveness**: Degree to which members are attracted to a group and share the group's goals.
- **Conflict**: Perceived incompatible differences in a group resulting in some form of interference or opposition to its assigned tasks.

### Frameworks / Theories — name, author, components verbatim, when applied

**1. Tuckman's 5 Stages of Group Development** — apply when scenario describes a newly formed/evolving team.
1. **Forming** — members join and begin defining the group's purpose, structure, and leadership.
2. **Storming** — intragroup conflict; individuals resist group control, disagree over leadership.
3. **Norming** — close relationships develop, group becomes cohesive, establishes norms for acceptable behavior.
4. **Performing** — fully functional structure; group focuses on performing the task at hand.
5. **Adjourning** — the group prepares to disband; no longer concerned with high levels of performance.

**2. Punctuated Equilibrium Model** — applies to temporary task groups working under deadline. Group sets direction in first meeting, enters period of inertia, undergoes a transition at roughly the midpoint of allotted time (a burst of activity / re-direction), then a second phase of inertia, then accelerated activity at deadline.

**3. Group Performance/Satisfaction Model (Exhibit 11-3)** — apply when a case asks "what determines this group's performance":
External Conditions Imposed on the Group → Group Member Resources + Group Structure → Group Processes → (with Group Tasks) → Performance and Satisfaction.

**4. Three Views of Conflict**
- **Traditional view**: conflict must be avoided.
- **Human relations view**: conflict is a natural and inevitable outcome in any group.
- **Interactionist view**: conflict can be a positive force and is absolutely necessary for effective group performance.

**5. Types of Conflict (Robbins)** — apply when classifying a workplace dispute:
- **Task conflict** — content and goals of the work — **Functional** at **low** level.
- **Relationship conflict** — interpersonal relationships — always **Dysfunctional**.
- **Process conflict** — how the work gets done — **Functional** at **low to moderate** level.

**6. Functional vs. Dysfunctional Conflict** — Functional = constructive (supports group goals, improves performance). Dysfunctional = destructive (hinders performance). Exhibit 11-7 inverted-U: Situation A (low/no conflict) → low performance, dysfunctional, apathetic/stagnant; Situation B (optimal level) → high performance, functional, viable/self-critical/innovative; Situation C (high) → low performance, dysfunctional, disruptive/chaotic/uncooperative.

**7. Thomas-Kilmann Conflict-Handling Styles (Exhibit 11-8)** — two axes: Assertiveness (assertive↔unassertive), Cooperativeness (cooperative↔uncooperative).
- **Forcing** — assertive + uncooperative — satisfying one's own needs at the expense of another's. Use when quick decisive action vital, unpopular actions need pushing.
- **Collaborating** — assertive + cooperative — seeking an advantageous solution for all parties (win-win). Use when both sets of concerns too important to compromise, integrate insights, build commitment.
- **Compromising** — middle on both — each party gives up something of value. Use when goals important but not worth disruption, time pressure, temporary settlement.
- **Avoiding** — unassertive + uncooperative — withdrawing from or suppressing conflict. Use when issue trivial, potential disruption outweighs benefits, need to cool down.
- **Accommodating** — unassertive + cooperative — placing another's needs and concerns above your own. Use when issue more important to other party, build social credit, harmony critical.

**8. Cohesiveness × Goal-Alignment Matrix (Exhibit 11-5)** — apply to predict productivity:
- High cohesiveness + High alignment → **Strong increase in productivity**.
- Low cohesiveness + High alignment → Moderate increase.
- High cohesiveness + Low alignment → **Decrease in productivity**.
- Low cohesiveness + Low alignment → No significant effect.

**9. Group Decision-Making Techniques (Exhibit 11-6)** — Brainstorming (idea-generating, withhold criticism); **Nominal Group Technique (NGT)** (members independently write ideas, each presents one until all presented, no discussion until all ideas presented); Electronic meetings (linked computers).

**10. Characteristics of Effective Teams (Exhibit 11-10)** — eight components, must list verbatim in case answer:
1. Clear Goals (clear understanding of goals)
2. Relevant Skills (competent members with technical and interpersonal skills)
3. Mutual Trust (in character and integrity of members)
4. Unified Commitment (to team goals)
5. Good Communication (systems)
6. Negotiating Skills (effective)
7. Appropriate Leadership
8. Internal & External Support (supportive environments)

### Lists to memorize

**Group Structure variables**: Roles · Norms · Conformity · Status System · Group Size · Cohesiveness.

**Common types of norms**: Effort and performance (output, absenteeism, promptness, socializing); Dress; Loyalty.

**External conditions imposed on group** (8): Organization's strategy · Authority relationships · Formal regulations · Available organizational resources · Employee selection criteria · Performance management (appraisal) system · Organizational culture · General physical layout of work space.

**Group Member Resources**: Knowledge · Skills (interpersonal: conflict mgmt, collaborative problem solving, communication) · Abilities · Personality traits.

**Group Size effects**: Small groups complete tasks faster, more effective use of facts. Large groups solve problems better, good for diverse input, more effective in fact-finding. Larger groups → more social loafing.

**Four types of teams**: Problem-solving · Self-managed work · Cross-functional · Virtual.

**Group decision-making advantages**: more complete info/knowledge · more diverse alternatives · increases acceptance · increases legitimacy. **Disadvantages**: time-consuming · minority domination · pressure to conform · ambiguous responsibility.

**Conflict-handling styles (5)**: Avoiding · Accommodating · Forcing · Compromising · Collaborating.

**Groups vs. Teams (Exhibit 11-9)**:
- Work Group: strong clearly focused leader · individual accountability · purpose same as broader org mission · individual work products · efficient meetings · effectiveness measured indirectly · discusses, decides, and delegates.
- Work Team: shared leadership roles · individual AND mutual accountability · specific team purpose team itself delivers · collective work products · open-ended discussion + active problem-solving meetings · performance measured directly by collective work products · discusses, decides, and does real work.

**Advantages of using teams**: outperform individuals · better use of employee talents · more flexible/responsive · quickly assembled, deployed, refocused, disbanded.

**Managing global teams considerations**: unique cultural characteristics · avoid stereotyping · less groupthink · status varies in importance across cultures · social loafing predominantly Western · cohesiveness harder · capitalize on diverse ideas · manager as culturally sensitive communicator.

**Current challenges in managing teams**: getting employees to cooperate · share information · confront differences · sublimate personal interest for team good.

### Notable examples
- **Asch line-judgment study** — demonstrates conformity pressure (Exhibit 11-4 cards). Use to back claims about peer pressure on judgment.
- Highly cohesive workgroup on a packaging line aligned with management goals → strong productivity gain (Exhibit 11-5 application).

## Communication (Ch 14)

### Key Definitions
- **Communication**: The transfer and understanding of meaning. Transfer = message received in interpretable form; understanding ≠ agreement.
- **Interpersonal communication**: Communication between two or more people.
- **Organizational communication**: All patterns, networks, and systems of communication within an organization.
- **Message**: A purpose to be conveyed.
- **Encoding**: Converting a message into symbols.
- **Channel / Medium**: The medium a message travels along.
- **Decoding**: Retranslating a sender's message.
- **Noise**: Any disturbances that interfere with the transmission, receipt, or feedback of a message.
- **Nonverbal communication**: Communication transmitted without words. Includes **body language** (gestures, facial configurations, body movements) and **verbal intonation** (emphasis given to words/phrases).
- **Formal communication**: Takes place within prescribed organizational work arrangements.
- **Informal communication**: Not defined by the organization's structural hierarchy.
- **Grapevine**: The informal organizational communication network.
- **Communication networks**: Variety of patterns of vertical and horizontal flows — chain, wheel, all-channel.
- **Ethical communication**: Communication that includes all relevant information, is true in every sense, and is not deceptive in any way.

### Frameworks / Theories — name, author, components verbatim, when applied

**1. Four Functions of Communication** — apply when asked "what role is communication playing":
- **Control** — formal and informal communications act to control individuals' behaviors in organizations.
- **Motivation** — clarifies what is to be done, how well, and how to improve performance.
- **Emotional Expression** — work-group communication provides a way for employees to express themselves socially.
- **Information** — individuals and work groups need information to make decisions or do their work.

**2. Interpersonal Communication Process Model (Exhibit 14-1)** — seven elements:
Sender → (Encoding) → Message → Channel/Medium → (Decoding) → Receiver → Message → Feedback (loops back to sender) — with **Noise** affecting every stage.

**3. Twelve Criteria for Choosing a Communication Method (Exhibit 14-2)** — apply to recommend the right channel:
1. **Feedback potential** — High: face-to-face, telephone, computer conference. Low: publications.
2. **Complexity capacity** — High: face-to-face. Low: bulletin boards.
3. **Breadth potential** — High: face-to-face, bulletin boards, e-mail. Low: postal mail, audio-videotapes.
4. **Confidentiality** — High: face-to-face, voice mail. Low: publications, bulletin boards, audio-videotapes, teleconference.
5. **Encoding ease** — High: face-to-face, telephone. Low: publications.
6. **Decoding ease (Time-Decoding Ease)** — High: face-to-face, telephone, hotlines, voice mail. Low: memos, postal mail, fax, publications.
7. **Time-space constraint** — High constraint: face-to-face, group meetings, formal presentations. Low: memos, postal mail, fax, publications, voice mail.
8. **Cost** — High: group meetings, formal presentations, videoconference. Low: bulletin boards.
9. **Interpersonal warmth (personal warmth)** — High: face-to-face. Low: memos, bulletin boards.
10. **Formality** — High: postal mail, publications. Low: face-to-face, telephone, voice mail.
11. **Scanability** — High: memos, postal mail, fax, publications, bulletin boards. Low: formal presentations, face-to-face, telephone, group meetings, audio-videotapes, hotlines, e-mail, computer conference, voice mail, teleconference, videoconference.
12. **Time of consumption** — sender vs. receiver controls when message is consumed.

**4. Channel Richness Hierarchy** — face-to-face (richest, most cues, immediate feedback) > video conferencing > telephone > voicemail/email > memos/letters > publications/bulletin boards (leanest). Use rich channels for complex/ambiguous/emotional messages; lean for routine/simple.

**5. Organizational Communication Networks (Exhibit 14-4)**:
- **Chain** — speed: moderate; accuracy: high; leader emergence: moderate; member satisfaction: moderate.
- **Wheel** — speed: fast; accuracy: high; leader emergence: high; satisfaction: low.
- **All-channel** — speed: fast; accuracy: moderate; leader emergence: none; satisfaction: high.

### Lists to memorize

**Functions of communication (4)**: Control · Motivation · Emotional Expression · Information.

**Seven elements of communication process**: Sender · Encoding · Message · Channel/Medium · Decoding · Receiver · Feedback (plus Noise across all).

**Methods of interpersonal communication (slides reference)**: Face-to-face · Telephone · Group meetings · Formal presentations · Memos · Postal mail · Fax · Publications · Bulletin boards · Audio-videotapes · Hotlines · E-mail · Computer conference · Voice mail · Teleconference · Videoconference · Blogs.

**Twelve evaluation criteria** (memorize as a unit): Feedback · Complexity capacity · Breadth potential · Confidentiality · Encoding ease · Decoding ease · Time-space constraint · Cost · Interpersonal warmth · Formality · Scanability · Time of consumption.

**Barriers to communication**:
- **Filtering** — deliberate manipulation of information to make it appear more favorable to receiver.
- **Emotions** — extreme emotions hinder communication, lead to emotional judgments, defensiveness, verbal attack.
- **Information overload** — when information exceeds processing capacity.
- **Defensiveness** — verbal attacks, sarcastic remarks, judgmental responses.
- **Language / Jargon** — specialized terminology used within a group.
- **National culture** — high-context (Japan) vs. low-context (US) differences.
- **Silence** — absence of information; signals lack of interest, powerlessness, unwillingness, negative emotion.

**Overcoming barriers (5 from slides)**:
1. **Use Feedback** — ask questions to confirm receipt and understanding.
2. **Simplify Language** — tailor to audience.
3. **Active Listening** — listen for full meaning without premature judgment.
4. **Constrain Emotions** — get emotions under control before communicating.
5. **Watch Nonverbal Cues** — actions must align with and reinforce words.

**Active listening behaviors (Exhibit 14-3)**: Don't interrupt · Show empathy · Paraphrase what's been said · Show interest by making eye contact · Avoid distracting actions or gestures · Exhibit affirmative head nods and appropriate facial expressions · Ask questions.

**Direction of communication flow**:
- **Downward** — manager → employees.
- **Upward** — employees → managers.
- **Lateral** — among employees on the same organizational level.
- **Diagonal** — cuts across work areas and organizational levels.

**Communication technology / role**: 24/7 connectivity, networked computer systems, internal social channels (e.g., HipChat), internal video portals, mobility, social media, knowledge management, CRM, employee input mechanisms; benefits = speed, remote collaboration, employee voice; downsides = distraction, lack of personal interaction, cybersecurity risk.

**Cybersecurity best practices**: strong/unique passwords + 2FA · suspicion of external links/phishing training · backups, secure Wi-Fi, antivirus/antimalware/firewall updates.

**How to become a better communicator**: persuasion · speaking · writing · reading skills.

### Notable examples
- High-context vs low-context culture: Japan (high) vs US (low) — non-verbal cues and situational context carry more weight in Japan.
- Sony / federal HR data breach — examples of real-world cyber risk affecting organizational communication channels.
- Chain network = traditional military hierarchy; Wheel = team with strong central leader; All-channel = self-managed team.

### Likely exam question forms
- "Trace the communication process in this miscommunication scenario and identify where noise/filtering broke it."
- "Recommend a communication method using at least 5 of the 12 criteria for this manager's situation."
- "Diagnose the barrier(s) to communication in the case and prescribe overcoming techniques."
- "Which network (chain/wheel/all-channel) suits this team and why? Use accuracy, speed, satisfaction."
- "Distinguish formal from informal/grapevine flows in the case; how should the manager use each?"
- "Apply the four functions of communication to a given message from a CEO."

## Personality (POM Personality Slides — Robbins/Judge basis)

### Key Definitions
- **Personality**: A dynamic concept describing the growth and development of a person's whole psychological system; the sum total of ways in which an individual reacts to and interacts with others.
- Etymology: Latin **persona** = "wearing a mask"; how one presents to the world, the effect one leaves on others.
- **Heredity**: Factors determined at conception (molecular structure of genes in chromosomes); the heredity approach argues genes are the ultimate explanation. Personality is also shaped by environment in formative years.
- **Person-Job Fit / Person-Organization Fit**: Used in hiring decisions; personality tests forecast best-fit candidates. Common measurement = self-report surveys.
- **Locus of control**: Internal (control own fate) vs. External (luck/chance/others control outcomes).
- **Self-monitoring**: Ability to adjust behavior to external situational factors. High self-monitors adapt readily; low self-monitors are consistent across situations.
- **Self-esteem**: Degree to which individuals like or dislike themselves.
- **Risk-taking**: Willingness to take chances; affects decision speed and information needs.
- **Type A personality**: Chronic sense of urgency, excessive competitive drive, impatient. **Type B**: Relaxed, non-competitive, patient.
- **Proactive personality**: Identifies opportunities, shows initiative, takes action, perseveres until change occurs.
- **Emotions**: Intense feelings directed at someone or something. **Emotional Intelligence (EI)**: Assortment of non-cognitive skills/abilities/competencies influencing capacity to succeed under environmental demands.

### Frameworks / Theories — name, author, components verbatim, when applied

**1. Jung's Personality Theory** — Carl Jung. Preferences are innate "inborn predispositions" but shaped by environment (family, culture, education). Nature (MBTI instrument) vs. Nurture (environment). Foundation for MBTI.

**2. Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI)** — Isabel Briggs Myers & Katharine Cook Briggs (built on Jung). The most widely used personality framework. **4 dichotomies → 16 types**:
- **Extraversion (E) ↔ Introversion (I)** — where attention/energy is focused (external world vs. internal world).
- **Sensing (S) ↔ Intuition (N)** — how information is taken in (sequential/factual vs. snapshot/big-picture).
- **Thinking (T) ↔ Feeling (F)** — how decisions are made (objective/cause-and-effect vs. empathetic/values-based).
- **Judging (J) ↔ Perceiving (P)** — attitude toward external world (planned/scheduled vs. spontaneous/open-ended).

E vs. I traits — E: energized by interacting, sociable, expressive, communicate face-to-face, work out ideas by talking, broad interests, learn by doing/discussing, take initiative readily, "Do-Think-Do." I: energized by reflection, private/contained, communicate by writing, work out ideas by thinking, deep focus, learn by reflection/mental practice, take initiative on important issues, "Think-Do-Think." Keywords E: Action, Outward, People, Interaction, Many, Expressive. Keywords I: Reflection, Inward, Privacy, Concentration, Few, Quiet.

S vs. N traits — S: focus on real/actual, observe/remember specifics, factual/concrete/sequential, build carefully toward conclusions, practical applications, specific/literal, trust experience. N: focus on patterns/meanings, remember specifics relating to a pattern, abstract/imaginative, move quickly to conclusions/follow hunches, generate ideas/theories, use metaphors/analogies, trust insight. Keywords S: Facts, Realistic, Specific, Present, Keep, Practical, What is. Keywords N: Ideas, Imaginative, General, Future, Change, Theoretical, What could be.

T vs. F traits — T: step back for objective view, analyze, cause-and-effect reasoning, solve with logic, strive for objective truth, "reasonable," "tough-minded," fair = treat everyone equally. F: step in to identify, empathize, guided by personal/group values, assess impact on people, strive for harmony, compassionate, "tenderhearted," fair = treat everyone as individual. Keywords T: Head, Detached, Things, Objective, Critique, Analyse, Firm but fair. Keywords F: Heart, Personal, People, Subjective, Praise, Understand, Merciful.

J vs. P traits — J: organized, systematic, methodical, plan and follow, like things decided, resist reopening decisions, avoid last-minute stress. P: adaptable, curious, casual, open-ended, adjust flexibly, like to explore options, resist closing options too soon, energized by last-minute pressure. Keywords J: Organised, Decision, Control, Now, Closure, Deliberate, Plan. Keywords P: Flexible, Information, Experience, Later, Options, Spontaneous, Wait.

**16 Types (the "16-Room House")**:
ISTJ · ISFJ · INFJ · INTJ
ISTP · ISFP · INFP · INTP
ESTP · ESFP · ENFP · ENTP
ESTJ · ESFJ · ENFJ · ENTJ
Examples noted: **INTJ = Visionaries**, **ESTJ = Organizers**, **ENTP = Conceptualizers**.

**3. Big Five Model (OCEAN)** — apply to predict job performance and OB outcomes:
- **Openness to Experience** — range of interests and fascination with novelty. Highly Open: creative, curious, artistically sensitive. Low Open: conventional, comfortable in the familiar. Workplace effects: increased learning, more creative, more flexible/autonomous → training performance, enhanced leadership, more adaptable to change.
- **Conscientiousness** — measures reliability. High: responsible, organized, dependable, persistent. Low: easily distracted, disorganized, unreliable. Effects: greater effort/persistence, more drive/discipline, better organized → higher performance, enhanced leadership, greater longevity.
- **Extraversion** — comfort level with relationships. Extraverts: gregarious, assertive, sociable. Introverts: reserved, timid, quiet. Effects: better interpersonal skills, greater social dominance, more emotionally expressive → higher performance, enhanced leadership, higher job and life satisfaction.
- **Agreeableness** — propensity to defer to others. High: cooperative, warm, trusting. Low: cold, disagreeable, antagonistic. Effects: better liked, more compliant/conforming → higher performance, lower deviant behavior.
- **Emotional Stability (inverse of Neuroticism)** — ability to withstand stress. Positive (stable): calm, self-confident, secure. Negative (neurotic): nervous, anxious, depressed, insecure. Effects: less negative thinking, less hypervigilant → higher job/life satisfaction, lower stress.

**4. Trait Activation Theory (Exhibit 5-3)** — certain Big Five traits become more relevant in specific job contexts. Examples: Detail-oriented jobs activate **Conscientiousness (+)** (air traffic controller, accountant, legal secretary); Social skills jobs activate **Extraversion (+) Agreeableness (+)** (clergy, therapist, concierge); Competitive work activates **Extraversion (+) Agreeableness (−)** (coach, financial manager, sales rep); Innovation work activates **Openness (+)** (actor, systems analyst, advertising writer); Dealing with angry people activates **Extraversion (+) Agreeableness (+) Neuroticism (−)** (correctional officer, telemarketer, flight attendant); Time pressure / deadlines activates **Conscientiousness (+) Neuroticism (−)** (broadcast news analyst, editor, airline pilot).

**5. Dark Triad** — three socially undesirable traits managers should screen for:
- **Machiavellianism** — pragmatic, emotional distance, "ends justify means," manipulative.
- **Narcissism** — grandiose self-importance, requires excessive admiration, sense of entitlement, arrogance.
- **Psychopathy** — lack of concern for others, lack of guilt/remorse over harmful actions.

**6. Other OB-Relevant Personality Traits**:
- Locus of control (internal vs. external).
- Self-monitoring (high adapts to situation; low is consistent).
- Self-esteem.
- Risk-taking propensity.
- Type A vs. Type B.
- Proactive personality.

**7. Holland's Personality-Job Fit Theory (RIASEC)** — John Holland. Six personality types matched to congruent occupational environments:
- **R — Realistic**: prefers physical activities requiring skill, strength, coordination. Traits: shy, genuine, persistent, stable, conforming, practical. Jobs: mechanic, farmer, assembly-line worker.
- **I — Investigative**: activities involving thinking, organizing, understanding. Traits: analytical, original, curious, independent. Jobs: biologist, economist, mathematician, news reporter.
- **A — Artistic**: ambiguous and unsystematic activities for creative expression. Traits: imaginative, disorderly, idealistic, emotional, impractical. Jobs: painter, musician, writer, interior decorator.
- **S — Social**: activities involving helping/developing others. Traits: sociable, friendly, cooperative, understanding. Jobs: social worker, teacher, counselor, clinical psychologist.
- **E — Enterprising**: verbal activities to influence others, attain power. Traits: self-confident, ambitious, energetic, domineering. Jobs: lawyer, real estate agent, PR specialist, small-business manager.
- **C — Conventional**: rule-regulated, orderly, unambiguous activities. Traits: conforming, efficient, practical, unimaginative, inflexible. Jobs: accountant, corporate manager, bank teller, file clerk.

The closer two types on the hexagon (R-I-A-S-E-C arrangement), the more compatible. Person-job fit predicts satisfaction and turnover.

**8. Goleman's 5 Components of Emotional Intelligence (EI)** — Daniel Goleman:
1. **Self-awareness** — being aware of what you are feeling.
2. **Self-management** — ability to manage own emotions and impulses.
3. **Self-motivation** — ability to persist in the face of setbacks/failures.
4. **Empathy** — ability to sense how others are feeling.
5. **Social skills** — ability to handle the emotions of others.

EI predicts job performance especially in roles demanding high social interaction.

### Lists to memorize

**MBTI 4 dichotomies**: E/I · S/N · T/F · J/P → 16 combinations.

**Big Five (OCEAN)**: Openness · Conscientiousness · Extraversion · Agreeableness · Neuroticism (Emotional Stability).

**Dark Triad**: Machiavellianism · Narcissism · Psychopathy.

**RIASEC**: Realistic · Investigative · Artistic · Social · Enterprising · Conventional.

**Goleman EI components**: Self-awareness · Self-management · Self-motivation · Empathy · Social skills.

**Other OB traits**: Locus of control · Self-monitoring · Self-esteem · Risk-taking · Type A/B · Proactive personality.

**Determinants of personality**: Heredity (genes) + Environment (formative years).

**Personality–Job link uses**: Resume (achievements, on-the-feet thinking, team events, leadership, crisis management); Group Discussion (leadership initiative, positive approach, consensus building, lateral thinking, listening, communication, extroversion/introversion); Job Interview (proactiveness, strengths/weaknesses, response to stress questions).

**General application areas of personality assessment**: Self-understanding · Communication · Team-building · Management & leadership · Coaching · Organizational development · Diversity/multicultural training · Problem-solving & conflict resolution · Career development · Education/learning style · Relationships.

**MBTI strengths/weaknesses (quick recall)**:
- I strengths: independent, works alone, diligent, reflective, careful before acting. I weaknesses: misunderstands external, secretive, loses opportunities to act, dislikes interruptions.
- E strengths: understands external, interacts, open, acts. E weaknesses: less independent, doesn't work without people, impulsive, impatient with routine.
- N strengths: sees possibilities/big picture, works with complicated, solves novel problems. N weaknesses: same traits in excess (loses detail).
- S strengths: detail, practical, memory for fact, patient, systematic. S weaknesses: doesn't see possibilities, mistrusts intuition, doesn't work out new.
- F strengths: considers feelings, understands needs/values, conciliatory, persuasive. F weaknesses: not logical, not objective, less organized, uncritical.
- T strengths: logical/analytical/objective, organized, critical ability, just, stands firm. T weaknesses: doesn't notice feelings, misunderstands values, uninterested in conciliation, shows less mercy.
- P strengths: compromises, sees all sides, flexible, open to changes, decides on all data. P weaknesses: indecisive, no plan, no order, distracted, doesn't finish.
- J strengths: decides, plans, orders, controls, quick decisions, sticks to task. J weaknesses: unyielding, inflexible, decides with insufficient data, judgmental.
