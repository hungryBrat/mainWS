# PART 1: INTRODUCTION

## T1: Introduction to Management & Organizations

### Key Definitions
- **Manager**: someone who coordinates and oversees the work of other people so that organizational goals can be accomplished.
- **Management**: coordinating and overseeing work activities of others so they are completed **efficiently** and **effectively**.
- **Efficiency**: doing things right — getting the most output for the least input (means).
- **Effectiveness**: doing the right things — attaining organizational goals (ends).
- **Organization**: a deliberate arrangement of people brought together to accomplish a specific purpose.

### Four Functions of Management (Fayol framework)
| Function | What it involves |
|---|---|
| **Planning** | Defining goals, establishing strategy, developing plans to coordinate activities |
| **Organizing** | Determining what needs to be done, how, and by whom (structure, staffing) |
| **Leading** | Motivating, directing, resolving conflicts, influencing employees |
| **Controlling** | Monitoring, comparing, correcting performance against goals |

Mnemonic: **POLC**

### Mintzberg's 10 Managerial Roles
Three categories:

**Interpersonal (from formal authority):**
1. Figurehead — symbolic duties (signing documents, greeting visitors)
2. Leader — motivating, training, staffing subordinates
3. Liaison — maintaining network of outside contacts

**Informational:**
4. Monitor — scanning environment for information, receiving reports
5. Disseminator — transmitting information inside the organization
6. Spokesperson — transmitting information to outsiders

**Decisional:**
7. Entrepreneur — initiating change, innovation, new projects
8. Disturbance handler — corrective action during crises
9. Resource allocator — assigning budgets, time, people, equipment
10. Negotiator — representing the unit in major negotiations

### Katz's Three Essential Skills
| Skill | Definition | Most critical for |
|---|---|---|
| **Technical** | Job-specific knowledge & techniques | Lower/first-line managers |
| **Human (interpersonal)** | Working well with others individually and in groups | ALL levels equally |
| **Conceptual** | Thinking about abstract/complex situations; seeing the whole | Top managers |

### Exam Application
> A CEO attends the board meeting (Figurehead), reads industry reports (Monitor), fires an underperforming VP (Disturbance handler), and allocates R&D budget (Resource allocator). Identify the Mintzberg role for each action.

## T2: Management History

### Schools of Management Thought

**Scientific Management — Frederick W. Taylor (1856–1915)**
Focused on "one best way" using time-and-motion studies; economically motivated workers.

**Taylor's 4 Principles:**
1. Develop a *science* for each element of work (replace rule-of-thumb with science)
2. Scientifically *select*, train, teach, and develop each worker
3. *Cooperate* heartily with workers to ensure work is done according to scientific principles
4. *Divide* work and responsibility almost equally between management and workers

Other contributors: Frank & Lillian Gilbreth (therbligs — 17 basic work motions, motion study).

**General Administrative Theory — Henri Fayol (1841–1925)**
Identified 5 functions (planning, organizing, commanding, coordinating, controlling) and **14 Principles of Management:**
1. Division of work  2. Authority  3. Discipline  4. Unity of command  5. Unity of direction  6. Subordination of individual interests to the general interest  7. Remuneration  8. Centralization  9. Scalar chain  10. Order  11. Equity  12. Stability of personnel tenure  13. Initiative  14. Esprit de corps

Also: Max Weber — Bureaucracy: division of labor, hierarchy, formal rules, impersonality, career orientation.

**Behavioral Approach:**
- **Mary Parker Follett**: organizations should be based on group ethics; "power-with" vs "power-over."
- **Hawthorne Studies** (Elton Mayo, 1924–1933, Western Electric): illumination experiments → social norms, group sentiments, and intergroup relations influenced productivity more than physical conditions. Founded **organizational behavior (OB)**.

**Quantitative Approach:** Operations research / management science — applying quantitative techniques (statistics, linear programming, queuing) to management decisions.

**Contingency Approach (Situational):** There is no one universally applicable set of management principles — the appropriate action depends on the situation.

**Systems Approach:** Closed system (no interaction with environment) vs **open system** (interacts with environment). Organizations as open systems: inputs → transformation → outputs → feedback.

### Exam Application
> "Describe the Classical approaches to management. How do they differ from the Behavioral approach?" — Answer: Classical = Scientific (Taylor's 4 principles) + General Admin (Fayol's 14 principles + Weber's bureaucracy); focused on efficiency and formal rules. Behavioral = Hawthorne effect, human relations, worker motivation through group dynamics.

## T3: External Environment

### Key Definitions
- **External environment**: all institutions or forces outside the organization that potentially affect the organization's performance.
- **Specific environment**: forces that directly and immediately affect the organization (customers, suppliers, competitors, industry pressure groups).
- **General environment**: broad economic, political/legal, socio-cultural, demographic, technological, global conditions (PESTLE).

### Environmental Uncertainty Matrix (Lawrence & Lorsch)
Two dimensions: **complexity** (few vs. many factors) × **rate of change** (stable vs. dynamic):

| | Stable | Dynamic |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple** | Low uncertainty | Moderately low uncertainty |
| **Complex** | Moderately high uncertainty | High uncertainty |

### PESTLE Analysis
| Factor | Key variables |
|---|---|
| Political | Govt. stability, tax policy, trade restrictions |
| Economic | GDP, inflation, interest rates, unemployment |
| Social/Cultural | Demographics, lifestyle trends, education |
| Technological | R&D activity, automation, innovation rate |
| Legal | Employment law, health & safety, consumer protection |
| Environmental | Climate, sustainability, energy |

### Stakeholder Analysis
Stakeholders: any group or individual who is affected by, or can affect, the organization. Key stakeholders: shareholders, employees, customers, suppliers, community, government.

### Exam Application
> Analyze ITC's specific environment: customers (FMCG buyers), competitors (Hindustan Unilever, P&G), suppliers (agricultural raw materials, paper). Use PESTLE for general environment: post-COVID digital shift (Technological), GST impact (Political/Legal).

## T4: Organizational Culture

### Key Definitions
- **Organizational culture**: shared values, principles, traditions, and ways of doing things that influence organizational members' behavior.
- **Strong culture**: key values widely shared and intensely held; low employee turnover, high performance orientation.
- **Weak culture**: values not widely shared; more reliance on formal rules and procedures.

### 7 Dimensions of Organizational Culture (Robbins)
1. **Attention to detail** — degree to which employees are expected to exhibit precision, analysis, and attention to detail
2. **Outcome orientation** — degree to which managers focus on results/outcomes rather than on processes used
3. **People orientation** — degree to which management decisions consider the effect of outcomes on people in the organization
4. **Team orientation** — degree to which work activities are organized around teams rather than individuals
5. **Aggressiveness** — degree to which employees are aggressive and competitive rather than easygoing and cooperative
6. **Stability** — degree to which organizational decisions and actions emphasize maintaining the status quo
7. **Innovation and risk taking** — degree to which employees are encouraged to be innovative and to take risks

### How Culture is Transmitted (Sources)
- **Stories** — narratives of significant events or people
- **Rituals** — repetitive sequences of activities expressing values (annual awards, orientation)
- **Material artifacts** — physical layout, dress code, company cars, office size
- **Language** — acronyms, jargon, special terms shared by members

### Types of Cultures
- **Ethical culture**: high ethics; leaders model ethical behavior, norms promote ethical behavior.
- **Innovative culture**: characterized by trust, openness, risk-taking; freedom to experiment.
- **Customer-responsive culture**: employees outgoing, friendly; minimal rigid rules; service above all.

### Exam Application
> "Explain why organizational culture is important using ITC as an example." — ITC has a high people-orientation and outcome-orientation; these 2 of the 7 dimensions explain why employee welfare programs coexist with aggressive revenue targets.

## T5: CSR and Ethics

### CSR Views
- **Classical view** (Milton Friedman): management's only social responsibility is to maximize profits for shareholders.
- **Socioeconomic view**: management's responsibility goes beyond profit-making to include protecting and improving society's welfare.

### Three Positions on CSR
1. **Social obligation** — does the minimum required by law; profit as sole goal.
2. **Social responsiveness** — adapts to changing societal demands; reactive.
3. **Social responsibility** — pursues long-term goals that are good for society; proactive.

### Green Management (Environmental Responsibility, 4 stages)
1. **Legal**: doing what is required by law (Stage 1, lowest)
2. **Market**: responding to customer/shareholder environmental preferences (Stage 2)
3. **Stakeholder**: meeting broad stakeholder expectations (Stage 3)
4. **Activist**: looking for ways to preserve the environment (Stage 4, highest)

### Ethics Views
- **Utilitarian**: greatest good for greatest number; focus on outcomes.
- **Rights**: protecting individual rights (life, freedom, due process); focus on process.
- **Theory of justice**: fair and impartial enforcement of rules; distributive justice.
- **Integrative social contracts**: general ethical principles (hypernorms) + community norms.

### Kohlberg's Stages of Moral Development
**Pre-conventional level** (self-interest):
1. Obedience/punishment — follow rules to avoid punishment
2. Instrumental purpose — follow rules when in immediate interest

**Conventional level** (social norms):
3. Interpersonal accord/conformity — live up to what others expect
4. Social accord/system — duty to maintain law and order

**Post-conventional/principled level** (universal principles):
5. Social contract — uphold basic rights of others; democratic rules
6. Universal ethical principles — follow self-chosen ethical principles even if they violate laws

### Factors Influencing Ethical Behavior
- Individual (stage of moral development, personality, values)
- Issue characteristics (intensity, moral framing)
- Structural variables (formal codes, clear performance criteria, reward systems)

### Exam Application
> "A pharma company bribes regulators to fast-track a life-saving drug. Analyze using ethics views." — Utilitarian: justified (saves lives). Rights: not justified (corrupts due process). Justice: not justified (unequal treatment).

## T6: Global Environment

### Attitudes Toward Global Business
- **Parochialism**: viewing the world solely through your own perspectives, values, and customs.
- **Ethnocentric attitude**: belief that home-country approaches are best; key positions filled by home-country nationals.
- **Polycentric attitude**: belief that host-country managers understand their own country best; key positions filled by host-country nationals.
- **Geocentric attitude**: world-oriented view; best people regardless of nationality fill key positions.

### Types of Global Organizations
| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| MNC / Multinational | Operations in multiple countries; each country acts independently |
| Multidomestic | Products/marketing customized per country |
| Global | Standardized products; centralized control |
| Transnational | Both global efficiency + local responsiveness (borderless) |

### How to Go Global (escalating commitment)
1. Exporting/Importing
2. Licensing (manufacturer) / Franchising (service firm)
3. Strategic alliance / Joint venture
4. Foreign subsidiary (wholly owned — highest commitment, highest risk)

### Hofstede's 5 Cultural Dimensions
1. **Power distance** — degree to which unequal distribution of power is accepted
2. **Individualism vs. collectivism** — prefer loosely knit social framework vs. tight groups
3. **Achievement vs. nurturing** (Masculinity vs. femininity) — assertive/competitive vs. caring/cooperative values
4. **Uncertainty avoidance** — degree of discomfort with ambiguity and uncertainty
5. **Long-term vs. short-term orientation** — future-oriented perseverance vs. respect for traditions

### Exam Application
> "Why might IndiGo's management style need to change when expanding into Japan?" — Hofstede: Japan has high uncertainty avoidance (need detailed SOPs, formal rules), high power distance, and long-term orientation. IndiGo should adapt hierarchy, communication channels, and planning horizons accordingly.

## T7: Teams

### Group vs. Team
| Group | Team |
|---|---|
| No strong commitment to common purpose | Shared purpose + mutual accountability |
| Individual accountability | Individual AND mutual accountability |
| No synergy (sum = parts) | Positive synergy (sum > parts) |

### Types of Teams
1. **Problem-solving team** — 5–12 members from same dept; meet to improve quality/processes; no authority to implement changes
2. **Self-managed team** — 10–15 members who take on managerial responsibilities (hiring, planning, scheduling); no traditional manager
3. **Cross-functional team** — members from same level but different work areas; coordinate complex projects
4. **Virtual team** — uses technology to link physically dispersed members; global teams

### Tuckman's 5 Stages of Group Development
1. **Forming** — purpose, structure, leadership being defined; high uncertainty
2. **Storming** — intragroup conflict; resistance to group control; leadership contested
3. **Norming** — close relationships develop; cohesion increases; norms established
4. **Performing** — fully functional structure; energetic focus on the task
5. **Adjourning** — group prepares to disband; concerned with closure rather than performance

### Characteristics of Effective Teams (8 — must list verbatim)
1. Clear goals
2. Relevant skills (technical + interpersonal)
3. Mutual trust
4. Unified commitment
5. Good communication
6. Negotiating skills
7. Appropriate leadership
8. Internal and external support

### Types of Conflict
| Type | Nature | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Task conflict** | Disagreement over content/goals | Functional at low level |
| **Relationship conflict** | Interpersonal incompatibility | Always dysfunctional |
| **Process conflict** | How work gets done | Functional at low-moderate level |

### Thomas-Kilmann Conflict-Handling Styles
- **Competing/Forcing** (high assertive, low cooperative): wins at others' expense
- **Collaborating** (high assertive, high cooperative): win-win solution
- **Compromising** (mid, mid): each gives something up
- **Avoiding** (low assertive, low cooperative): withdrawing
- **Accommodating** (low assertive, high cooperative): self-sacrifice

### Exam Application
> "Ivan works in a team where members disagree about sales targets (task conflict). His colleague refuses to speak to him (relationship conflict). How should the team leader handle each?" — Task conflict at low level is functional — allow debate. Relationship conflict is always dysfunctional — use Collaborating or Accommodating style to address interpersonal issues before it escalates.

## T8: Communication

### Key Definitions
- **Communication**: transferring meaning and understanding from one person to another.
- **4 Functions**: 1. Control (via formal channels), 2. Motivation, 3. Emotional expression, 4. Information

### Communication Process Model
**Sender** → [encodes message] → **Message** → [through Channel] → **Receiver** → [decodes message] → **Feedback** loop back to sender. **Noise** can occur at any stage.

### Methods of Interpersonal Communication (selection criteria)
Key criteria for choosing a channel: feedback speed, complexity, breadth potential, confidentiality, encoding ease, decoding ease, time-space constraint, cost, interpersonal warmth, formality, scanability.

**Channel richness hierarchy** (richest to leanest):
Face-to-face → Video conference → Phone → Email → Memo/Letter → Impersonal written/reports

Rich channels: multiple cues, fast feedback, natural language, personal focus.
Lean channels: single cue, slow feedback, formal language, impersonal.

### Barriers to Effective Communication
1. **Filtering** — deliberately manipulating information so it appears favorable
2. **Emotions** — emotional state of receiver affects interpretation
3. **Information overload** — too much information; receiver can't process all
4. **Defensiveness** — threatened by communication; leads to attacks/sarcasm
5. **Language** — words mean different things to different people; jargon
6. **National culture** — different norms around directness, formality, nonverbal cues

### Overcoming Barriers
- Use feedback; simplify language; listen actively; constrain emotions; watch nonverbal cues; use multiple channels.

### Direction of Communication Flow
- **Downward**: manager to employee (assignments, policies, feedback)
- **Upward**: employee to manager (reports, suggestions, complaints)
- **Lateral/Horizontal**: between peers
- **Diagonal**: across work areas and levels
- **Grapevine** (informal): cuts across formal channels; fast but inaccurate; managers should monitor

### Exam Application
> "You are CEO closing 500 jobs. Which 2 communication methods will you use? Justify using criteria." — Face-to-face (town hall) for emotional warmth, immediate feedback, managing reactions. Written memo (formal letter) for legal clarity, confidentiality, documented record. Apply channel richness — high-stakes bad news requires richest channel.

# PART 3: MANAGEMENT FUNCTIONS

## T10: Making Decisions & Planning

### Key Definitions
- **Decision**: a choice among two or more alternatives.
- **Structured problems**: straightforward, familiar, easily defined → **Programmed decisions** (procedure, rule, policy).
- **Unstructured problems**: new/unusual, ambiguous information → **Nonprogrammed decisions** (custom solutions).

### 8-Step Decision-Making Process
1. Identify the problem
2. Identify decision criteria
3. Allocate weights to the criteria
4. Develop alternatives
5. Analyze alternatives (score each on each criterion × weight)
6. Select an alternative (highest weighted total score)
7. Implement the alternative
8. Evaluate decision effectiveness

### Decision-Making Conditions
- **Certainty**: all outcomes known
- **Risk**: probability of outcomes can be estimated
- **Uncertainty**: no reasonable probability estimates available

### ★ DECISION-MAKING UNDER UNCERTAINTY (PYQ essential — compute all 4)

Given payoff matrix (rows = strategies, columns = competitor/nature actions):

**1. MAXIMAX (Optimistic):** For each row, find the *maximum* payoff. Then choose the row with the *highest* maximum. ("best of the bests")

**2. MAXIMIN / Wald (Pessimistic):** For each row, find the *minimum* payoff. Then choose the row with the *highest* minimum. ("best of the worsts")

**3. LAPLACE / Equal-likelihood:** Average payoff for each row (sum ÷ number of columns). Choose the highest average.

**4. MINIMAX REGRET (Savage):**
- Step 1: Build regret matrix — for each column, subtract every cell value from the column's maximum. (Regret = what you missed by not picking the best in that state)
- Step 2: For each row, find the maximum regret.
- Step 3: Choose the row with the *smallest* maximum regret.

**Worked Example** (from slides — Visa vs. MasterCard):

Payoff Matrix ($ million):

| Strategy | CA1 | CA2 | CA3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| S1 | 13 | 14 | 11 |
| S2 | 9 | 15 | 18 |
| S3 | 24 | 21 | 15 |
| S4 | 18 | 14 | 28 |

- **MAXIMAX**: row maxima = 14, 18, 24, 28 → **S4 (28)**
- **MAXIMIN**: row minima = 11, 9, 15, 14 → **S3 (15)**
- **LAPLACE**: averages = 38/3=12.7, 42/3=14, 60/3=20, 60/3=20 → **S3 or S4 (tie at 20)**
- **MINIMAX REGRET**: Col maxima = 24, 21, 28. Regret matrix:
  - S1: 24-13=11, 21-14=7, 28-11=17 → max regret = 17
  - S2: 24-9=15, 21-15=6, 28-18=10 → max regret = 15
  - S3: 24-24=0, 21-21=0, 28-15=13 → max regret = 13
  - S4: 24-18=6, 21-14=7, 28-28=0 → max regret = 7
  → **S4 (max regret = 7)**

### Decision-Making Perspectives (4)
1. **Rationality** — logical, maximize value, all alternatives known.
2. **Bounded rationality** — limited by ability to process info; **satisficing** (good enough, not optimal); **escalation of commitment**.
3. **Intuition** — based on experience, feelings, accumulated judgment.
4. **Evidence-based management (EBMgt)** — systematic use of best available evidence. 4 elements: (1) decision-maker's expertise, (2) external evidence, (3) stakeholder values, (4) internal organizational factors.

### 12 Decision-Making Biases
Overconfidence, Immediate gratification, Anchoring, Selective perception, Confirmation, Framing, Availability, Representation, Randomness, Sunk costs, Self-serving, Hindsight.

### Planning
- **Planning**: primary function — setting goals, establishing strategies, developing plans to coordinate activities.
- **Why plan?** Provides direction; reduces uncertainty; minimizes waste/redundancy; establishes goals/standards for controlling.

**Types of Plans (4 dimensions):**
| Dimension | Types |
|---|---|
| Breadth | Strategic (whole org) / Operational (specific area) |
| Time Frame | Long-term (>3 years) / Short-term (≤1 year) |
| Specificity | Specific (clearly defined) / Directional (flexible guidelines) |
| Frequency | Single-use (one-time event) / Standing (ongoing/repeated) |

**MBO** (Management by Objectives): Setting *mutually agreed upon* goals and using those goals to evaluate employee performance. Process: Set organizational objectives → translate to dept goals → individual goals → action plans → periodic review → performance appraisal.

**SMART goals**: Specific · Measurable · Attainable · Relevant · Time-based.

> Given the Air India payoff matrix from PYQ: Apply all 4 uncertainty methods showing full computation step by step. Label which psychological orientation each method corresponds to (optimistic = Maximax, pessimistic = Maximin, neutral/equal = Laplace, regret-minimizing = Minimax Regret).

## T11: Organizational Structure

### 6 Key Elements of Organizational Design
1. **Work specialization** — dividing work activities into separate job tasks for efficiency
2. **Departmentalization** — basis by which jobs are grouped: *functional, product, geographic, process, customer*
3. **Chain of command** — line of authority from top to bottom; clarifies who reports to whom
4. **Span of control** — number of employees a manager can efficiently and effectively supervise
5. **Centralization vs. decentralization** — where decision-making authority resides
6. **Formalization** — degree to which jobs are standardized; extent to which rules/procedures guide behavior

### Mechanistic vs. Organic (Comparison Table)
| Mechanistic | Organic |
|---|---|
| Rigid hierarchical relationships | Collaboration (vertical and horizontal) |
| Fixed duties | Adaptable duties |
| Many rules | Few rules |
| Formalized communication channels | Informal communication |
| Centralized decision authority | Decentralized decision authority |
| Taller structures | Flatter structures |

### Contingency Factors Affecting Structure
1. **Strategy** (Chandler): structure follows strategy — cost-leadership → mechanistic; innovation → organic
2. **Size**: large organizations → more specialization, more rules, more decentralization
3. **Technology** (Woodward): unit/small batch → organic; mass production → mechanistic; process (continuous flow) → organic
4. **Environmental uncertainty**: stable → mechanistic; dynamic/complex → organic

### Traditional Structures
- **Simple**: low departmentalization, wide spans, centralized authority, little formalization (typical of small firms/owner-managed).
- **Functional**: groups similar occupational specialties together (HR, Finance, Marketing depts).
- **Divisional**: separate business units or divisions, each with own goals and functional departments.

### Contemporary Structures
- **Team structure**: entire organization made up of work teams
- **Matrix**: specialists from functional departments assigned to project managers (dual reporting)
- **Project**: employees continuously work on projects (no permanent depts)
- **Boundaryless**: not defined by predefined structures; free flow of info; e.g., Jack Welch (GE)
- **Virtual/Network**: core employees + outsourced work activities
- **Learning organization**: capacity to continuously learn, adapt, and change (characterized by boundaryless design, teams, empowerment; strong culture of relationships and trust; open information sharing; shared leadership vision)

### Exam Application
> "Flipkart restructures to shrink its 22 office floors to 5. Identify the restructuring impact using the 6 key elements of org design." — Span of control increases (fewer managers), formalization decreases, decentralization increases → shifting toward organic structure.

## T12: Motivation

### Definition
**Motivation**: the process by which a person's efforts are **energized**, **directed**, and **sustained** toward attaining a goal. Three elements: 1. Energy (intensity/drive), 2. Direction (toward org goals), 3. Persistence (sustained over time).

### Early Theories

**Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs** (A.H. Maslow)
5 levels (must meet lower before higher becomes motivator):
1. Physiological — food, water, shelter, sex, sleep
2. Safety — security, stability, freedom from physical/emotional harm
3. Social/Love — affection, belonging, acceptance, friendship
4. Esteem — self-respect, achievement, autonomy, status, recognition
5. Self-actualization — growth, self-fulfillment, achieving full potential

**McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y**
- **Theory X**: employees dislike work, are lazy, avoid responsibility, must be coerced/controlled/threatened with punishment.
- **Theory Y**: employees are creative, enjoy work, seek responsibility, can exercise self-direction — they don't need to be coerced.

**Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory** (motivation-hygiene theory)

| **Motivators** (job satisfaction) | **Hygiene Factors** (job dissatisfaction) |
|---|---|
| Achievement | Supervision |
| Recognition | Company policy |
| Work itself | Relationship with supervisor |
| Responsibility | Working conditions |
| Advancement | Salary |
| Growth | Relationship with peers |
| | Personal life, Status, Security |

Key distinction: Removing hygiene factors removes dissatisfaction but does NOT create satisfaction. Motivators create satisfaction; absence of motivators = no satisfaction (NOT dissatisfaction).

**McClelland's Three-Needs Theory**
- **nAch** (Need for Achievement) — drive to excel, succeed against standards, strive for accomplishment
- **nPow** (Need for Power) — need to make others behave in a way they would not otherwise
- **nAff** (Need for Affiliation) — desire for friendly, close interpersonal relationships

### Contemporary Theories

**Goal-Setting Theory** (Locke): Specific and challenging goals lead to higher performance than vague goals. Key factors: goal commitment, self-efficacy, feedback, task complexity, national culture.

**Job Characteristics Model (JCM)** — Hackman & Oldham
5 Core Job Dimensions:
1. **Skill variety** — uses different skills and talents
2. **Task identity** — completing a whole and identifiable piece of work
3. **Task significance** — substantial impact on others' lives or work
4. **Autonomy** — freedom and independence in scheduling and determining procedures
5. **Feedback** — direct, clear information about effectiveness

Dimensions 1–3 → **Experienced meaningfulness**
Dimension 4 → **Experienced responsibility**
Dimension 5 → **Knowledge of results**

**MPS Formula** (Motivating Potential Score):
> MPS = [(Skill Variety + Task Identity + Task Significance) / 3] × Autonomy × Feedback

Job redesign strategies: combine tasks → skill variety + task identity; form natural work units → task identity + significance; establish client relationships → skill variety + autonomy + feedback; expand jobs vertically → autonomy; open feedback channels → feedback.

**Equity Theory** (Adams)
Employee compares **Outcomes/Inputs** ratio to a *referent* (other-inside, other-outside, self-inside, self-outside).
- Outcomes A/Inputs A < Outcomes B/Inputs B → Inequity (underrewarded)
- Outcomes A/Inputs A = Outcomes B/Inputs B → **Equity**
- Outcomes A/Inputs A > Outcomes B/Inputs B → Inequity (overrewarded)

**Equality vs. Equity (PYQ trap):**
- **Equality** = same reward to all employees regardless of contribution
- **Equity** = reward proportional to each employee's input/contribution

**Distributive justice**: perceived fairness of the *amount* of rewards.
**Procedural justice**: perceived fairness of the *process* used to determine rewards.

**Expectancy Theory** (Vroom, 1964)
> Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence

- **Expectancy (E→P)**: belief that effort leads to performance
- **Instrumentality (P→O)**: belief that performance leads to reward
- **Valence**: value the individual places on the reward

All three must be high for motivation. If any is zero, motivation = zero.

### Exam Application
> "Rommy plans a hair salon for high-end clients in Delhi. Which motivation theory would best explain why her stylists will be satisfied if given creative freedom in designing treatments?" — JCM: Autonomy (freedom to design treatments) + Skill variety (different techniques) + Task significance (impact on client self-image) will produce high MPS → experienced meaningfulness + responsibility → high job satisfaction.

## T13: Being an Effective Leader

### Definitions
- **Leader**: someone who can influence others and who has managerial authority.
- **Leadership**: the process of influencing a group toward the achievement of goals.

### Early/Trait Theories
8 Traits Associated with Leadership:
1. Drive (high effort, ambitious, persistent)
2. Desire to lead
3. Honesty and integrity
4. Self-confidence
5. Intelligence
6. Job-relevant knowledge
7. Extraversion
8. Proneness to guilt

### Behavioral Theories

**University of Iowa (Lewin)** — 3 styles:
- **Autocratic**: dictates methods, unilateral decisions, limits participation
- **Democratic**: involves employees, delegates, uses feedback for coaching
- **Laissez-faire**: lets group make all decisions; provides no direction

**Ohio State Studies** — 2 dimensions:
- **Initiating structure**: defining roles, work structures, goals to meet
- **Consideration**: mutual trust, respect for subordinates' ideas and feelings

**University of Michigan Studies** — 2 dimensions:
- **Employee-oriented**: emphasizes interpersonal relationships → high productivity and satisfaction
- **Production-oriented**: emphasizes technical aspects and task accomplishment

**Managerial Grid (Blake & Mouton)** — 5 categories on 9×9 grid (concern for people vs. concern for production):
- **(1,1) Impoverished** — minimum effort for both
- **(9,1) Task/Authority-compliance** — high production, low people
- **(1,9) Country club** — high people, low production
- **(5,5) Middle-of-the-road** — adequate performance/morale
- **(9,9) Team management** — high production + high people commitment (ideal)

### Contingency Theories

**Fiedler's Contingency Model** — leadership effectiveness depends on match between leader's style (via LPC score) and situational favorableness.

**LPC (Least-Preferred Coworker) questionnaire**: High LPC = relationship-oriented leader; Low LPC = task-oriented leader.

**3 Situational Variables:**
1. **Leader-member relations** — confidence, trust, respect (Good/Poor)
2. **Task structure** — degree of formalization and structuring of job assignments (High/Low)
3. **Position power** — power to hire, fire, discipline, promote (Strong/Weak)

**8 Situation Categories (I–VIII):**
- I (Good/High/Strong) → Highly favorable → Task-oriented (Low LPC)
- II, III → Favorable → Task-oriented
- IV, V, VI → Moderate → Relationship-oriented (High LPC)
- VII, VIII (Poor/Low/Weak) → Highly unfavorable → Task-oriented (Low LPC)

Key insight: Task-oriented leaders perform best in highly favorable and highly unfavorable situations; relationship-oriented leaders perform best in moderately favorable situations.

**Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership Theory (SLT)**
Focuses on **follower readiness** (ability + willingness to do specific task).

| Readiness | Style | Description |
|---|---|---|
| R1: Unable + Unwilling | **Telling** (high task, low rel.) | Define roles, tell what/how/when/where |
| R2: Unable + Willing | **Selling** (high task, high rel.) | Directive + supportive behavior |
| R3: Able + Unwilling | **Participating** (low task, high rel.) | Share decisions; facilitate/communicate |
| R4: Able + Willing | **Delegating** (low task, low rel.) | Little direction or support needed |

**Path-Goal Theory (House)** — leader's job: help followers attain goals + provide direction/support needed.

4 Leadership Behaviors:
- **Directive**: schedules work, provides specific guidance
- **Supportive**: friendly, shows concern for followers
- **Participative**: consults with group members, uses suggestions
- **Achievement-oriented**: sets challenging goals, expects high performance

Contingency factors: *Environmental* (task structure, formal authority system, work group) and *Subordinate* (locus of control, experience, perceived ability).

**LMX (Leader-Member Exchange)**: leaders create in-groups (trusted, more communication, more authority) and out-groups. In-group members → higher performance, lower turnover, greater job satisfaction.

### Contemporary Views

**Transactional leaders**: lead through social exchanges; clarify role/task requirements; provide rewards contingent on performance.

**Transformational leaders**: stimulate and inspire followers to achieve extraordinary outcomes. They are attentive to individual followers' needs (charismatic + visionary + developmental).

**Charismatic leader**: enthusiastic, self-confident; personality and actions influence others.
**Visionary leader**: creates and articulates a realistic, credible, attractive vision of the future.

**5 Sources of Power:**
1. **Legitimate** — power from formal position
2. **Coercive** — power to punish/control
3. **Reward** — power to give positive rewards
4. **Expert** — power from expertise/knowledge
5. **Referent** — power from personal traits/admiration (charisma)

**5 Dimensions of Trust:**
Integrity · Competence · Consistency · Loyalty · Openness

### Exam Application
> "Ram is CEO of a startup. His team is new, enthusiastic but lacks skills (R2). According to Hersey-Blanchard, what style should he adopt? What if 2 years later they're fully competent but bored (R3)?" — R2 → Selling (high task + high relationship). R3 → Participating (low task + high relationship — share in decision-making to re-engage).

## T14: Controlling and Balanced Scorecard

### Definition
**Controlling**: the management function that involves monitoring, comparing, and correcting work performance.

### 3-Step Control Process
1. **Measure actual performance** (via personal observation, statistical reports, oral reports, written reports)
2. **Compare against standard** (determine range of variation; do nothing if within acceptable range)
3. **Take managerial action**: (a) correct actual performance (immediate or basic corrective action) OR (b) revise the standard if goal was unrealistic

### Three Types of Control
- **Feedforward control**: before activity begins; anticipates problems (e.g., quality checks on raw material inputs)
- **Concurrent control**: while activity is in progress; concurrent correction (e.g., management by walking around — MBWA)
- **Feedback control**: after activity; corrects problems after they occur (e.g., financial reports)

### Control Tools
- **Benchmarking**: the standard of excellence against which to measure and compare
- **MIS (Management Information System)**: provides management with needed information on a regular basis
- **Balanced Scorecard** — see below

### Balanced Scorecard (BSC) — Kaplan & Norton
A performance measurement tool that looks beyond financial perspective alone.

**4 Perspectives** (must know KPIs for each):

| Perspective | Question | Typical Objectives | Typical KPIs |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Financial** | How do we appear to shareholders? | Revenue growth, profitability, cost reduction | ROI, ROE, EVA, profit margin, revenue growth, cash flow |
| **Customer** | How do customers see us? | Satisfaction, retention, market share | Customer satisfaction score, retention rate, market share, NPS |
| **Internal Business Process** | What must we excel at? | Cycle time, defect rate, on-time delivery | Process efficiency, defect rate, on-time delivery, productivity |
| **Learning & Growth** (Org. Capacity) | How can we continue to improve? | Employee training, innovation, skills | Training hours/employee, employee satisfaction, skill index, retention |

**Strategy Map** — shows cause-and-effect chain: Learning & Growth → Internal Process → Customer → Financial.
*If employees improve skills (L&G) → internal processes improve → customer satisfaction increases → financial performance improves.*

### BSC Table Format for Exam
| Perspective | Strategic Objective | KPI | Target | Initiative |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Financial | Increase profitability | ROI | 15% | Cost reduction program |
| Customer | Improve satisfaction | NPS | 70+ | CRM implementation |
| Internal Process | Reduce defect rate | Defects/1000 units | <5 | Six Sigma rollout |
| Learning & Growth | Upskill workforce | Training hrs/employee | 40 hrs/year | L&D budget increase |

### Exam Application
> "Elon Musk's space tourism company. Design a BSC." — Financial: revenue per launch, payback period. Customer: safety rating, rebooking rate. IBP: launch success rate, turnaround time. L&G: engineer training hrs, innovation projects launched.

4. Developing measurement instruments
5. Collecting and preparing data

## T17: Financial Management

### Financial Statements
- **Balance Sheet**: Assets = Liabilities + Equity (snapshot at a point in time)
  - Current Assets: cash, receivables, inventory
  - Non-current: PP&E, intangibles
  - Current Liabilities: payables, short-term debt, accrued expenses
  - Long-term Liabilities; Equity: common stock + retained earnings

- **Income Statement (P&L)**: Revenue − COGS = Gross Profit − Opex = EBIT − Interest − Tax = Net Income

- **Cash Flow Statement**: Operating / Investing / Financing activities

- **Fund Flow Statement**: Sources of funds (increase in liability/equity, decrease in asset) vs. Application of funds (decrease in liability/equity, increase in asset).

### Financial Ratios (★ computation guaranteed)

**LIQUIDITY (short-term solvency):**
- **Current ratio** = Current Assets / Current Liabilities (>1 = can cover short-term)
- **Quick (Acid-test) ratio** = (CA − Inventory) / CL [or (Cash + Receivables) / CL]

**LEVERAGE (long-term solvency):**
- **Debt-to-equity** = Total Debt / Total Equity
- **Debt ratio** = Total Debt / Total Assets
- **Times interest earned** = EBIT / Interest Expense

**ACTIVITY (efficiency):**
- **Inventory turnover** = COGS / Average Inventory
- **Total asset turnover** = Net Sales / Total Assets
- **Receivables turnover** = Net Sales / Average Accounts Receivable

**PROFITABILITY:**
- **Net profit margin** = Net Income / Net Sales × 100
- **ROA** = Net Income / Total Assets × 100
- **ROE** = Net Income / Shareholders' Equity × 100

### Capital Structure vs. Capital Budgeting
- **Capital structure**: optimal mix of debt and equity to finance the firm (minimize WACC).
- **Capital budgeting**: evaluating long-term investment projects:
  - Payback period = Initial investment / Annual cash inflow
  - NPV = PV of inflows − Initial investment (accept if >0)
  - IRR = discount rate at which NPV=0 (accept if > cost of capital)

### Worked Ratio Example (from PYQ — MEGA Ltd. Balance Sheet March 31, 2024)
Given (₹): CA=100,000; Inventory=20,000; CL=25,000; Total Debt=105,000; Equity=145,000

- **Quick Ratio** = (100,000 − 20,000) / 25,000 = 80,000 / 25,000 = **3.2**
- **Debt-to-equity** = 105,000 / 145,000 = **0.72**
- Interpretation: Quick ratio >1 → adequate liquidity. D/E <1 → low leverage, conservatively financed.

### Exam Application
> "Explain capital structure and capital budgeting using an example from manufacturing." — Capital structure: Tata Motors may use 40% debt + 60% equity; debt is cheaper (tax shield) but increases financial risk. Capital budgeting: investing in a new EV assembly line → calculate NPV using expected cash flows from EV sales over 10 years; accept if NPV > 0.

## T18: Operations Management

### Definition
**Operations Management**: designing, controlling, and improving the processes and systems that create and deliver goods/services.

### Value Chain Management (Porter)
A framework for analyzing how organizations create value by breaking activities into **primary** and **support** functions.

**PRIMARY ACTIVITIES** (directly create value):
1. **Inbound Logistics** — receiving and storing raw materials
2. **Operations** — converting inputs into final product
3. **Outbound Logistics** — delivering products to customers
4. **Marketing & Sales** — promoting and selling products
5. **Service** — after-sales support

**SUPPORT ACTIVITIES** (support primary activities):
1. **Firm Infrastructure** — management, finance systems, legal
2. **HR Management** — hiring and training employees
3. **Technology Development** — innovation and process improvement
4. **Procurement** — purchasing inputs

**Margin** = value created − cost of activities.

### Resource Allocation Techniques
1. **Linear Programming** — mathematical technique to optimize resource allocation under constraints (maximize profit/minimize cost).
2. **Forecasting** — predicts future demand using historical data.
3. **Simulation** — creates virtual models to test different scenarios.
4. **Queuing Models** — manage waiting lines and service efficiency.
5. **Resource Allocation** — assigning manpower, machinery, money, materials optimally.

### Scenario Planning
Preparing for *multiple plausible futures* (best-case, worst-case, most-likely). Process:
1. Identify key drivers of change
2. Develop different scenarios
3. Analyze impacts on operations
4. Prepare strategies for each scenario

### Exam Application
> "Draw the value chain for Maruti Suzuki (car manufacturer in India)." — Inbound: steel/components from Maruti Suzuki Showa/suppliers. Operations: Manesar/Gurugram assembly lines. Outbound: Dealer distribution network across India. Marketing & Sales: dealer showrooms, Nexa premium network. Service: Maruti Authorized Service Stations. Support: Suzuki technology JV, Maruti's HR policies, vendor procurement systems.

## T19: Strategic Management

### Definition
**Strategic management**: a set of managerial decisions and actions that determines the long-run performance of a corporation.

### 6-Step Strategic Management Process
1. **Identify** the organization's current mission, goals, and strategies
2. **External analysis** (opportunities and threats — macro + industry environment)
3. **Internal analysis** (strengths and weaknesses — resources, capabilities, core competencies)
4. **SWOT Analysis** (combining steps 2 + 3)
5. **Formulate** strategies (corporate, competitive, functional)
6. **Implement** strategies (structure, staffing, culture, action plans)
7. **Evaluate** results (how effective have strategies been?)

*(Note: textbook says 6 steps; evaluate is sometimes listed as step 6 inclusive of formulate+implement.)*

### SWOT Analysis
- **Strengths**: activities the organization does well / unique resources it possesses
- **Weaknesses**: activities it does not do well / resources it lacks
- **Opportunities**: positive trends in the external environment
- **Threats**: negative trends in the external environment

### Corporate Strategies

**Growth strategy** — expand number of markets served or products offered:
- **Concentration**: focuses on primary business, increases products/markets within it
- **Vertical integration**: backward (become own supplier) or forward (become own distributor)
- **Horizontal integration**: combining with competitors (mergers/acquisitions)
- **Diversification**: related (synergies with existing business) or unrelated (completely new business)

**Stability strategy**: continues what it is currently doing; no growth.

**Renewal strategy**: address declining performance:
- **Retrenchment**: short-term, minor problem — cut costs, tighten operations
- **Turnaround**: more serious problems — drastic restructuring

### BCG Matrix (Boston Consulting Group)
2×2 grid; X-axis = Relative Market Share; Y-axis = Industry Growth Rate:

| | High Market Share | Low Market Share |
|---|---|---|
| **High Growth** | **Stars** — invest to maintain/grow | **Question Marks** — invest or divest |
| **Low Growth** | **Cash Cows** — milk for cash | **Dogs** — divest or liquidate |

- **Stars**: high investment needed; will become Cash Cows if market slows
- **Cash Cows**: mature, profitable; generate cash to fund Stars/Question Marks
- **Question Marks**: need decisions — invest heavily to become Stars, or drop
- **Dogs**: low share in low-growth; liquidate or divest

### Porter's Five Forces
1. **Threat of new entrants** — economies of scale, capital requirements, switching costs, brand loyalty, government policy
2. **Bargaining power of suppliers** — few suppliers, unique inputs, high switching costs
3. **Bargaining power of buyers** — few large buyers, standardized products, low switching costs
4. **Threat of substitutes** — many substitutes available at lower cost
5. **Rivalry among existing competitors** — number of competitors, growth rate, fixed costs

High force = lower profitability. Strategy is to position where these forces are weakest.

### Porter's 3 Generic Competitive Strategies
1. **Cost leadership** — lowest costs in industry (not lowest price); broad market
2. **Differentiation** — unique product widely valued; can charge premium; broad market
3. **Focus** — cost focus or differentiation focus within a narrow niche/segment

**Blue Ocean Strategy** — combine cost leadership + differentiation (value innovation); create uncontested market space.
**Stuck in the middle** — fails to develop at least one of the three strategies; below-average performance.

### Exam Application
> From PYQ: "From the ITC timeline, identify and list at least 5 specific evidences of BCG quadrant strategies." — 1975: Enters Hospitality (Question Mark). 1920s-present: Cigarettes (Cash Cow). 2002: Education & Stationery (Question Mark). 2003: Agri/food (Star developing). 2017: MasterChef safe spaces (Star/Question Mark).

**CASE PREAMBLE (common to Q1–Q4):**
*Quantum Chemicals Ltd. is a large chemical manufacturing company facing difficulties. Management has proposed laying off 500 employees from the operations department. The CEO, Anjali, is known for her ability to inspire the team even in adversity. She believes in setting challenges for her team and is fully trusted by all 5 team members she has worked with for years. ---

**Q1.** Anjali's leadership inspires employees beyond job expectations — her vision energizes the team to achieve extraordinary outcomes. Based on this description, identify the leadership style of Anjali. Also, in accordance with three dimensions of Hersey-Blanchard's Situational Leadership Theory, identify the most suitable leadership style Anjali should adopt for the subordinates described above. **[4 M]**

**Model Answer:**

*Part 1 — Leadership Style:*
Anjali demonstrates **Transformational Leadership**. Transformational leaders stimulate and inspire followers to achieve extraordinary outcomes and transform followers' attitudes and behaviors by inspiring them with a vision. Unlike transactional leaders (who lead through social exchanges/transactions), transformational leaders attend to individual followers' needs, help them see old problems in new ways, and excite and inspire followers to accomplish goals.

*Part 2 — Hersey-Blanchard SLT:*
Hersey and Blanchard's Situational Leadership Theory (SLT) proposes that leadership effectiveness depends on the **readiness** (ability + willingness) of followers.

Anjali's subordinates are described as *capable but unwilling* — this corresponds to **R3 readiness** (Able but Unwilling).

For R3 followers, the recommended style is **Participating** (Low task behavior, High relationship behavior). In the Participating style, the leader and followers share in decision-making; the leader's main role is facilitating and communicating. Anjali should involve the subordinates in discussions about the restructuring, hear their concerns, and build their motivation to accept and implement the change — rather than directing or delegating.

**Q2.** In accordance with Vroom's Expectancy Theory, what went wrong in the scenario below? **[2×3 = 6 M]**

*Scenario: Tom is a data analyst at InfoSys Ltd. He works hard (Effort), but his manager doesn't recognize performance accurately (Performance-Reward link broken). His organization also has given only minimal bonus increments this year (Reward has low value).*

**Model Answer:**

Vroom's **Expectancy Theory** states:
> **Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence**

- **Expectancy (E→P)**: Tom's belief that effort will lead to performance — *this link is intact* (he puts in effort and performance is good).
- **Instrumentality (P→O)**: Tom's belief that performance will lead to reward — *this link is BROKEN* (manager doesn't recognize performance accurately). Tom sees no connection between his performance and receiving a reward.
- **Valence**: Tom's value placed on the reward — *this is LOW* (minimal bonus increments are not valued sufficiently).

Since both Instrumentality and Valence are deficient (one = 0 or near-0), the multiplication results in near-zero motivation. Tom will reduce his effort, become disengaged, or eventually leave.

Managerial implication: InfoSys must (1) make performance evaluations transparent and accurate (fix Instrumentality), and (2) offer rewards that employees genuinely value — not just monetary (fix Valence).

**Q3.** Consider the balance sheet of MEGA Ltd. as on March 31, 2024 (amounts in ₹):
- Cash: 50,000 | AR: 30,000 | Inventory: 20,000 | Land: 100,000 | Equipment: 50,000 | Total Assets: 250,000
- AP: 15,000 | Short-term loans: 5,000 | Accrued expenses: 5,000 | Long-term loans: 80,000 | Common stock: 50,000 | Retained earnings: 95,000

Calculate and comment on: (a) Quick Ratio (b) Debt-to-equity Ratio. **[2×2+1 = 5 M]**

**Model Answer:**

**(a) Quick Ratio (Acid-test Ratio)**
Formula: Quick Ratio = (Current Assets − Inventory) / Current Liabilities

Current Assets = 50,000 + 30,000 + 20,000 = 1,00,000
Inventory = 20,000
Current Liabilities = 15,000 + 5,000 + 5,000 = 25,000

**Quick Ratio = (1,00,000 − 20,000) / 25,000 = 80,000 / 25,000 = 3.2**

*Comment*: A quick ratio of 3.2 (well above the benchmark of 1.0) indicates that MEGA Ltd. has strong short-term liquidity. For every ₹1 of current liabilities, the company has ₹3.2 of liquid assets to meet them. The company is comfortably solvent in the short term.

**(b) Debt-to-Equity Ratio**
Formula: D/E = Total Debt / Total Shareholders' Equity

Total Debt = Short-term loans + Accrued + Long-term = 5,000 + 5,000 + 80,000 = 90,000
(OR: Total Liabilities = 15,000+5,000+5,000+80,000 = 1,05,000 if AP included)

Using Total Debt (interest-bearing only): **D/E = 90,000 / 1,45,000 = 0.62**

*Comment*: A D/E ratio of 0.62 indicates MEGA Ltd. is primarily equity-financed. Low leverage suggests low financial risk; the company is conservatively financed and likely has significant borrowing capacity for future investments.

**Q4.** Using the Value Chain framework, draw the value chain for Maruti Suzuki (Car manufacturer in India). **[4 M]**

**Model Answer:**

Porter's **Value Chain Management** framework (Michael Porter) breaks organizational activities into primary and support activities to identify sources of competitive advantage.

**PRIMARY ACTIVITIES:**
1. **Inbound Logistics** — receiving steel coils from MSIL's supplier ecosystem (Maruti Suzuki Showa), managing inventory at Manesar/Gurugram plants

**SUPPORT ACTIVITIES:**

**Q5.** The Air India marketing head is deciding among 4 marketing strategies against 3 possible competitive actions by IndiGo. The payoff matrix (₹ million) is given:

| Air India Strategy | IndiGo CA1 | IndiGo CA2 | IndiGo CA3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| S1 | 15 | 14 | 12 |
| S2 | 9 | 16 | 20 |
| S3 | 24 | 22 | 15 |
| S4 | 19 | 14 | 30 |

Based on different psychological orientations, identify the methods the Head-Marketing may use for deciding among the strategies. Show all computations. **[2×3 = 6 M]**

**Model Answer:**

Under **uncertainty** (no probabilities known), four methods apply based on psychological orientation:

**1. MAXIMAX (Optimistic manager)** — "best of bests"
Row maxima: S1=15, S2=20, S3=24, S4=30
**Decision: S4 (payoff = 30)**

**2. MAXIMIN / Wald (Pessimistic manager)** — "best of worsts"
Row minima: S1=12, S2=9, S3=15, S4=14
**Decision: S3 (payoff = 15)**

**3. LAPLACE / Equal-likelihood (Neutral manager)** — equal probability to each column:
- S1 avg = (15+14+12)/3 = 41/3 = 13.67
- S2 avg = (9+16+20)/3 = 45/3 = 15.0
- S3 avg = (24+22+15)/3 = 61/3 = 20.33
- S4 avg = (19+14+30)/3 = 63/3 = **21.0**
**Decision: S4 (avg = 21.0)**

**4. MINIMAX REGRET (Savage — regret-minimizing)** — build regret matrix:
Column maxima: CA1=24, CA2=22, CA3=30

| | CA1 | CA2 | CA3 | Max Regret |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| S1 | 24-15=9 | 22-14=8 | 30-12=18 | **18** |
| S2 | 24-9=15 | 22-16=6 | 30-20=10 | **15** |
| S3 | 24-24=0 | 22-22=0 | 30-15=15 | **15** |
| S4 | 24-19=5 | 22-14=8 | 30-30=0 | **8** |

**Decision: S4 (max regret = 8)** — minimum of {18, 15, 15, 8}

*Summary: An optimistic manager, a neutral manager, and a regret-minimizing manager would all choose S4. A pessimistic manager would choose S3.*

# MARK-LEAK CALLOUTS

> These are the most common places students drop marks. Memorize the traps.

1. **Herzberg trap**: Salary is a HYGIENE FACTOR, not a motivator. If a scenario gives a salary increase and says "this will motivate employees" — challenge it. Removing dissatisfaction ≠ creating motivation.

2. **Equality vs. Equity trap** (PYQ-tested): Equal pay to all = EQUALITY. Proportional pay based on contribution = EQUITY. Do not confuse them. Vroom and Adams both care about equity.

3. **Minimax Regret computation trap**: The regret matrix is column-maximum minus each cell (NOT row maximum). A common mistake is building the regret matrix incorrectly. Always: for each column, subtract every value from that column's maximum.

4. **Fiedler trap**: Task-oriented (Low LPC) is effective in both highly favorable AND highly unfavorable situations — not just when things are bad. Relationship-oriented (High LPC) works best in moderate situations.

5. **Herzberg motivators list trap**: Students often forget the complete list. Must list all 6: Achievement, Recognition, Work itself, Responsibility, Advancement, Growth. Hygiene has 10: Supervision, Company policy, Relationship with supervisor, Working conditions, Salary, Relationship with peers, Personal life, Relationship with employees, Status, Security.

6. **BSC perspective name trap**: The 4th perspective is officially "Learning and Growth" (or "Organizational Capacity" in some slides). Do NOT write "Innovation" alone — it's always paired with Learning.

7. **Tuckman "forming" trap**: Forming is when the group *defines* purpose/structure — not when performance begins. Performance = Performing stage.

8. **Value Chain sequence trap**: The primary activities in order are: Inbound → Operations → Outbound → Marketing & Sales → Service. Students often reverse outbound/marketing.

9. **BCG axes trap**: X-axis = Relative Market SHARE (not growth). Y-axis = Market/Industry GROWTH RATE. Stars = high growth + high share. Cash Cow = low growth + high share (not high growth!).

10. **Expectancy theory trap**: If ANY of the three (Expectancy, Instrumentality, Valence) equals zero, motivation collapses to zero. Multiplication, not addition.

11. **Porter's 5 Forces vs. Generic Strategies**: 5 Forces = for analyzing the industry; Generic Strategies = for choosing how to compete. Do not conflate them.

12. **Financial ratio signs**: Current ratio >1 = good liquidity. D/E <1 = low risk. High ROE = good but check if driven by debt. Always interpret after computing.
